# The Siege-Breakers
*The Imperium's answer to the prepared position: a church that walks, leased by the campaign.*

---

## 1. What They Are

The Imperium's answer to the prepared position: walking fortresses — Guild-built, Guild-maintained, another leash on the houses — that wade through kill zones soaking fire that would annihilate a battalion, cracking the line so the tide pours through the breach. Inefficient, monstrously expensive, effective.

Each is unique, named, and old; houses lease them from the Guilds by campaign, at prices that have decided elections. Levies are forbidden to pray to them, which tells you what the levies do.

Two facts frame everything else about them, and the second is stranger than the first. The first: a siege-breaker carries a **Guild shield core** — the same sacred monopoly that veils the fleet — and on the ground the veil does what no armor can: it takes the full weight of a prepared defense and cycles. The physics is the void's physics brought down into the mud. Strikes and shellfire ablate a veil that recharges between blows; nothing kills a shielded thing from a respectful distance; and so the ground war, like the void war, has a press — except that on the ground, the press is made of men.

The second fact: the hulls are not the shortage. The Guilds, when the lease prices are questioned — and every generation, some house questions them — reply mildly that they will forge as many siege-breakers as the Imperium can walk, and then they name a number: the count of living certified **walkers**. The number is never large. The machines are not scarce. The people who can wear them are.

## 2. The Patterns

The Guilds forge to Patterns here as they do in the void — sanctified design lineages, argued over by enthusiasts on both sides of the wire. Four walk the Reach.

| Pattern | Meaning | Scale | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Helepol Pattern** | "taker of cities" | 100m+ | The colossi. Naval-grade batteries on legs: a walking siege train that ends arguments with fortresses. Three were ever forged. One walks. |
| **Polyorket Pattern** | "the besieger" | 60–80m | The classic siege-breaker of doctrine — the line-breaker that cracks prepared fronts. A dozen and some serve in the Reach. |
| **Krios Pattern** | "the ram" | 25–35m | The breachers: ram-heads, thermal lances, shield-stripping arrays. They walk with the first wave and do their work in person. The most numerous, which still means dozens. |
| **Aspida Pattern** | "the shield" | ~50m | The oddity: walking veil-bearers that carry almost no guns and umbrella advancing infantry under their shields. A handful exist. They are, by UGF statistical analysis, the most fired-upon objects in the ground war. |

The Patterns define the spectrum, and the spectrum is the doctrine: a Krios makes a breach, a Polyorket makes a front, a Helepol makes a decision. Terrain still votes — a Krios can climb what a Polyorket cannot, and the Roof of Halvard's World defeats them all; there are still wars where mechs can't walk and Paladins are kings.

Fewer than sixty walk, all Patterns counted. The exact number is known to the Guilds and estimated, jealously and continuously, by everyone else.

## 3. The Walkers

A siege-breaker is not driven. It is *worn*.

The walker lies in the **gait-cradle** at the machine's heart, and the machine maps itself onto him: his balance becomes its balance, his stride its stride, his flinch its forty-thousand-ton flinch. The crew fights the mech — guns, veil, damage control, a house commander choosing its battles. The walker *is* the mech, for as long as the cradle holds him, in a union the Guilds administer, certify, and decline to explain.

Almost no one can do it. The gait-trials run continuously — Guild testing circuits working through the levy musters, the crèche batches, the village fairs, panning patiently and mostly in vain — and of the few the trial passes, training breaks most. Of those who serve, the cradle keeps its tithe: careers are short, Krios walkers' shortest of all, and no walker leaves the work intact. Veterans of the cradle stand strangely in wind, leaning into gusts that could not move a man, because for five years their body was a tower and towers lean. The condition has a clinical name in Guild records and a better one in the villages: **the long stride**. It never entirely returns a man to his own size.

And the machines are picky. A mech accepts a walker or it does not — the cradle simply will not close its circuit — and no pattern in the choosing has ever been established, though every house has tried. *Adamastor* has refused every candidate three noble lines have sent him for a century, and accepted two shepherds and a smith's daughter. Walker lineages accrete around each machine anyway, by hope as much as heredity, and the names of every walker a mech has borne are engraved inside its cradle, where only the next one will read them.

This is the scarcity, stated plainly: the Guilds can forge hulls freely and say so; they cannot forge walkers, and neither can anyone else. Cynics on both staffs have noted how convenient the bottleneck is. The cynics have not produced a walker.

## 4. Aboard

A leased siege-breaker carries three allegiances in one hull. The **fighting crew** is the house's — gunners, veil-tenders, the commander who owns the campaign and the glory. The **walker** is sworn to the house but made by the Guilds, and belongs, in the way that matters, to the machine. And the **minders** are the Guilds' own: wrights who live aboard permanently, tend what only they may touch, keep their own mess, their own watch, and their own counsel. Every crew assumes the minders can stop the mech where it stands. No crew has ever reported seeing it done, which is not the same thing.

The interior culture is half warship, half shrine. The crews keep the machine's history the way Imperial naval crews keep a genealogy; feast days are observed in the gun galleries; the cradle chamber is entered like a chapel, because it is one.

## 5. Employment

Doctrine is unchanged in fifty years because it works: the mech cracks the line so the tide pours through. A siege-breaker is the crowbar of the tempo doctrine — the Unresting Assault supplies the pressure, the mech supplies the breach, and the Paladins go through it at the network's worst moment. The UGF's perfect defense is a machine for answering everything; the siege-breaker is the one question it cannot answer at any sustainable price.

The lease politics are a war of their own. A campaign lease runs to sums that houses mortgage worlds against — Ming holds the paper on most of them — and the Guilds' allocation decisions are read at Elysium the way augurs read weather: a house granted *Makrothym*'s services is a house the Forge smiles on, this season, at this price. The prices have decided elections. Everyone involved insists this has never influenced the Guilds' choices, and no one involved believes it.

### Getting there

A siege-breaker reaches its war by one of two roads.

The common road is **the long walk**: landed by heavy barge at a secure depot far behind the front, then marched — days or weeks overland, at a pace that never varies, toward a defender who can see it coming the entire way. The approach is the first weapon. The UGF tracks the march by drone and satellite; estimated time of arrival becomes public knowledge on both sides of the wire; and the front it is walking toward goes quiet, sector by sector, as everyone does arithmetic. A dot on the horizon that is larger every dawn does things to a defense that no barrage can.

The rare road is **the Knight-fall**: a direct combat drop from orbit, the mech riding a one-use descent cradle down through a corridor the fleet has swept and bought — veil blazing through reentry, a falling star that resolves, horribly, into a shape. A Knight-fall spends a resupply window's worth of naval effort to put the breach *inside* the enemy's line on day one, and so it is reserved for campaigns worth exactly that. UGF traffic logs it under the brevity code MECHFALL; UGF soldiers who have seen one do not use brevity about it. Twice in fifty-two years the defense has killed a mech in the sky. Both craters are places with names now.

## 6. Killing One

UGF counter-mech doctrine, refined over half a century, admits three answers, in ascending order of cost and descending order of dignity.

**Overload the veil.** Mass the fires of an entire sector onto one target until it ablates faster than it cycles. It works. It also means that for those hours, nothing else in the sector is being fired upon — and the tide walking in behind the mech knows it. This is the arithmetic the siege-breaker was built to impose: its true weapon is what it costs to kill. Sector commanders who make the call describe it identically, generation after generation: *you don't shoot the mech, you buy it, and the price is the battle.*

**Strand it.** You rarely kill a siege-breaker; you take its legs. Cut a knee joint, drop the only bridge rated for its mass, mire it in mud your engineers spent three weeks preparing. A mobility kill converts the mech into a fortress — still veiled, still firing, going nowhere — and its house into a hostage: honor demands the stranded machine be reached, and the Guild lease demands it in writing, and the relief operation is where the real killing gets done. A stranded siege-breaker is the best bait in the ground war. It is also still a siege-breaker, as several relief-minded UGF commanders have been reminded at the cost of the reminder.

**Go up.** The ground press. Inside the veil's recharge envelope — which is to say, at its feet, on its hull — the shield arithmetic fails, and what remains is armor, and armor can be cut. UGF assault engineer teams close under the guns, through the escort infantry, onto the machine itself: magnetic anchors, thermal cutters, charges laid at joints and vents by hand, forty meters up, in battle. It is the bravest thing the ground war asks of anyone. The teams have no special designation — doctrine officially prefers the other two answers — but among UGF veterans the verb is its own credential. *He's been up.* Survivors of a climb are the ground war's press veterans, and are treated accordingly, which is to say: bought drinks, given room, and never asked twice.

No siege-breaker has ever been captured. The Guild minders are assumed to be the reason. The assumption has never been tested and described afterward.

## 7. The Union's Answer

The UGF builds no siege-breakers, and its doctrine writers have spent fifty years explaining, in increasingly refined prose, that it does not want to. What it builds instead is the **Rampart-class land monitor**: a tracked super-heavy direct-fire platform — one enormous gun, a Union-forged shield, a crew of nine, no legs, no liturgy. Ramparts exist to make a siege-breaker's arithmetic worse: dug in on a likely axis, a Rampart is the heaviest thing on the field that can hide, and its gun was specified, openly and in the procurement language, *to be sufficient to the veil*. They serve in numbered batteries, are produced in the dozens, and are crewed like vehicles, not shrines.

The Union does not name them; the hulls carry registry numbers and the DPI photographs them from the side without the crew art. The crews name them anyway, in paint, under the gun where the drones don't look, and the names are rude. This is the entire contrast, in one place: the Imperium builds cathedrals, and the Union builds calibers — and the Union's calibers have nicknames their government pretends not to see, because no army has ever fought beside a big machine without loving it a little.

Somewhere in the Deferred Systems Annex, by standing rumor, there is a drawer with a walking machine in it. FLEETCOM's spending on the question of *how the gait actually works* is small, renewable, and nineteen years old. The Union's contempt for the siege-breakers is real. So is the drawer.

## 8. The Legends

Fewer than sixty walk; five of them, everyone knows.

### *Adamastor* — Helepol Pattern — "Grandfather"
The oldest walking thing in human space: four centuries of service, older than the war, older than most of the houses' current names for themselves. His hull is a relief map of every war the Imperium has fought since his consecration — by Guild custom his scars are maintained, not repaired — and staff colleges on both sides can date historical campaigns by which of Grandfather's wounds they recognize. Thirty-one walkers' names are engraved in his cradle. He has refused every noble candidate for a hundred years; his last four walkers came from the same shepherd district on Dzimba, and the Guilds have quietly resettled the district closer to a testing circuit, and do not discuss it.

### *Makrothym* — Polyorket Pattern — "Old Patience"
The resident god of Halvard's World. Landed at Kostantine Landing thirty years ago under Rurikov's perpetual lease; has never left, because extraction through a mutually denied orbit would cost a fleet action, and so he walks the Long Front's winter offensives decade after decade, patient as the house that leases him. His footprints along the Long Front hold rainwater and offerings in roughly equal measure, and are marked on trench maps of both armies — the UGF's for targeting, the Imperium's for pilgrimage, and in practice each side uses the other's purpose too. UGF gunners on the Long Front hold that you do not fire on Old Patience on a quiet day. On loud days he takes his chances with everyone else.

### *Katalu* — Krios Pattern — "the Unbuilder"
The great breacher; the press-fighter; the proof of what the ram Pattern is for. It was *Katalu* who opened the Anders Line in war year 41 — through the wire, through the counter-fires, through the wall itself, first machine into the breach and the tide behind him — and Imperial chroniclers have been failing to improve on a UGF survivor's line ever since: *it came through the wall like the wall had been the mistake.* His ram-head is replaced after every campaign, and each spent one is presented to the leasing house as a relic; two great houses now display an Unbuilder's ram in their reliquary halls, and a third has petitioned. Krios cradles burn walkers fastest, and Katalu's are the shortest-served and most venerated of all: five in the last decade, every name known to the levies who followed him.

### *Skepe* — Aspida Pattern — "Mother"
The shield that walks. Eleven campaigns and has never carried a gun: Skepe's work is to stand over advancing infantry with her veil spread wide, taking what was meant for ten thousand men. The levies' devotion to her breaks the Remembrancers' rules more thoroughly than all the other cults combined — Mother receives more prayers than some saints, and the College's inspectors have filed the same recommendation, without action, for forty years. UGF fire doctrine designates her priority one, kill on identification, and UGF soldiers hate the doctrine with an intensity their own officers find alarming: everyone in the Reach, on both sides of the wire, understands exactly what she is for. She has been "killed" in Union after-action reports four times. The reports have stopped saying it.

### *Antaios* — Helepol Pattern — fallen
The second colossus, and the lesson. Mired in the Cauter Fens in war year 26 — a mobility kill three UGF engineer battalions spent a month preparing — Antaios fought as a stationary fortress for nine months while House Normand, which held his lease, mounted four relief operations and lost the flower of a campaign season in the trying. The fourth relief reached the Fens in time to watch his veil fail. When it was over, the Guilds arrived under a truce nobody remembers negotiating, took the crew's bodies and the gait-cradle, and left the hull where it stands. It stands there now: the **Antaios Stone**, a landmark on the charts of both armies, a navigation fix, a shrine, a warning — all four, officially, on the Imperial maps. The Undertakers asked once to buy the wreck. The Guilds' answer is not recorded, but the Undertakers never asked again.

*The third Helepol has never walked. It is finished, consecrated, and sleeping in a Guild vault, and it has no name, because the Guilds do not name a mech until it has a walker. It has been waiting for ninety years.*

## 9. The Cults

Levies are forbidden to pray to them. What the levies do instead, with the Remembrancers watching, is a masterpiece of theological needle-threading: they pray *at* them — formally addressing the walkers and crews interred in each machine's history, the names in the cradle, the honored dead of the hull, which is ancestor-veneration and perfectly licit. That every word of it is spoken to forty thousand tons of standing machine is a matter the College has declined to examine for as long as the machines have walked. Prayer belongs to the ancestors. The mechs, the levies will tell you, are *full* of ancestors. The inspectors file their recommendation. The recommendation is not acted upon.

The practice is the same on every front: offerings left in footprints — the last swallow of anything, poured out, the same rite as Old Halvard's; tokens passed up to crews before a battle, to ride in the hull where the veil is; the machine's name unsaid on the morning of an assault, because naming calls attention, and attention on such a morning belongs to the enemy. Villages along a long walk's route bring children out to stand in the mech's shadow as it passes — the shadow is luck; the footprint, afterward, is more — and the Guilds have never once varied a march route to avoid a village, which the villages have noticed, and the staff planners have too.

The Guilds say nothing about any of it. The Guilds maintain the scars, engrave the cradles, leave the wrecks standing where they fall, and say *nothing*, and after four centuries the silence has taken on the character of an answer nobody has fully translated.

The Remembrancers forbid the worship of machines. The machines, everyone agrees, are not worshipped. The Reach is full of such agreements.

## Open questions

- The third Helepol is finished, consecrated, unnamed, and ninety years asleep. Who is it waiting for — and does the Synod already know the name it isn't saying?
- What does the gait-cradle actually read in a candidate? The Guilds certify walkers without ever explaining a refusal — is the trial measuring an aptitude, or recognizing something?
- The fleet's martyred hulls have their keel-fragments taken into house reliquaries; *Antaios* was left standing whole in the Fens. Why is a dead mech treated differently from a dead ship — and by whose ruling?
- What do the Guild minders do during battle? Every crew has stories. The stories do not agree, and the minders have never corrected any of them.
- Whom do the mechs refuse? Adamastor turned away three noble lines and took two shepherds and a smith's daughter — has anyone mapped the pattern of acceptance, and is the map why the Guilds resettled that district?
- Where does a spent Unbuilder ram actually come from and go to — is the relic presentation a courtesy, a payment, or a tithe running in a direction nobody has admitted?
- Has a walker ever refused to walk on the morning of a battle — and what happened to the walker, the campaign, and the lease?
- Rampart crews name their hulls under the gun where the drones don't look. One name was actually suppressed by DPI order — the only one, ever. What did Rampart 44's crew paint on her?

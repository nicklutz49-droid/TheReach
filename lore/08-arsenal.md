# The Arsenal
*What a soldier carries is a portrait of the government that issued it.*

---

## 1. The Kinetic Split

Fifty-two years of war and neither side has fielded an energy rifle. This is not backwardness; it is choice, made independently by two societies who arrived at the same conclusion from opposite directions: a bolt of accelerated metal is cheap, explainable, repairable by hand, and kills exactly as dead as anything more elegant. Directed-energy weapons exist in the Reach — in naval batteries, in siege-breaker and Rampart-grade mech armament, in the handful of relic arms a Paladin might carry into a breach — but they are naval-scale, mech-scale, or relic-scale: expensive enough to need a keel, a gait-cradle, or a genealogy standing behind them. Everything a rifleman or a gun crew touches on either side of the wire is kinetic, and the split in *how* it is kinetic is the clearest portrait in the Reach of the two societies that built it.

The Imperium builds for **throw-weight**: large-bore, slow-cycling, mechanically simple, built to be understood by the man firing it and, at a pinch, repaired by the smith who shoes his horse. A House Rurikov rifle does not need a diagnostic bay; it needs a rasp, a file, and a village that has not forgotten how. The doctrine is not a compromise forced by poverty, though poverty helps sell it — it is a considered preference for weapons whose failure modes are visible, whose intimidation is honest, and whose manufacture does not require anyone to trust a stranger's electronics. A big gun is also, not incidentally, an argument: the levy who has watched a Bombard-Pattern tube fire once does not need to be told the enemy is doomed. He has felt it in his teeth.

The Union builds for **precision**: high-velocity, rail-assisted, sensor-slaved, smart-fuzed down to the grenade. INDMOB's design philosophy compresses to a phrase every UGF ordnance officer can recite: *hit exactly what you aimed at, and account for the round.* A Union weapon knows where it is, what it is pointed at, and — through the squad network — what everyone else's weapons are pointed at too. This is casualty politics wearing the shape of engineering: every shell fired is a line item, every miss is a wasted vote's worth of tax, and a war whose price is denominated in franchise cannot afford to waste ordnance the way it cannot afford to waste men.

Put the two calibers side by side and you are looking at two theories of legitimacy. The Imperium's gun says: *we have always had more than you, and we always will.* The Union's gun says: *we spent exactly what this was worth, and we can prove it.* Neither side finds the other's argument persuasive. Both field it anyway, because it is not really addressed to the enemy.

## 2. The Imperial Soldier's Kit — A Patchwork, Proudly

There is no standard Imperial rifle. There has never been one, and no house has ever proposed fixing it, because the absence is not an oversight — it is doctrine, load-bearing, and about as negotiable as a genealogy. Artillery tubes are Guild-forged, and Guild-forged means uniform, sacred, and leased at prices that decide elections. Rifles, carbines, and every vehicle short of a siege-breaker are **house-built**: designed, tooled, and issued by each house's own armories, in each house's own idiom, to each house's own standard of what a soldier deserves to carry. Twelve great houses field, by conservative Guild count, upward of forty distinct small-arms **patterns** currently in front-line service, plus whatever the benches and marcher lords have inherited, jury-rigged, or bought secondhand through Skarn.

### What the patchwork costs

The patchwork is not merely aesthetic. Calibers do not match. A Rurikov sotnya that has fired its last cartridge cannot be resupplied by the Karolus regiment holding the next traverse of trench, because the Karolus regiment's ammunition will not chamber. Joint operations therefore carry parallel logistics trains for every house present — an appalling, deliberate inefficiency the Great Council has never once proposed solving by standardization, because standardization would mean one house's pattern winning and eleven houses' pride losing. **Inter-house resupply is a diplomatic act**, negotiated sector by sector, campaign by campaign, and more than one relief operation has arrived on schedule and useless, its wagons full of the wrong bore. Quartermasters trade calibers the way electors trade marriages: as leverage, not as charity.

### The houses, armed

*A sample of the patchwork — six houses of twelve, illustrative and not exhaustive.*

| Pattern | House | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Akritan-pattern carbine** | Palaiogos | Ornate, ancient in lineage, Guild-maintained past any honest claim to antiquity. Ceremonial issue is flawless; line-regiment copies are adequate, and no one pretends otherwise. |
| **Durendal-pattern battle rifle** | Karolus | The Imperium's closest thing to a "standard" — heavy-bored, orthodox, built to be understood by a coalition of strangers. Other houses' officers judge a rifle by how far it falls short of a Durendal. |
| **Strelet-pattern rifle** | Rurikov | Crude, robust, and field-repairable to a degree the other houses find faintly embarrassing. A village smith can true a Strelet's barrel with a rasp and a prayer, and Rurikov has built an entire logistics doctrine on the fact that he sometimes has to. |
| **Osbern-pattern carbine** | Normand | Light, short-ranged, built for men who expect to be inside the enemy's wire within the hour and dead or victorious shortly after. Named for the Margrave who fired the war's first shot and never once apologized for it. |
| **Janisar-pattern rifle** | Osmanli | The best-made line-infantry weapon in the Imperium, and — alone among the houses — issued without distinction to levy and long-service Vatborn alike. The choice is doctrine: Osmanli arms its Vatborn as soldiers, not as ordnance, and the rifle is the first thing that says so. |
| **Tanega-pattern rifle** | Yamatai | Small-run, exquisitely made, suppressed for the silence-first assault; fielded in numbers that would embarrass any other house's ordnance minister and answer to almost no one else's doctrine but their own. |

*The remaining six houses field six more patterns, each proud, and none of them interchangeable. A UGF intelligence officer can identify a sector's defenders by the sound of the incoming fire alone, the way a naval officer reads a screen's house by its silhouette.*

### The levy's rifle

What a common levy actually carries is a further patchwork below the patchwork. A sworn retainer regiment fields its house's current pattern in good repair; a marcher levy from an indebted bench-house fields whatever the armory issued two campaigns ago, remanufactured, occasionally re-bored from agricultural stock in years when the smithy had nothing better to plane. *The Imperium beats its plowshares back into swords when the tithe runs short, and does not discuss the theology of it.* A levy's rifle is a biography of his lord's finances before it is a weapon, and any Imperial soldier can read the balance sheet at a glance in the man standing next to him.

## 3. The UGF Rifleman's Kit — Ruthless Standardization

Every UGF rifleman on every front, from a garrison world's honor guard to a firebase on the Long Front, carries the same rifle, made to the same tolerance, by contracted plants on dozens of worlds that have never seen each other's paperwork and do not need to. The current-issue service rifle is the **R-19** — rail-assisted, sensor-slaved to the squad network, its integral grenade module smart-fuzed for programmable airburst — universally known in the ranks simply as *the nineteen*, because nobody has ever needed to specify which one. INDMOB runs the design under a single Type-series: the **R-6** sidearm, the **R-24** light support weapon, the **R-31** precision rifle, each interchangeable in parts and doctrine across the entire Union military. A soldier rotated from convoy-escort duty on a core world to a firebase on Halvard's picks up the issue weapon at the armory window and is proficient with it before she has finished checking the sights.

This is the whole point, stated as procurement policy: **standardization is doctrine**. It means a Warden-class frigate's marine detachment and a UGF rifle company share spare parts. It means a captured soldier's kit, if recovered, tells an intelligence officer nothing about *which* unit he served in — every unit is armed alike — which infuriates the Imperium's collectors of such things and delights INDMOB in roughly equal measure. It means a supply convoy that misses its window can be made up from any depot on the front, because every depot stocks the same bore. And it means, quietly, that a UGF rifleman never has to wonder whether the ammunition crate beside a dead ally's body will fit her own weapon. It always will. *This is not a small mercy, in a war fought this far from home.*

The rifleman's kit beyond the weapon is equally flat: the **Infantry Protective Ensemble**, current mark issued and stenciled with nothing but a serial number, called *the shell* by everyone who has worn one under fire; a squad-networked comm/optics rig that shares targeting data with the platoon's drone handler in real time; a field medical kit standardized down to the bandage roll. Nothing is decorative. Nothing is named after anyone. The uniformity is, in its own understated way, as much a statement of faith as a Remembrancer's reading of the dead — just a faith in the ledger instead of the genealogy.

## 4. Armor: Tanks and Fighting Vehicles

### The houses' steel

Imperial armor is house-built, exactly like the rifles, and for the exact same reasons — which means the same patchwork, at ten times the price and consequence. Three patterns illustrate the spread.

House **Normand** fields the **Neustrie-pattern battle tank**, the finest assault armor in the Imperium and the material explanation of a line every UGF intelligence officer has memorized: *Grade One for the first two hundred hours, Grade Three thereafter.* The Neustrie is built for speed and shock — thin by Imperial standards, fast by anyone's, its fuel cells and ammunition racks sized for a breakthrough, not a campaign. It wins the opening hour of every battle it fights and runs dry doing it; House Normand has never once budgeted for the tank it would need to hold what the Neustrie takes.

House **Karolus** fields the **Roncal-pattern siege tank**, heavier, slower, and built to the opposite theory: sustained, orthodox, coalition-friendly armor that holds a line as readily as it breaks one, because Duke Lothar needs allied houses to trust what rolls up beside them. The Roncal is the tank other houses' staff officers describe, without much irony, as "the boring one that is still there in month four."

House **Rurikov** fields the **Zimny-pattern tank**, cheap, winterized, and individually unimpressive — a Zimny does not need to win a duel; it needs to be the fourth one this week. Rurikov's armor doctrine is Rurikov's infantry doctrine wearing plating: numbers absorb what quality would have to earn.

Yamatai fields almost no armor at all, and says so without embarrassment; a sworn regiment considers massed steel a Rurikov solution to what ought to be a discipline problem. Mutapa's engineers build things that move, eventually, and would rather you came to them.

### The Union's machines

FLEETCOM's cousin service builds to the same alphanumeric flatness as everything else INDMOB ships. The **M-7 main battle tank** — *the seven* — pairs a rail-assisted smoothbore with full squad-network integration, taking target handoffs from a platoon's drones before its own crew has finished traversing the turret. The **M-4 infantry fighting vehicle** carries a rifle squad and its attached drone kit forward under armor and doubles as a relay node once the squad dismounts. The **M-9 combat engineer vehicle** — bridging, mine-clearing, the unglamorous machine that makes the other two usable — is, soldiers note, the only UGF vehicle whose nickname is affectionate rather than sarcastic: *the nine does the work nobody photographs.*

*One army's tanks are named for the men who own them. The other's are named for the number in the contract. Both crews would die for theirs, and have.*

## 5. Artillery: Guild Tubes and Precision Fires

Artillery is the one class of weapon where the Imperium is *not* a patchwork, and the exception proves the rule: tubes are Guild-forged, exactly like capital keels and shield cores, and Guild-forged means every house buys from the same catalogue at the same extortionate, election-deciding price. The Guilds cast to **Patterns**, as they do in the void and in the siege-breakers, and four walk the Reach in their thousands.

| Pattern | Role | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Bombard Pattern** | Siege-grade heavy tube | The tube behind every week-long, map-square barrage in the Reach. Enormous, glacially slow to serve, dumb by any precision standard, and devastatingly effective as area denial and sensor-drowning weather. |
| **Culverin Pattern** | Standard divisional gun | The Imperium's actual backbone: the tube every house owns hundreds of, because there was never a serious argument for owning anything else. |
| **Mangonel Pattern** | Light mobile support | Keeps pace with the tide, feeding close support into a breach faster than a Bombard battery can traverse. |
| **Naphtha Pattern** | Saturation / incendiary rocket | A garbled homage to an older fire the Imperium is fond of remembering. Area-denial rockets fired in volleys, prized less for accuracy than for the fact that nothing survives where the volley lands, sensor included. |

Guild tubes are allocated on the same election-deciding, court-watched terms as siege-breaker leases, and inspire the same anxiety: a house denied its accustomed Culverin allotment in a given campaign season is a house the Forge has judged, and everyone at Elysium reads the shipping manifests accordingly.

The UGF's fires doctrine is the mirror image: fewer shells, better information, no barrage as such. The **H-14** self-propelled howitzer and the towed **H-9** railgun deliver rail-assisted, sensor-slaved fires against pre-identified targets — a drone marks a position, the fire-control net verifies it, a single round arrives. For targets that need to disappear rather than merely be suppressed, the **H-22** precision-guided rocket system delivers what the ground war's veterans already describe without needing a manual: a bunker flagged in the morning, and gone before lunch. One army buys shells by the trainload and calls it weather. The other buys certainty by the round and calls it accountable to the taxpayer. Both bury people either way.

## 6. Air Defense: Why the Sky Stays Shut

Every armor and rifle pattern above answers to the same overhead fact: orbit is contested, low orbit over a defended world is a killing ground, and the reason it stays that way is ground-based air defense dense enough to make it so. House **Mutapa** did not invent air defense, but it is, by unanimous professional agreement on both sides of the wire, the house that mastered it, and it has turned that mastery into the nearest thing the Imperium has to a voluntary standard.

The **Munhumutapa Pattern flak array** — named, without much subtlety, for the house's own garbled dynastic title — is the mass tier: simple, dense, house-built, and licensed to every other chair at Mutapa's price. It is the one weapon system in the Imperium that eleven jealous houses have all quietly agreed to buy the same version of, for the same reason everyone agrees dying is bad. The guided tier, the **Nyanga Pattern interceptor**, reaches further and hits what it aims at, but needs Guild-grade fire-control cores to do it — which means even Mutapa's own guns run partly on borrowed Guild sanctity, a fact the house's engineers acknowledge with visible discomfort. Mutapa sells the doctrine that nets a thousand independent flak batteries into one coherent picture; it does not, quite, own the parts that make the net see. *Who actually owns the sky over Halvard's World is a question the lease paperwork has never had to answer, because nobody has ever needed to test it in court.*

The UGF's answer overhead is the same answer it gives everywhere: a number, a network, and no ceremony — the **D-8** mobile surface-to-air system, networked into the same targeting picture as everything else INDMOB ships, and the static **D-3** point-defense flak that rings every fixed UGF installation worth the name. Nothing about Union air defense is glamorous. It does not need to be. It works with the same grim consistency as everything else the Union builds, and the soldiers under it have stopped thanking it out loud, the way no one thanks the roof for staying up.

Between the two nets, the sky over every seriously contested world in the Reach has been closed for decades, and both staffs have made their peace with fighting a war entirely in the mud, exactly as the geometry demands.

Neither side has grounded itself entirely — both nets leave gaps for their own side to fly, briefly, on purpose. House Mutapa flies the **Rukwa-pattern strike wing** in the minutes its own batteries stand down to let a sortie through: close support timed to a single push, not a standing air arm, and gone again before the net closes behind it — the "aircraft" a UGF scouting report logs alongside a house's tanks and mechanized formations, present for one battle and absent for the next ten. INDMOB fields the equivalent restraint from the other side: strike craft launched off orbital tenders for a single deconfliction window and recovered before the targeting picture updates, carried on the books as a fires asset rather than an air force, because INDMOB's ledgers have no line item for anything that doesn't come home by design. Both are, in practice, artillery that happens to fly.

## 7. Drones: Recon Partners

UGF doctrine integrates unmanned reconnaissance at the platoon level as a matter of law as much as tactics — the Union's autonomy ceiling keeps a human hand on every trigger, but nothing forbids a machine from *looking*, and the UGF looks constantly. The **GR-6 Kestrel** is the squad-level tossable: palm-sized, thrown by hand through a doorway or over a wire before anyone follows it. The **GR-11 Petrel** is the platoon's standing eye, a quadrotor that loiters above an advance and feeds the squad network continuously. The **GR-9 Heron** walks rather than flies — a route-clearance and jamming crawler that goes first down roads nobody trusts, because it is cheaper to lose a Heron than a soldier, and INDMOB has never pretended otherwise.

None of these fly weapons; that decision belongs elsewhere in the Union's soul, and stays there. What they do is see, first, continuously, and tell the truth about what a sector actually holds — which is why a UGF platoon's drone handler is treated, by long informal custom, less like a technician than like a scout who happens to stay dry. Soldiers name their Kestrels. INDMOB pretends not to notice, exactly as the DPI pretends not to see the paint under a Rampart's gun.

The Imperium fields almost nothing comparable. Feudal armies built around house pride and precedence have no institutional home for a machine that reports to everyone equally, and the few houses that have tried — Ming, mostly, whose wealth buys most things, and Mutapa, whose engineers see the appeal — field scout craft in handfuls, Guild-licensed, expensive, and regarded by levies with the same superstitious unease as any other machine that moves without a visible hand on it. A UGF drone is a tool the infantry trusts. An Imperial scout craft, on the rare front where one is seen at all, is a small flying mystery the chaplains have not yet been asked to rule on.

## 8. Logistics: From Tender to Mud

Everything above is worthless without the last three kilometers, and the last three kilometers are where both sides' quartermasters actually live.

The UGF's ground logistics run on the flattest naming convention in the whole arsenal: the **T-9** general hauler, universally *the mule*; the **T-14** heavy convoy truck for line-of-communication runs between depot and forward dump; the **T-3** field ambulance. What matters is not the vehicles but the discipline behind them — a tender crosses the shoal, survives the window, offloads at a secure landing head, and from there the T-series moves cargo forward under drone escort on a published, ruthlessly kept schedule, because the whole system runs on the promise the same way the bond drives do. A UGF supply column missing its slot is treated, up the chain, exactly like a missed resupply window in orbit: an emergency, logged, escalated, fixed if it can be fixed and mourned honestly if it cannot.

The Imperial pipeline is the same physics wearing entirely different clothes. Ground haulage runs on the **Tumbrel-pattern** — a garbled, faintly ominous name for what is, on a well-funded front, a grav-sled convoy and, on an indebted marcher lord's front, literal draft beasts hitched to the last three kilometers because nobody in the bench-house budget has renewed the grav contract. **House Ming**, which already owns the arteries of the ground war in orbit, has quietly bought its way into the ground pipeline too — leasing Tumbrel fleets to neighboring houses on the same terms it leases tithe-tenders, which means the old intelligence maxim scales down without changing a word: you do not fight House Ming's convoys. You fight everyone House Ming has fed, one muddy kilometer at a time.

## 9. Military Organization

### The UGF: one pyramid, everywhere

The UGF's combined-arms structure is as standardized as its rifles, and built to the same logic: a soldier transferred between formations should be productive by the end of the first watch. The basic building blocks:

| Echelon | Approximate strength | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Fireteam | 4 | The atomic unit; never split further |
| Squad | ~9 | Standard infantry element, one Kestrel handler |
| Platoon | ~40 | Adds a Petrel and its handler, a medic |
| Company | ~170 | First echelon with its own heavy weapons |
| Battalion | ~800 | First echelon that plans, not just executes |
| Brigade combat team | ~4,800 | The basic deployable formation: infantry, armor, fires, engineers, and drones under one commander |
| Division | ~16,000 | The formation the Assembly counts in; a division's casualty report is a by-election |
| Expeditionary army | ~90,000 | The scale of the Ninth at Corvant — and of the inquiry afterward |

Fourteen-month tours are honored at every echelon, mid-campaign or not, because the whole architecture assumes rotation the way it assumes resupply: on schedule, or the system stops meaning anything. The Union's real command structure lives, as it always has, in the NCO ranks rather than the insignia. A UGF colonel commands a brigade. A UGF sergeant makes sure it survives contact with a plan that has already failed.

### The Imperium: no pyramid at all

There is no Imperial table of organization, full stop, and no house has ever proposed one — a common structure would imply a common army, and a common army would imply a common house, which is precisely the argument the Council has spent nine centuries not having. What follows are illustrations, not a system.

**House Yamatai** fields no mass levy at all. Its basic and often only unit is the **Yashima banner** — a sworn regiment of a few hundred hand-picked retainers, drawn from vetted families, named for its ancestral colors, and never diluted with outside levies to make good its losses. A Yamatai "army" for a given campaign might run to a dozen banners: a few thousand men, tiny beside a Rurikov levy, and rated Grade One anyway. Losses are mourned. They are never replaced with strangers.

**House Rurikov** fields the opposite structure entirely: village and district levies pooled into **sotnyas** (nominally a hundred, rarely at strength) and massed into **polks** — thousands strong, commanded by a **voyvod** who answers, in practice, to nobody until the campaign ends and the casualty count is read at Elysium. A Rurikov winter offensive might commit a dozen polks — tens of thousands of men whose individual training and equipment would embarrass a Yamatai retainer, arriving in numbers a defended position cannot arithmetically survive.

**House Chandragupt** fields a structure that is, in its way, stranger than either: **Ganana companies**, raised to an exact audited quota from a specific province and tracked, individually, in ledgers a Chandragupt staff officer can produce on demand. A Ganana that has taken losses runs at a documented deficit — the deficit is filed, not felt, and replacements are requisitioned like inventory rather than mourned like men, which the other houses find distasteful and Chandragupt finds honest.

Below all of it sit the **Vatborn legions**, roughly ten thousand strong (the Fortieth, lost in the Greywater, was typical in this if in nothing else), Guild-decanted and house-sworn. They carry the setting's one true accident of standardization: because a legion is raised from a single Guild crèche-and-armory contract, every rifle issued to it, house-pattern or not, comes off the same production run. It is the only place in the Imperium where every soldier in a formation carries an identical weapon — not by policy, which no house has ever adopted, but by the plain economics of buying ten thousand men and their arms in one transaction. *No quartermaster designed it. No Remembrancer will bless it. It is, by pure accident, the most efficient army in the Imperium, and the Imperium does not think of it as an army at all.*

## 10. Captured Kit: What the Engineers Say

Both sides study what they take off the other, and both sides come away unsettled in opposite directions.

A UGF ordnance technician who gets a Guild-forged Culverin tube intact does not treat it like scrap. It goes back to New Geneva under guard heavier than most prisoners rate, into DRS's queue, where it joins a slow, well-funded, largely fruitless project to understand a metallurgy that behaves, on every test the Deferred Systems Annex has devised, exactly as advertised and utterly resists replication. A captured house-pattern rifle is worth less as a weapon than as an index: which house's levies hold a given sector is legible from the rifle alone, and DIS values the intelligence a great deal more than INDMOB values the gun.

An Imperial house armorer confronted with a captured **R-19** has a harder time being honest about what he thinks of it. The rail-assist coil is small, clever, and embarrassingly easy to understand once opened — which is itself the insult; Imperial pride runs on things being difficult to make, not merely difficult to explain. More than one house armory has quietly cloned the coil into a house-pattern rifle of its own, unofficially, unblessed, and never once mentioned at Elysium — a small, practical heresy that sits, in the Imperium's own moral ledger, somewhere near Normand's bought heraldry: everyone suspects it happens, no one has ruled on it, and the silence is doing useful work for everybody. Other houses go the other direction and destroy captured Union gear ceremonially, in front of the levies, as a demonstration that Apostate ironmongery holds no fascination here — a demonstration that requires, of course, that there was fascination to perform against.

A Guild wright, asked once to appraise a captured R-19 pulled from beside a dead rifleman on a quiet stretch of the Long Front, is recorded to have turned it over twice, sighted down the barrel, and handed it back with the entire assessment the incident produced: *"The barrel's honest. I don't know what to do with that."* Nobody has improved on the line since, on either side of the wire, because nobody has needed to.

## Open questions

- Calibers genuinely do not cross house lines. Has a joint Imperial operation ever been lost specifically because two allied units ran dry of ammunition the other couldn't spare — and if so, whose fault did the inquiry find it, and whose fault was it actually?
- The Vatborn legions are the one accidentally standardized force in the Imperium. Do quartermasters outside Osmanli notice this, and has anyone in a poorer house ever quietly wished his own men were Vatborn, purely for the logistics?
- Munhumutapa Pattern exports mean Mutapa sells the air defense it also charges other houses to lease and staff. Who profits when a Nyanga battery bought from Mutapa shoots down an attack meant for Mutapa's own sector on some other front?
- House armorers who clone captured R-19 coils are committing a small, unblessed heresy everyone tolerates. Has anyone ever been formally punished for it — and if the College of Remembrancers were ever asked to rule, which way would the theology actually fall?
- Is there a name — official or slang, either side — for the specific failure of two allied units unable to resupply each other under fire? If the UGF's Bestiary has coined one, does the Imperium have a matching word, and do the two words mean the same thing?
- The Deferred Systems Annex has held captured Guild tubes for years without reproducing the metallurgy. Is this a failure of Union science, a Guild countermeasure baked into the forging itself, or something the Annex has learned and simply not filed?
- Ganana companies track their dead as documented deficits rather than casualties. Has any Chandragupt officer ever refused to file a deficit report — and what, in an audited house, does refusal even look like?
- A UGF platoon's drone handlers name their Kestrels against regulation, and INDMOB looks away. Has a Kestrel's unofficial name ever ended up in an after-action report, on the record, because there was no other way to say which drone did what?

# The Holy Terran Imperium
*One thousand years of misremembering, ruled from beside the grave.*

---

## 1. What It Claims to Be

The Imperium does not call itself the Concord's successor. Successor is a word for heirs, and heirs admit a death. The Imperium's constitutional theory — proclaimed at the **Acclamation of Mars** and unrevised since — is that the Concord of Man never ended: the High Assembly stands adjourned, and the throne holds the chamber in trust until the ancestors' house is made whole. Every Imperial institution is built on that single act of refusal. The Great Council meets in the Hall of Concord itself, among the Assembly's empty benches, which are dusted daily and never sat in. The emperor's formal style includes *Warden of the Adjournment*. The Union is not, in Imperial law, a foreign power; it is a quorum problem.

The capital is **Mars, fully terraformed** — green, wealthy, and arranged so that its holiest architecture faces the sky. Dead Earth hangs above it, the supreme shrine, and the Imperium's legitimacy is, in the end, a grave-tending: whoever keeps the tomb speaks for the family.

The Imperium is not nostalgic. Nostalgia admits the thing is gone.

## 2. Why It Is Feudal

Deliberate suppression. The population is kept agrarian, dense, and manageable. **Cloning technology** lets the Imperium rapidly seed new worlds and mass-produce soldiers and labor — removing the historical pressures (armed peasantry, labor scarcity) that ended feudalism on old Earth.

This deserves saying plainly, because the Imperium never will: its feudalism is not a survival, it is an *installation*. The founding magnates of the Sol sphere — governors-become-dynasts, charter companies fossilizing into bloodlines — had watched the Concord dissolve for want of anything that could hold worlds together across silence, and chose the one social technology with a proven record of running civilizations on loyalty, land, and awe, without couriers. It worked. It has kept hundreds of worlds obedient for nine centuries. Whether anyone now living understands that it was a choice is another matter: the great houses teach the order of estates as sacred inheritance, and the Guild-tended machinery that makes it stable — the crèches, the deliberate scarcity of learning, the long slow strangling of every ladder but the ones the throne owns — is administered by men who believe they are conserving, not constructing.

The first dynasts chose feudalism the way an engineer chooses a material. Their heirs worship the material.

## 3. Structure

- **Elective monarchy.** The great houses choose the emperor. HRE-flavored politics: bribery, marriage, kingmaking elector houses.
- **The Great Council / Council of Houses** — the assembly of houses; also the sole authority the Artificer Guilds answer to. It sits in the Hall of Concord at Elysium, in the adjourned Assembly's chamber, held in trust.
- **The great houses** — each is a loose, garbled derivative of a medieval or ancient Earth culture. Not authentic preservation: thousand-year-old *misrememberings* fossilized into real aristocratic cultures, defended with religious fervor.

### The chairs

Twelve great houses hold **chairs** in the Great Council, and the chairs elect the throne: seven voices crown an emperor. Below the chairs sit the benches — dozens of lesser houses, sworn marcher lords, and (by careful fiction, at the back, without voice) the envoys of client princes. The chairs have not always been twelve. A thirteenth stands in the chamber to this day, draped in gray felt, and has stood so for three centuries: the chair of **House Sarkany**, which the Guilds interdicted and the other houses ate (see below). No one sits in it. Novgoroy's Prince, who campaigns openly for elevation, is understood to be campaigning for that chair, which every superstition in the Imperium advises against wanting.

### What an emperor is

Between elections, the emperor commands the **Sacred Armada**, declares and concludes the Imperium's wars (the houses' private wars are their own affair, regulated mostly by exhaustion), collects the imperial tithe, grants and — with the Council's assent — strips titles, and embodies the Adjournment. What he cannot do is nearly everything else. He cannot touch a house's internal succession. He cannot command the Guilds, or anyone else's army, except by the slow leverage of honor and shame — which, as the Emperor's Wager proved, is more than it sounds. And he reigns under his **Undertakings**: the sheaf of promises signed to the electors before his crowning, different for every emperor, negotiated chair by chair. Every emperor signs Undertakings. Every emperor spends his reign interpreting his way out of them.

### How an election runs

On a vacancy, the crown, seal, and regalia pass into Guild custody — *the Guilds hold the crown between reigns, so that no house does* — and the chairs convene as **the Conclave**. What follows is the Imperium's supreme political art form: months (the record is four years) of marriages proposed and withdrawn, of *gratitudes* that are certainly not bribes conveyed by houses that certainly did not send them, of candidacies floated to die so that the real candidate looks like relief. The Conclave may take as long as it likes. The coronation cannot: by rite it is held at dawn on the steps of the Hall of Concord, with dead Earth standing as the morning star above Elysium — as it stood for Kostantin I — and the heavens do not negotiate. More than one election has accelerated suddenly because the astronomers gave the Conclave a date.

The four-year interregnum is remembered as the **Regency of the Forge**: the Guilds held the crown, no man reigned, and the largest empire in human space functioned identically. The houses drew no lesson from this. The Guilds may have.

## 4. The Emperor: Alexios IV

**Alexios IV of House Palaiogos** was elected in A.M. 1095 — fifty-five years ago — as the compromise nobody feared: a mild, scholarly Palaiogos of middling reputation, agreeable to eleven chairs precisely because he seemed unlikely to use the twelfth. Three years into his reign, House Normand's guns at Serevan handed him a war he had not sought and could not refuse. He has presided over it for fifty-two years — which is to say, he has spent half a century keeping a war exactly the size the houses will pay for, a feat his admirers compare to seamanship and his enemies to drift.

Once, he did more. In the war's twenty-second year he staked the Sacred Armada — the ancestors' own fleet — to force nine jealous houses to sail as one, and the Imperium calls that battle **the Emperor's Wager** to this day (the Union calls it Cerulyn Deep; the account belongs to the void war). The Wager made him: the compromise nobody feared became the emperor who had wagered the holiest thing in the Imperium and not lost it. It also unmade his successors in advance. The houses watched an emperor discover what the throne could compel, and the Conclave-in-waiting has spent thirty years quietly resolving never to crown another man who might.

Now he is 97, and failing. He has not left the palace complex at Elysium in six years; the audiences continue, briefer each season, behind the **porphyry rail** that separates the throne from petitioners, at a distance kind to failing men. The day-to-day empire is signed by the **Grand Logothete**, Theodora Kantakuzen, an office and a family that have served the purple so long that no one can name the date the signatures changed meaning — which is, of course, the point. The Imperium is administered in the name of a man almost no one has seen stand.

The empire's official position is that the emperor is well. The empire's actual position is visible in the shipping registries: every chair's household fleet has found reasons, this year, to keep hulls near Mars.

## 5. The Election

There has been no election in fifty-five years. Half the men who will vote in the next one have never voted in one; the art has been kept alive in theory, like a liturgy for a feast that stopped coming. And it looms now over everything — the war's conduct is campaign material, every offensive in the Reach is read at Elysium as a candidacy speaking, and the great houses are maneuvering with the unhurried violence of people who have waited decades and intend not to wait one hour longer than necessary.

The postures, as the whole Imperium reads them:

- **House Karolus runs as the war.** Duke **Lothar Karolus** has spent a generation building the largest committed house army in the Reach and a doctrine of coalition victories, and his candidacy is explicit: crown the man who will *finish* it. A Karolus throne means the Undertakings of total war — the ceiling of fifty-two years, torn down on purpose. The UGF's analysts and the Union's Directory read Karolus offensives the way the houses do: as electioneering conducted in corpses.
- **House Habsberg runs no one, and is running hardest of all.** Archduke **Rudolf-Amade Habsberg** has married three grandchildren into three chairs since the emperor stopped walking. Habsberg's product is the seventh voice — the majority-maker — and its price will be Undertakings, offices, and marriages, payable to House Habsberg in perpetuity. They lose every battle and win every election.
- **House Yamatai stands on prestige.** Their presumptive candidate is Lord **Benkei Ashiwara** — the marshal of Corvant, the man who paraded his regiments to salute a starving enemy, possibly the only commander both empires' soldiers would trust to keep a treaty. His difficulty is at home: his own house's chronicle is silent on the episode the rest of the Imperium reveres him for, for reasons the house does not give, and a candidate whose sanctity embarrasses his own chair is a candidate half-armed.
- **House Palaiogos maneuvers to keep what it has.** The dynasty's man is **Andronikos Palaiogos**, the emperor's grand-nephew — young, correct, unproven, and campaigning on continuity: the Wager's house, the tomb's keepers, the safe pair of hands. House Palaiogos has won the throne back more often than statistics can politely excuse, and the other chairs have begun saying so out loud.
- **House Ming bankrolls several candidates at once**, holds the paper on at least two more, and wants nothing so vulgar as the crown. Ming wants the crown *in debt*.

The remaining chairs — Rurikov, Normand, Osmanli, Chandragupt, Mutapa, Jorvik, Aztlan — are the electorate, and their prices are the Conclave's real agenda. Rurikov's price is known to everyone: Novgoroy stays a client forever. Normand's is absolution for starting the war. Aztlan's price has never been successfully predicted by anyone, which is why every campaign courts them last, nervously.

What each outcome means for the war is arithmetic the staff colleges of both powers have already run — a war candidate moves the ceiling; a disputed election recalls house armies and hands the Union a windfall it cannot digest (the ground war's file counts the cracks). The Union's DIS maintains, by reliable rumor, a standing estimate of the Conclave called *the other front*.

Fifty-two years of war have not moved the border a tenth as far as one dawn on the steps of the Hall could.

## 6. The Twelve Great Houses

Each house is a garbled derivative of an Earth culture that no longer exists to correct it — a mosaic with no single imperial flavor. The sketches below are the bible's view; the UGF's franker one is catalogued in the ground war's file as the Bestiary.

### House Palaiogos — *the purple*
Byzantine-pastiche; holders of the current throne and, by the Council's polite non-accounting, of a suspicious plurality of past ones. Seat at **Porphyra**, a world named for a color, where the dye-vats are an industry, a liturgy, and a monopoly enforced with tariffs and scripture. Ancient, ceremonial, cunning; their armies are parade-ground mediocrities save the **Porphyry Guard**, which appears only where the throne's honor is staked and fights accordingly; their fleet is the oldest and holiest in private hands, sacred and slow. Palaiogos does not win arguments. It outlives them.

### House Karolus — *the war candidate*
Garbled Carolingian; claims descent from "Karl the Great, first uniter of Earth," a genealogy the Remembrancers certify and historians on both sides decline to. Seat at **Aix**. The largest committed house army in the Reach, aggressive, orthodox, and — uniquely among the houses — genuinely cooperative in coalition, because Duke Lothar needs coalition victories to claim the throne. Their flagship *Karl Vindicant* appears in every war-hawk broadsheet as the shape of imperial resolve. Karolus is what the Imperium looks like when it means it.

### House Habsberg — *the marriage-brokers*
Garbled Habsburg; seat at **Ostmark**. A small, indifferent army that has not won an unassisted battle in living memory, wrapped in the most dangerous diplomatic instrument in the Imperium: a web of marriage-treaties that drags crack allied formations onto any field where Habsberg blood is spilled. Their squadron arrived at the Emperor's Wager, with exquisite timing, four days after the battle. Kingmakers by trade and temperament; they have held the throne twice in nine centuries and found it, both times, bad for business.

### House Yamatai — *the blade kept sheathed*
Nipponic pastiche; seat at **Yashima**. An honor culture misremembered through a dozen filters into something with its own austere integrity: small crack regiments, the finest infantry in the Imperium, night assaults launched in total silence, the Ceres Conventions observed to the letter even when — especially when — the enemy does not. Yamatai's word is the one Imperial currency the Union accepts at face value. What it costs to maintain that currency, the house's own chronicle does not say.

### House Rurikov — *winter's landlords*
Rus pastiche; seat at **Zimny**. Winter-world levies in numbers that embarrass arithmetic, a staff culture of glacial patience, and the tempo doctrine's truest believers — they attack in conditions that ground drones and thicken sensor fog, which is to say they attack in winter, which is to say they hold the Imperium's cold fronts by ancient arrangement and are never rotated out. Blocks the elevation of its client-rival Novgoroy at every Council, publicly on procedure, actually because a thousand years of fossilized misremembering is an inheritance Rurikov has no intention of splitting.

### House Normand — *the conquest that keeps not finishing*
Pseudo-Norman; seat at **Roan**. Marcher lords with conquest in place of a culture: the finest mechanized assault formations in the Imperium, breakthrough artists to two hundred hours and beggars thereafter, chronically overextended in law as well as logistics — Normand has issued titles to volumes of space it has never visited, one of which started the war. It has never expressed regret for Serevan; regret would concede the writ. Its neglect of its own sworn clients cost the Imperium the Margraviate of Kest without a shot, a fact every other chair mentions whenever Normand speaks of honor.

### House Osmanli — *the guns, and the waves that think*
Ottoman pastiche; seat at **Edrine**, called the Sublime Harbor. The heaviest, best-served artillery in the Imperium — their barrages are the weather the other houses merely forecast — and, more consequentially, the Imperium's great anomaly: Osmanli fields the most Vatborn and treats them best, a misremembered slave-soldier tradition inverted into the one ladder in the Imperium that a batchman can climb. Osmanli Vatborn are long-service, promoted on merit, proud, and *retained*. The other houses call the practice unsound. The other houses' levies, facing Osmanli's, call it something else.

### House Ming — *merchants in crowns*
Garbled Chinese; seat at **Zhongdu**. The richest house by an interval that changes hands only among Ming accountants: bureaucratic, ceremonial, magnificent, and despised — *merchants in crowns* — and indispensable, because Ming's vast tithe-tender fleets are the arteries of the ground war, and where Ming administers, every neighboring house's army is fed, fueled, and paid. Its garrisons are competent and visibly uninterested in dying for anyone's precedence; its squadrons are magnificently maintained and mysteriously never in the van. Ming has not sought the throne in four centuries. It prefers the mortgage.

### House Chandragupt — *the audited ocean*
Mauryan pastiche; seat at **Pataliputta**. Enormous populations, vast levies, and a philosophical bureaucracy running inside the feudal shell like a second engine: Chandragupt's civil examinations are the closest thing the Imperium has to a meritocracy, and its staff colleges calculate what a position is worth in lives, pay exactly that, and stop. The other houses find the auditing of glory distasteful. Chandragupt finds glory distasteful, and keeps winning sieges by owning the arithmetic everyone else performs by prayer.

### House Mutapa — *the houses of stone*
Great Zimbabwe pastiche; seat at **Dzimba**. Fortress-builders in their misremembered myth and in their perfectly remembered practice: the Imperium's siege engineers and air-defense masters, the reason the sky stays shut over Imperial armies, the authors of the reduction of Vell — taught, through gritted teeth, at the Union's own staff college. A month of Mutapa occupation closes the orbit and honeycombs the ground. Their courtly culture prizes permanence over display; a Mutapa lord's boast is what will still be standing.

### House Jorvik — *the bill that comes down like weather*
Garbled Norse-and-York; the house and its world are both **Jorvik**, and neither yields precedence. Void-raiders and boarders without equal — their **drakkes** and their boarding-carrier *Hrafnking* hold the war's tally of captured hulls, and paint no trophies, the absence being the boast. Deep, uneasy, centuries-old kinship with the Ederi Khagans, whom they raid with, trade with, and marry, to the Council's institutional despair. Jorvik holds ground badly and knows it; its entire strategic culture is arrivals and departures.

### House Aztlan — *the flower war*
Mesoamerican pastiche; seat at **Tenocht**. Shock infantry beyond rational doctrine, assaults pressed home through fire that breaks every other house, all of it moving on a ritual calendar that outside analysts can partially predict and not at all understand. Religiously intense even by Imperial standards — Aztlan's observances predate its oaths to the throne, a sentence the Remembrancers have been declining to examine for nine hundred years. They take prisoners by strong preference. The Council does not ask about the prisoners. The Council has never asked about the prisoners.

*Dozens of lesser houses hold benches, marches, and grudges below the chairs — any one of them a novel; none of them a vote.*

## 7. Religion

An **ancestor/founder cult** centered on the Concord of Man. House genealogies as scripture; Concord-era relic-ships as holy objects; dead Earth as the supreme shrine. The UFS's secession is framed as apostasy — desecration of the ancestors.

### The Remembrancers

The priesthood is the **College of Remembrancers**: keepers of the genealogies, fixers of the calendar (it was Remembrancer liturgists who set Year 0, working backward from mourning-rites), celebrants of the dead. Every house feast is, at bottom, a reading — the names of the house's dead, in order, aloud; the great houses' full readings take days, and attendance is politics. Children's names are loans from the dead, granted by the Remembrancers and repaid with deeds. The threshold-catechism is recited at every crossing into Sol. Above all the College administers the **pilgrimage**: the silent orbit of dead Earth, engines cold, transmitters dark — which it has never once closed to Apostates, because every pilgrim is an argument for the Imperial story.

### Scripture and forgery

Because the genealogies are scripture, the Imperium's gravest heresy is **genealogical fraud**: to forge an ancestor is to counterfeit the sacred dead. Houses have been broken for it; wars of the benches have been fought over an interpolated grandmother. The relic-ships sit adjacent to the same nerve — Guild assayers have privately noted that several celebrated relics have hull plating younger than the events they witnessed, and the notes stay private, because the College and the Guilds long ago arrived at an unspoken concordat: the Guilds do not date the relics, and the College does not ask what the Guilds believe.

### The Vatborn question

The unresolved doctrinal knife-fight at the cult's heart: does a decanted man have ancestors? The batch has no lineage; the gene-stock does. One school holds the Vatborn soul inherits with the flesh and every batchman is, technically, scripture's kin to the lines he was drawn from — a ruling with consequences no house wishes to fund. The other holds the Vatborn stand outside the genealogies entirely — a ruling that would make a fifth of the Imperium's soldiers theologically disposable, and say so out loud. The College has drafted rulings. The College has sealed them. It has been sealing them for two hundred years, because any answer moves the price of a Vatborn, and the war is priced to the current ambiguity.

The Imperium's church knows the answer to its hardest question is in a drawer. It considers the drawer a form of mercy.

## 8. The Vatborn

- Formal term: **the Vatborn.** Rude term: **batchmen.**
- Decanted in batches to seed colonies, fill legions, staff factories.

Their civil status is a patchwork the Imperium has never dared harmonize: property in the harsher marches, hearth-subjects in most house law, soldiers everywhere, and — under Osmanli colors alone — officers. The crèches are Guild-run, which means no house fully controls the composition of its own population, a fact the houses have collectively agreed to find unremarkable. Whole villages, whole worlds, were seeded from the vats in the settlement generations; whether their descendants know is unknowable, because the Imperium has never audited which of its villages began in a vat. This is not an oversight. After enough generations of natural birth, the distinction dissolves into rumor and slander — *batch-blood* is a thing said at village weddings, and occasionally proven wrong at the cost of a feud.

What the Vatborn themselves believe — their quiet batch-cultures, their songs, what a batchman walks toward when he walks into the guns — is treated in the ground war's file, and remains one of the setting's great mysteries. Ask the institutions what a Vatborn is and every one gives a different answer. Ask a Vatborn and he will tell you his batch.

## 9. The Military

- Houses command their own armies and fleets.
- The masses: levied peasants and Vatborn legions. Doctrine of expendable quantity.
- How this fights — the tempo doctrine, the siege-breaker mechs, the feudal patchwork at war — is the ground war's file; the house fleets and the Sacred Armada are the void war's.

### The Paladins

**Paladins** — power-armored super-soldiers chosen from proven loyalists. The one ladder a commoner or even a Vatborn can climb.

There have never been many. A great house fields Paladins by the dozen, not the thousand; the Imperium entire musters fewer Paladins than the UGF has colonels. Selection is by deed alone — the single Imperial institution where birth buys nothing — and the deed must be *witnessed*, which has made witnessing its own quiet economy on every front.

Elevation is the empire's most sacred ceremony, and it is easy to see why once it is understood what the ceremony does: the candidate keeps a night's vigil beside the genealogy of the house he will serve, and at dawn his name is written into it. Into scripture. A commoner enters the sacred dead; his descendants, if he lives to have any, are kin to the line forever. And when the candidate is Vatborn, the Imperium — wisely, without ever ruling on the theology — performs the same rite, and thereby manufactures an ancestor for a man who had none. The Remembrancers attend these elevations in force. They have never explained whether they attend in celebration or in study.

The armor is relic: named suits, older than their bearers, maintained by the Guilds and inherited from the dead — a Paladin is fitted to the armor, never the reverse, and most suits have borne more names than any genealogy cares to count. A Paladin's honor is the one aristocratic virtue in the Imperium that is real, because it is paid in person.

## 10. The Artificer Guilds

- A single trans-imperial order maintaining all high technology — and the clone-crèches.
- Exist **outside** the noble hierarchy; answer only to the Great Council.
- Every house depends on them; a Guild interdict is deadlier than any army. Arguably the third superpower nobody counts.

### Structure

The order ranks **prentice**, **wright**, and **magister**, and is governed by the **Synod of the Forge**, whose seat no house has ever been invited to visit and whose sessions no outsider has attended in nine centuries. Its presence in the Imperium is the **enclaves**: walled Guild quarters in every house's territory and in favored client states, extraterritorial by immemorial custom, each part workshop, part seminary, part embassy. Prentices are taken young — by tithe from the commons, by contract from the levies, by arrangements with the crèches that no one has ever seen written down — and are returned to nowhere: a prentice's family receives an honorarium, a certificate, and no further news, ever. The Imperium finds this price acceptable. The Imperium is not consulted.

### The monopolies

The Guilds forge and anoint every capital keel and every shield core in Imperial service; they build and lease the siege-breaker mechs; they run the crèches; they maintain the **reckoning-engines** — the narrow machines that compute fire solutions and keep centuries-old hulls alive, which *reckon* and do not *advise*, a line the Guilds hold with theological ferocity and refuse to explain theologically. Houses may build escort hulls, small arms, tractors, and glory. Everything that decides wars is Guild work, on Guild terms, at Guild prices — prices that have decided elections.

### The interdict

An interdict is not sabotage; the Guilds are never so crude. The enclave gates close. The wrights stop coming. And the machines — the shield cores, the crèches, the reckoning-engines, the thousand systems only Guild hands may touch — begin to age a decade a season, until the interdicted house is defending thousand-year pretensions with what it can maintain itself, which is to say, with tractors and glory.

It has fallen in full exactly once in living memory of the institution: three centuries ago, on **House Sarkany** — then a chaired great house. What Sarkany did is not taught, not recorded in any open archive, and not asked; the not-asking is understood to be part of the sentence. Within a generation its worlds were carved up by its neighbors with the Council's blessing, its genealogy was closed — not struck: *closed*, a distinction the Remembrancers insist upon and decline to explain — and its gray-draped chair still stands in the Hall of Concord, teaching.

### The agenda

What the Guilds want is the Imperium's oldest unasked question. The theories are catalogued: that they are conserving human knowledge through a dark age they believe is not over; that they serve the Adjournment more literally than the throne does, keeping the machinery ready for a Concord that may yet reconvene; that they are simply what a monopoly becomes when it outlives every government it has ever served. The Guilds endow no chairs, claim no worlds, crown no emperors — they merely hold the crown between them. They have never picked a side in the war, and both empires' planning assumes they never will.

The Guilds keep the Imperium alive the way a falconer feeds a hawk. It is not affection. It is not explained.

## 11. Commoners and Technology

Commoners know starships and high tech exist, but it is so far removed from their lives that they cannot comprehend it. A shuttle landing in a barley field reads as an angel.

The village's whole experience of the machine age arrives on schedule: the tithe-shuttle at harvest, the Guild wagon when the mill breaks, the levy-officer's climate-controlled tent that is warm in winter by no fire anyone can see. None of it is hidden — the Imperium conceals nothing, which is far more effective than secrecy. The knowledge is simply priced out of reach: schooling runs through the parish Remembrancer, apprenticeship through the Guild tithe, and both ladders are exactly as wide as the throne requires. A bright village child who asks how the shuttle flies will be told, accurately, *the Guilds keep it*, and will learn nothing further in her lifetime unless the Guilds take her, in which case her village will learn nothing further of *her*.

The stars over an Imperial village are, in the most literal sense, feudal: everyone knows lords live there. Everyone is right.

## Open questions

- What did House Sarkany do? The Guilds know; the Remembrancers *closed* rather than struck its genealogy, which implies a verdict. Whose mercy was that — and is the gray chair a warning, or a reservation?
- The College's sealed rulings on the Vatborn soul: what do they conclude — and have the Guilds, who run the crèches, ever been asked, or ever answered unasked?
- House Palaiogos wins elections beyond statistical excuse. Habsberg brokerage, Remembrancer favor, Guild custody of the regalia — or a mechanism nine centuries of jealous rivals have failed to find?
- On what date did the Grand Logothete's signature stop transmitting the emperor's will and start replacing it? Someone in the chancery knows the honest answer to a day.
- Habsberg has held the throne twice and calls it bad for business. What happened, both times — and is *kingmaker forever* a strategy, or a scar?
- Where exactly do the Guild-crèche "arrangements no one has seen written down" run? Are any prentices Vatborn — and would the Synod consider the question meaningful?
- Aztlan's observances predate its oaths. What is actually on the ritual calendar the analysts can half-predict — and why has the College declined to examine it for nine hundred years?
- If the Conclave deadlocks past the astronomers' dawn, the rite must wait for the next one. Who benefits from a long interregnum — and has someone already done that arithmetic for the one now coming?

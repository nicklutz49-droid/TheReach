# History: the Concord, the Sundering, and the Road to War
*The most fiercely contested territory in human space is the past.*

---

## 1. The Concord of Man

### What it was

A thousand years ago, humanity was united under the **Concord of Man**. That single sentence is the whole of the agreed record. Everything after it — what the Concord was, how it worked, when it began, whether it deserved to die — is claimed, garbled, sanctified, or litigated by its two successor empires, each of which rules hundreds of worlds and insists the other has misread the corpse.

The Concord has no founding date. Every attempt to establish one lands in an argument between archives that were already contradicting each other while Earth was still alive. The honest position, held by a small and unpopular class of scholars on both sides, is that the Concord's origin is not a date but a horizon: humanity expanded outward for so long under one flag that no one thought to record when the flag went up. *Treat it as an age, not an event.*

At its height — whenever that was — the Concord's own registry claimed jurisdiction over more than a thousand settled worlds. The registry also lists worlds no one has ever found. Whether those entries are clerical ghosts or colonies still out there in the dark, keeping the old calendar, is a question both empires prefer not to think about.

### How it governed (a reconstruction)

What follows is the best sober reconstruction, assembled from relic-documents both sides accept as authentic — which is to say, it is disputed in every particular.

- **The High Assembly** — the Concord's legislature: one delegation per registered world, a quorum of one hundred seated delegates required to conduct business. It sat on Earth for most of its history. After the Death, it withdrew to Mars, to a purpose-built chamber on the edge of Elysium Planitia — the **Hall of Concord**. The last government of united humanity spent its final two centuries in a hall named for the afterlife.
- **The Ecumenical Survey** — the charter authority. It charted space, named what it charted, and allotted settlement rights by formal **Writs of Settlement**. Its master charts are the one document neither successor disputes, because both inherited the same copies: when Imperial and Union officers name a star the same thing, the name is the Survey's. It was the Survey that labeled the contested volume between the powers *the Farther Reach*. The one thing the two empires have never argued about is the name of the battlefield.
- **The Grand Courier Service** — the Concord's nervous system. With no signal faster than a ship, governance moved at the speed of scheduled courier circuits: laws, census returns, tax receipts, appointments, pardons, all riding the loops. A world's membership in the Concord was, in practice, its place on a courier schedule.
- **The Ceres Conventions** — the Concord's laws of armed conflict, drafted (per their own preamble) for suppressing piracy and rebellion, because the Concord never imagined organized war between human states. These are the "old Concord-era laws of war" both powers still cite at each other across the Reach — a legal code for police actions, stretched over a fifty-year war it was never built to hold.
- **Standard measures** — the **Concord Standard Year**, the standard day, the second: all keyed to the orbit and rotation of Earth, and never replaced. Every human world, on both sides of the war, keeps time by the turning of a dead planet.

How much of this machine actually functioned, and for how long, is unknowable. The Concord governed the way a lighthouse governs shipping: by being seen, believed, and mostly obeyed.

### What survives

- **Relic-ships.** A scattering of Concord-era hulls, preserved with varying honesty. The most famous is the **CS *Unanimity***, an Assembly courier enshrined in Martian orbit, aboard which — supposedly — half a dozen founding instruments were signed. Guild assayers have privately noted that several celebrated relic-ships have hull plating younger than the events they allegedly witnessed. The notes stay private.
- **The charters.** The Survey's registry survives in two great bodies: the original writs held in the **Charterhouse** on Mars, and a salvaged duplicate registry held in the **Registry Annex** at New Geneva. For most entries the two agree. Where they disagree, people die; see the Serevan Incident, below.
- **The calendar.** Both successor calendars are patches on the Concord's. The Imperium counts from the Death of Earth — Year 0 **A.M.**, *After Mourning* (earlier dates are styled **B.M.**). The Union counts from its own founding — **Charter Year** 0. Both still run on Concord Standard Years.
- **The language.** Both powers speak descendants of **Concordant**, the old lingua franca — the Union's plain "Standard" and the Imperium's everyday tongues beneath a liturgical register of **High Concordant**. They remain mutually intelligible with effort, which is worse than not at all: every negotiation is conducted in a shared language whose every second word has drifted. Envoys meeting through Skarn's back channels default to nine-hundred-year-old Concord diplomatic protocol, because it is the only etiquette both sides accept.
- **The paperwork.** The Union will not thank anyone for observing that its directorates, registries, and accreditation ladders are recognizably Concord administrative practice with the mysticism sanded off. The Union abolished the Concord's prayers and kept its filing system.

## 2. How Distance Killed It

No war destroyed the Concord — distance did. The mechanism deserves to be understood precisely, because both empires have built their souls on misunderstanding it.

There is no signal faster than a ship. A query from the Assembly to a rim world, and the reply, could take a decade round trip; a disputed succession, a famine, a heresy would be resolved locally years before instructions arrived. So the Concord governed by standing instruction — thick books of doctrine meant to be interpreted on the spot. Interpretation is drift with a legal seal. Two worlds a century apart in courier-time did not hold the same law, the same accent, or the same memory of Earth, whatever the registry said.

The dying was undramatic and took two hundred years, and it looked like this:

- Courier circuits consolidated "temporarily" as budgets thinned — loops of months stretching to loops of years.
- Worlds answering less often; then answering with apologies; then not at all. No secession, no defiance — just silence where the census returns used to be.
- The Assembly's quorum kept alive by procedural taxidermy: delegates seated by proxy, then proxies of proxies, then delegates who had died in transit seated posthumously by writ because the rules had no provision for a chamber that could not refill itself.
- And the strangest artifact of all: the tithes. Some worlds kept paying Concord dues for generations after the Assembly stopped sitting, the payments riding surviving trade loops to Mars, where clerks booked them to a dead treasury account because no procedure existed for refusing money sent to a government that no longer met. The last Concord tax payment arrived roughly a hundred and forty years after the Last Quorum, from a world that had not heard. Archivists call the account the **Ledger of the Faithful**.

The Imperium teaches that the Concord was murdered by faithlessness. The Union teaches that it collapsed under its own administrative weight. The record supports something quieter and less useful to either: the Concord was not overthrown. It lapsed.

## 3. The Death of Earth (c. 1,150 YBP)

Something killed Earth around eleven and a half centuries ago. That sentence, too, is nearly the whole of the agreed record. Even the date is conventional: Year 0 A.M. was fixed by Imperial liturgists working backward from mourning-rites, and scholars on both sides privately concede that nobody is certain of the year. The epoch of the most widely used calendar in human space is a guess with a crown on it.

### The three accounts

**Imperial orthodoxy: the Martyrdom.** Earth was killed — in most tellings by weapons, in some by abandonment — and the killing and the Sundering are one sin with two symptoms: faithlessness toward the ancestors. The homeworld was forsaken by its children, and died of it. "Earth died of being forsaken" is catechism, recited at every threshold crossing into Sol. The orthodoxy is deliberately vague about the murderer, which makes every generation free to recognize the murderer in its own enemies. The Apostates fit the robes nicely.

**Union schoolbooks: the Collapse.** Earth died of mismanagement — ecological exhaustion, systemic failure, a governing class that answered decline with ceremony. No villain, only a model: the Concord's model, hereditary and centralized and unaccountable, demonstrated to destruction on its own capital. Earth is the first exhibit in every Union civics course, the proof that the old way kills what it rules. The lesson is sincere. It is also convenient.

**The fringe account: the Long Death.** A minority of scholars on both sides — tolerated, unfunded — hold that the records genuinely do not survive; that the die-off may have taken a century; that "the Death of Earth" may be a name for a process, not an event, with no single cause at all. This account has the distinction of being the best supported and the least believed. Both civilizations rejected it for the same reason: an answer that unsatisfying is worse than no answer. *The setting never resolves which account is true. Neither does anyone in it.*

### The veil

Earth today is a shrouded world — wrapped in a permanent high pale overcast that no orbital instrument has ever usefully pierced, whether by atmospheric chemistry or sheer Imperial refusal to look properly. The Imperium forbids landings as desecration and enforces the ban with the pilgrimage patrol; the Union has never been in a position to try. No verified account of the surface exists from the last thousand years. Pilgrims disagree about its color.

### The pilgrimage

The holiest pilgrimage in human space is a silent orbit of the dead homeworld: one full circuit, engines cold, transmitters dark. The custom is older than both empires — it began during the Long Silence, when crews passing through Sol started cutting their drives for a single orbit, nobody now knows why or who was first — and this is precisely why it cannot be owned. The Imperium administers the pilgrimage; it has never once closed it to Apostates. Union pilgrims come every year on neutral hulls through client intermediaries, and Mars lets them, because every pilgrim is an argument for the Imperial story. The silence over Earth is the one liturgy both sides still share.

### What the dead world is for

The Imperium built its capital beside the grave and its legitimacy on tending it. The Union built its entire civilization to face away from the grave — and cannot stop citing it. One power needs Earth martyred; the other needs Earth negligent; both need it dead in their particular way, which is why the fringe scholars are so resented. They threaten to make the death *meaningless*, and meaninglessness is the one thing neither empire can absorb.

## 4. The Long Silence and the Sundering

### The Long Silence (c. 1,150–950 YBP)

After the Death, the drift accelerated. The Assembly on Mars governed a shrinking circle of worlds that still answered, with revenues from the Ledger of the Faithful and authority from habit. Two centuries of contraction, dignified and unbroken by any single catastrophe — the era historians on both sides call the **Long Silence**, because the sources themselves go quiet: fewer returns, fewer minutes, fewer names.

### The Last Quorum (c. 950 YBP)

The Concord of Man ended on an ordinary administrative morning in the Hall of Concord at Elysium. The session was called; the roll was taken; ninety-one delegates answered it. The rules required one hundred. The Speakership had been vacant for years — the last Speaker had died untitled, awaiting a confirmation vote the chamber could no longer legally hold — so the adjournment fell to a deputy clerk of session named **Auria Ferro**, who declared the Assembly adjourned *pending quorum* and closed the minutes in her own hand.

Quorum never came. There was no dissolution, no decree, no final address. The Concord's constitutional position, to this day, is that it stands adjourned.

Ferro's signature closing the minutes is the most reproduced handwriting in human history. The Imperium keeps the original minute-book in the Charterhouse as a relic; the Union keeps a facsimile in the Registry Annex as a warning. What Auria Ferro did afterward, no archive on either side records — a silence so complete that a certain kind of scholar finds it louder than the signature.

Both successor powers have retroactively filled that bureaucratic whimper with meaning it visibly cannot hold. Imperial art paints the ninety-one as mourners at a bier, lit like saints. Union historiography treats the adjournment as the moment humanity was freed to try something better. The minutes themselves record that the chamber's climate plant was under repair and the session was moved up an hour to beat the heat. Civilizations do not fall the way their heirs need them to have fallen.

### The name

The dissolution is called **the Sundering** — by everyone. The name is Imperial in temperament: sacral, wounded, implying a whole that was torn and must be rejoined. It is the Imperium's founding grievance and the wound the throne exists to heal.

The Union means something opposite by it — a natural death, even a liberation; the old model perishing of its own weight so that consent could replace inheritance — and Union historians have tried for six centuries to rename the event into something suitably clinical. *The Dissolution. The Discontinuity. The Administrative Lapse.* None has ever stuck, in the literature or the street, and every Union scholar who writes "the Sundering" — which is all of them — feels the small humiliation of conducting secular history in the enemy's theology. The failed renames survive only as a joke told by the people who proposed them.

Both empires say *the Sundering*. Only one of them can say it without wincing.

## 5. The Two Successors

### The Holy Terran Imperium (c. 890 YBP)

Sixty years after the Last Quorum, the magnates of the Sol sphere — governors-become-dynasts, charter companies fossilizing into bloodlines, the embryos of the great houses — assembled at Elysium and proclaimed the **Holy Terran Imperium**. In the event remembered as the **Acclamation of Mars**, they crowned the first emperor, **Kostantin I of House Palaiogos**, on the steps of the Hall of Concord at dawn, with dead Earth standing as a pale morning star above the chamber where the old world had adjourned. The staging was the argument: the Imperium does not claim to have *succeeded* the Concord. It claims the Concord never ended — that the Assembly stands adjourned, and the throne holds the chamber in trust until the ancestors' house is made whole.

The throne has been elective from that first morning, and has passed among the houses ever since — though House Palaiogos has won it back more often than statistics can politely excuse. The Imperium's constitutional theory of itself, its ancestor cult, its house genealogies-as-scripture, all descend from the Acclamation's central move: turning a lapsed bureaucracy into an inheritance.

### The Union of Free Systems (c. 610 YBP)

Two hundred and eighty years later and half of human space away, the far worlds — the ones the courier loops had abandoned first, that had governed themselves for centuries because no one else was going to — did something with no precedent in the old registry. Thirty-one worlds ratified the **Charter of the Union of Free Systems** at New Geneva: Charter Year 0. The founding generation, remembered in Union civics as **the Thirty-One**, had drawn its lesson from the drift directly. Worlds survive by service, not by descent; the Concord had been a promise nobody was obliged to keep, so the Union would be an obligation before it was anything else. Citizenship earned, never inherited, was in the founding text from the first draft.

The Charter reached Mars by trade courier roughly four years later. The Great Council's response, pronounced in A.M. 544, was the **Great Anathema**: the signatory worlds declared apostate — deserters not from a state but from their own ancestors — sealed by every house then seated. It remains in force. Several of the worlds anathematized could not, at the time, be located on the Council's own charts; the Imperium excommunicated places it could not find. Imperial official documents still refuse the name New Geneva, using the capital's old Survey designation instead: **Fenwick's Landing**, after a Concord surveyor about whom nothing else is known. The Union erased a man for an ideal; the Imperium wields the man against the ideal; the man himself is a name on a chart.

Neither founding involved the other. The Imperium was proclaimed to an audience of Sol-sphere houses; the Charter was ratified by worlds that knew the Imperium mostly as travelers' rumor. The two empires are siblings raised in separate houses, each certain it is the elder.

## 6. The Cold Centuries (c. 610–52 YBP)

### The Finding of the Lost Flock

For generations after the Anathema, the two spheres shared a language, a calendar, and no border. Contact was rumor riding trade loops. The first documented state-to-state encounter came around 380 YBP, when a Union long-range survey vessel met an Imperial tithe convoy at a red dwarf listed on Union charts as **GS-4471** and on Imperial charts as **Sant-Elmo's Star**. Union archives file the event, with perfect institutional composure, as **Reunification Contact Event 1**. Imperial chronicles call it **the Finding of the Lost Flock**. The meeting was cordial. They traded: the Imperial convoy-master bought Union charts; the Union survey-master bought what his manifest catalogues as "ornamental brasswork" and the Imperium mourns as a lost reliquary. They had been one people for most of recorded history. The first thing they did was correct each other's charts.

### Learning to hate by rehearsal

Two spheres that had never fought learned hatred the way garrisons learn drill: by rehearsal. The Cold Centuries were three and a half centuries of practice.

- **The client race.** Expansion brought the spheres toward each other through a belt of unaligned successor-worlds, and both powers discovered the same cheap arithmetic: it costs less to sponsor a border state than to garrison one. Worlds between the powers stopped being places and became prizes — the origin of both client stables and of the habit, older than the war, of counting territory in defections.
- **The Vay Intervention (c. 305–298 YBP).** The proving ground. When the **Commune of Vay**, a mid-border successor polity, fell into civil war, the Imperium armed one faction and the Union advised the other; both denied everything; the war ended in the **Vay Partition**, and within two generations each half had been digested into its sponsor's stable. Vay taught both powers that they could fight each other without fighting each other. It was the most dangerous lesson of the age, and both graduated with honors.
- **The Callas Reef Incident (117 YBP).** The first direct exchange of fire: a Union picket and an Imperial house picket, blundering into each other at a brown dwarf on the sensor frontier, seven dead across both hulls. Both governments apologized. Callas Reef is remembered chiefly as the last apology.
- **The Blind Years (c. 100 YBP onward).** As the detection frontiers met and hardened, blinding the other side's pickets and arrays decayed from provocation into routine — the constant low-grade sensor war that has never since stopped, truce or no truce. By the end of the Cold Centuries, both staff colleges had been killing each other on paper for generations; officers on each side studied the other's doctrine before they had ever seen the other's ships. When the shooting finally started, nobody had to improvise the hatred. The war arrived pre-rehearsed.

## 7. The Serevan Incident (52 YBP)

### The cluster

The **Serevan Cluster** is a tight knot of resource-rich systems at the coreward lip of the Reach — survey-grade habitables, heavy-metal belts, and position, which mattered more than either. It is named for **Adan Serevan**, the Concord surveyor who charted it and, per the registry, died in it. Both powers use his name for the cluster; neither knows anything else about him. Union charts label the anchor system **Serevan Anchorage**. House Normand's charts had, for some time, been calling it **Neustrie**.

### The writ

House Normand's claim rested on **Writ of Settlement 7-441** — "the Serevan Writ" — issued by the Ecumenical Survey some fourteen centuries ago, before the Death of Earth, granting settlement rights over the Serevan volume to the **Normande Charter Company**, the Concord-era concern from which House Normand traces its descent. The original writ, held in the Charterhouse, is authentic and bears no annulment. The duplicate registry in the Registry Annex at New Geneva is also authentic — and records Writ 7-441 annulled for non-performance two centuries before Earth died. Both parchments are genuine. At least one is lying, and fourteen hundred years of dead clerks are not available for questioning.

It should be recorded, because it is the most House Normand fact in the affair, that the house had been issuing titles to the Serevan Cluster for roughly a century before any Normand ship went there. The office of **Margrave of the Serevan March** was created, granted, and twice inherited over territory its holders had never seen.

### The incident (A.M. 1098 / Charter Year 558)

A Union survey flotilla had been working the Anchorage for five months: the survey vessels **UFS *Plumb Line***, **UFS *Datum***, and **UFS *Groundwork***, escorted by the frigate **UFS *Perimeter***, under **Survey Director Rohan Adeyemi-Marsh**. Routine work — the Reach was filling with exactly such flotillas from both sides, and everyone's standing orders assumed the pattern of the Blind Years would hold: harassment, protest, withdrawal, repeat.

Then a Normand squadron crossed the threshold: the Guild-forged battleship ***Right of Conquest*** — her keel blessed in A.M. 500, which makes her older than the Union she came to evict — with the cruisers ***Hastin*** and ***Falayse*** and their tenders, under **Roul fitz-Osbern, Margrave of the Serevan March**, come at last to see his title.

The physics of the thing shaped the tragedy. Emerging at the system's edge, the squadron burned inward for six days in full view — six days of ultimatums and replies crawling across hours of light-lag, each message answering a situation that no longer existed. Roul demanded the flotilla strike its surveys and submit to inspection as trespassers under Writ 7-441. Adeyemi-Marsh's reply entered Union legend: *"The Union does not recognize claims by inheritance."* The exchanges hardened for five days. On the sixth, Roul transmitted a final ultimatum: depart the system within twenty-five hours.

Then the clocks did their work. Normand's log started the deadline at transmission, as Imperial usage assumes. Union regulation counts an ultimatum from receipt — and at the range of transmission, light took forty-one minutes to cross. Within that gap, everyone behaved impeccably. Adeyemi-Marsh, her survey data already secured, elected to withdraw under protest and transmitted formal notice; the flotilla lit its drives and ran for the shoal. Aboard *Right of Conquest*, the deadline had passed, the withdrawal notice was still in transit, and four ships accelerating outward read as poachers in flight with the evidence. She opened fire.

*Perimeter* turned back and died covering the run — no survey ship reached the shoal. Three hundred and eleven surveyors, technicians, and naval ratings were killed. Twenty-six survivors were pulled from *Plumb Line*'s severed bow section by Normand rescue crews, held with formal correctness, and returned eight months later through Skarn intermediaries. Their debriefs contradict one another on the only detail that ever mattered: the timing.

By *Right of Conquest*'s log, the deadline had expired half an hour before she fired. By *Perimeter*'s log, eleven minutes remained. Both logs are honest. The distance between them is forty-one light-minutes. The Reach War began over the reading of a clock.

### The year of escalation

The Union demanded Roul fitz-Osbern's surrender for trial under the Ceres Conventions. The Great Council could not comply without ruling that Concord writs — the legal substance of every house's legitimacy, and of the throne's — were dead paper; it endorsed the Serevan claim instead. The Union could not accept the endorsement without ruling that the service of three hundred and eleven of its people bought nothing; five months later, FLEETCOM executed **Operation RECEIPT**, burning House Normand's victualling anchorage at **Bregil** and killing some two thousand sworn men — among them sons of two other houses, which converted a Normand quarrel into an Imperial one overnight. The Council proclaimed the defense of the ancestral charters; the first legions landed on Union-surveyed worlds in the Reach within the year.

Each side names the other the aggressor, and each is precise about it. Imperial histories date the war from Bregil — the Apostate reprisal against a lawful enforcement. Union histories date it from the first salvo at Serevan Anchorage. Fifty-two years on, the war's birthday is still disputed; both sides solemnly observe their own. Roul fitz-Osbern lived another nine years and died at war, which spared everyone a decision.

Neither power planned the war. Both had spent three centuries rehearsing it, and rehearsal is a plan you make without admitting it.

## Open questions

- What is under Earth's veil — and has any human being stood on the surface in eleven centuries? (Persistent rumor holds that someone has, twice. The rumor never names who, which is itself interesting.)
- Auria Ferro adjourned the government of united humanity and then vanishes from every archive on both sides. Where did she go — and is the silence around her accident, grief, or redaction?
- Which copy of Writ 7-441 is the forgery, who made it, and how long before Serevan was it waiting to be used?
- The Concord registry lists settled worlds no one has ever found. Clerical ghosts — or colonies still out there past both frontiers, keeping the Concord calendar and waiting for a courier that stopped coming eight centuries ago?
- The Ledger of the Faithful booked generations of tithes to a government that no longer met. Where is that account now, who has custody of its balance, and what could it buy?
- Who moved Roul fitz-Osbern to act on a century-dormant claim in that particular season — Normand ambition alone, or a hand from some other house that profited by the war it started?
- The twenty-six survivors of *Plumb Line* disagree, under oath, about the timing of the salvo. Coached? Traumatized? Or were they shown different things aboard the ships that rescued them?
- The Ecumenical Survey named the contested volume *the Farther Reach*. Farther from what? The Survey's own key to its master charts has never been found.

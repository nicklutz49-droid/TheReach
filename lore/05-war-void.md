# The War in the Reach: the Void

*Everyone sees the battle coming for days. That is the whole tragedy of it.*

---

## The Shape of the War Above

The void war is small, and that is the most important fact about it. A dozen capital ships is a major fleet. Capital losses are national tragedies in the Union and house-breaking catastrophes in the Imperium, and both admiralties know it in their bones, because thirty years ago they proved it to each other at ruinous expense. The daily war above the Reach is convoy escort, commerce raiding, and picket duels — a war of sentries and thieves. Blinding the enemy is constant low-grade warfare even during truces; fleet battles happen where someone punches through a blind spot, and both sides work very hard to make sure nobody does.

There is no faster-than-light communication. Every order in the void is either a standing order, a courier, or a guess. Fleets are commanded by intent written months in advance, executed by captains who will not be able to ask permission, judged by boards that convene long after everyone involved is dead or decorated. Both navies have built their entire cultures around this one fact — they have simply built opposite cathedrals on the same foundation.

Naval war is chess, not attrition. The board is sensor coverage; the pieces cannot be replaced; and both players have already had their one great exchange of queens.

*The void war is decades old and has had exactly one battle worth the name.*

---

## The Grammar of Battle

Every fleet action in the Reach follows the same grammar, because the physics permits no other. Warp drives fail deep inside gravity wells. The boundary where warp becomes possible is called **the shoal** on Union charts and **the threshold** on Imperial ones — the mirrored naming is old, and nobody remembers who named it first.

### Emergence

A fleet arrives at the edge of a system in a blaze of warp translation that every sensor platform in-system registers within hours. There is no surprise at the strategic level, ever. Surprise lives earlier in the chain — in which system the fleet emerges at, which the defender learns only when it happens, because the attacker's intentions traveled by courier and the defender's warnings do too. Once the emergence flare lights up the shoal, the clock starts, and everyone can read it.

### The Long Burn

From the shoal to anything worth fighting over is days of sublight deceleration — **the Long Burn**, three to eight days of it, under everyone's eyes. Union crews call this phase **the parade**; Imperial crews call it **the procession**, and mean it religiously. The defender watches the attacker's drive plumes and counts hulls. The attacker watches the defender choose: fight, run for the shoal on the far side, or scatter. Whole campaigns are decided in this interval without a shot — a defender who does the arithmetic and leaves has lost a system and saved a fleet, and both admiralties have medals that are quietly understood to be for leaving.

The Long Burn is also when men break. There is no comparable experience in the ground war: four days of watching your death decelerate toward you at a known rate, with nothing to do but drills and mass.

### The Sensor Duel

Before any weapon fires, the fleets fight over *truth*. Drones, picket sloops, and strike craft push out to strip away the enemy's decoys and count his real gun line; escorts burn active sensors and jammers at each other; both sides seed the approach with false contacts. FLEETCOM is acknowledged — even in Imperial staff colleges, through gritted teeth — to be better at this. The Union doctrine is that the fleet which sees first decides first, and the fleet which decides first can decline. The Imperial counter-doctrine is that it does not matter how well the Apostate sees if the line is coming regardless.

### The Opening: Strike Wings

Carriers open at range, and their strikes are counted like coins, because they are finite: a carrier sails with a fixed magazine of torpedoes and expendable airframes, and when they are spent they are spent for the rest of the campaign. Strikes almost never kill capital ships — shields ablate under sustained fire and recharge between blows, and a capital ship's shield envelope can drink a full torpedo wave and be whole again in an hour. What strikes do is *undress* the enemy: kill escorts, wear down screens so the gun line arrives with shields already ragged, and above all blind sensors, burning out the arrays and pickets the enemy's gunnery will need. A perfectly fought strike phase kills nothing important and decides everything.

### The Gun Lines Close

Then the heavy ships come on behind their screens — the escorts and screening vessels whose job is to die in place of something irreplaceable. This is the slowest, most deliberate phase: hours of closing, long-range fire ablating shields that recharge almost as fast, each admiral reading the other's formation for the commitment he can refuse. Either fleet can still break off. Battle happens only when both commanders conclude the exchange favors them, or when one of them is wrong about being able to leave.

### The Press

Decisive kills require heavy naval guns inside shield-recharge range. The knife-fight range where shields collapse faster than they can cycle is called **the press**, and it is the most violent interval in human warfare — minutes, almost never more than ten, in which gun lines hang beside each other at ranges their designers considered obscene and simply *unmake* one another. Shields go bare in the first exchanges. Then it is armor, and then it is not. More human beings die per minute in a press than in any other human activity, war included. Both navies structure their entire doctrine, their training, their theology, around the question of how to enter the press on favorable terms — or how to make the enemy enter it on yours.

### Boarding

When a ship's shields collapse and her engines are lamed, boarding becomes possible, and boarding is an Imperial art. Assault shuttles and **drakkes** — the boarding craft of House Jorvik's tradition, the finest in the Imperium — cross the last kilometers under the guns, and Paladins go in through the breaches. The Union regards boarding as a barbarism that wastes elite troops on ships artillery would kill more cheaply. The Imperium regards a captured hull as worth fifty destroyed ones, and a boarding action as the only part of void war where honor is paid in person. Both are correct.

### Retreat

Escape means running for the shoal under fire — days of stern-chase, the crippled and the slow falling out of formation one by one, the pursuer choosing which ships to overhaul and which to let go. A retreat is not a rout; it is a long, cold auction in which the fleeing admiral decides what he can afford to sell. The rearguard knows what it is. Rearguard duty in a shoal-run is the void war's Halvard's — the assignment from which arithmetic says you do not return.

### Why Battle Is Rare

A Guild yard takes a generation to forge a capital keel. A Union yard takes six years and a legislative war to build a battleship, and every hull and every crew is a political event. Neither fleet has returned to its strength of thirty years ago. Both admiralties therefore play the same game: preserve the fleet, poison the enemy's sensor picture, win the convoy war, and decline every battle that is not a gift. The fleets are not shy. They are *priceless*, and both sides did the pricing at the same auction.

*Two fleets in being, each deterring the other from existing loudly.*

## Two Navies

### The Imperial Fleets: Guild Iron and House Colors

There is no single Imperial navy. There are house fleets — squadrons of capital ships held by the great houses the way their ancestors misremember holding castles — and above them the emperor's own **Sacred Armada**, the largest single fleet in human space, crewed by sworn men of every house: a ruinously expensive symbol of imperial unity, and the only formation in the Imperium where precedence is settled.

Only Guild yards can forge capital keels and shield cores. The Guilds do not build *classes*; they forge to **Patterns** — sanctified design lineages, some older than the war, some older than several wars. Houses may build their own escort hulls in their own yards, but every shield core in every one of them is Guild-forged and Guild-anointed, which is another leash, and everyone feels it.

An Imperial capital ship has a **genealogy**: a recorded lineage of keel-laying, consecrations, actions, deaths, and refits, kept by the ship's own hereditary lore-keepers and read aloud on feast days. Some hulls are centuries old — rebuilt so many times that the genealogy is most of what remains original — and serving aboard one is closer to holding a benefice than holding a job. The oldest hulls are addressed with honorifics. Officers have been broken for referring to a Kataphraktos by pronoun.

**Named hulls of the Imperium.** The *Autokrator Nikephor*, Kataphraktos Pattern, four hundred and seventy years old, flagship of the Sacred Armada, survivor of the Emperor's Wager — her battle scars have never been fully repaired, by decree, and sworn men touch the great weld on her spine when they board. The *Karl Vindicant*, flagship of House Karolus, worked into every war-hawk broadsheet as the shape of imperial resolve. The *Hrafnking*, House Jorvik's boarding-carrier, whose drakke crews hold the most captured hulls in the war and paint nothing on her flanks — the absence of trophies being the boast.

### FLEETCOM: Hulls, Sensors, Votes

Union warships are newer, lighter, faster, and see better. They are also fewer than the Union would like, because every one of them is bought from an Assembly that counts crews as constituents. FLEETCOM's design philosophy is written in its post-Wager classes: a Union warship is built to fight the engagement it chooses and to *survive leaving* the one it doesn't.

**Named hulls of the Union.** The *UFS Quorum*, Mandate-class, oldest battleship in FLEETCOM, survivor of Cerulyn Deep, where her crew fought Jorvik boarders bulkhead to bulkhead and kept her — the name was a jab at the Concord's dying assembly when she was launched, and the Imperium has never once printed it. The *UFS Long Watch*, Vigil-class, lost at Cerulyn launching strikes until her flight deck lost pressure; every Union carrier's ready room keeps a chair empty for her. The *UFS Honest Measure*, of whom more below, in the ledger of dirty tricks.

### The Strike Cultures: Errants and Fetches

The two navies put opposite things in their strike craft, and it tells you everything.

Union strike wings are mixed: a crewed **SB-14 Osprey** strike bomber conducting a hand of four to six **Fetch** drones — semi-autonomous airframes, narrow-minded and obedient, that extend the pilot's eyes and soak the defensive fire. Pilots "play the hand": the Fetches probe, jam, and die so the Osprey's torpedoes arrive with honest targeting. A Union strike pilot is a systems conductor whose instrument occasionally includes herself. FLEETCOM loses fewer pilots per sortie than any air arm in history and agonizes about every one, while the standing temptation — wings with no pilot at all — is discussed in New Geneva in rooms with the recorders off.

Imperial strike craft are called **lances**, and they are crewed by **errants**: minor nobility, third sons and unlanded daughters, flying individually named and heraldically painted craft in a culture that is best understood as knight-errantry with fusion torches. An errant's lance is his arms, his honor, and frequently his entire inheritance. Errants duel Union drones and are insulted by them; the formal Imperial position is that sending a machine to do battle is a species of cowardice, and the informal Imperial position, held by every errant who has watched Fetches soak the fire that should have killed an Osprey, is the same but louder. The errantry dies young, gorgeously, and in numbers the houses pretend are sustainable.

*One navy sends its future leaders into the strike wave. The other sends its budget. Each finds the other obscene.*

## Doctrine and Culture

### FLEETCOM: the Ledger

Union naval doctrine is networked, sensor-first, and coldly transactional: every possible engagement is an exchange to be priced, and the standing rule since the Wager — attributed to Fleet Admiral Odile Nakamura-Reyes, who earned the right to coin it — is **"No even trades."** FLEETCOM fights when the ledger favors it and leaves when it doesn't, and it has built the sensor infrastructure to know the difference: the deep arrays, the P-boat lines, the courier schedules that move the *picture* of the Reach around faster than fleets can move through it. A FLEETCOM admiral's real weapon is a truer map. Because no order can outrun a ship, Union captains sail under **mission orders** — pages of intent, contingencies, and pre-delegated authority that read like contracts, because culturally, they are.

### The Imperium: the Line as Liturgy

The Imperial line of battle is a religious procession that happens to be armed. Station in the line is an honor, recorded in genealogies, inherited, litigated, and occasionally the cause of the battle going worse than the enemy could have managed alone. The order of battle is literally an order of *precedence* — drawing one up for a joint operation takes months of heraldic diplomacy, and two full expeditions in the war's second decade died in committee over which house held the honor of the van. The line advances together, fights together, and holds station while dying, because the line is the Imperium made visible: every house in its place, moving as one body toward the enemy. Union analysts call the doctrine wasteful, inflexible, and — in the classified appendices — *frightening*, because a fleet that believes holding station is a sacrament does not break, and the press rewards exactly one virtue above all others: not breaking.

### The Sacred Armada

The emperor's own fleet, crewed by sworn men of every house serving side by side — the only place in the Imperium where a Rurikov takes orders from a Yamatai and a batchman's oath is heard in the same chapel as a duke's son's. It is ruinously expensive, strategically irreplaceable, and above all *symbolic*: the Armada is the argument, rendered in Guild iron, that the Imperium is one thing. Its commander bears the ancient garbled title of **Megadux**. Its hulls are the oldest and holiest in service. And thirty years ago, an emperor bet all of it.

### House Fleets in Joint Operations

House squadrons fight the way house armies do: unevenly, jealously, and according to signal books that differ by house. Jorvik squadrons close to board when doctrine says stand off. Ming squadrons are magnificently maintained and mysteriously never in the van. Palaiogos hulls are ancient, sacred, and slow. A house admiral may keep station perfectly and still let a rival's squadron take the worst of the press, and prove afterward — genealogy in hand — that his station was exactly where honor required. FLEETCOM catalogues house squadrons like a scouting report and plans engagements around which houses are present. The Imperium's admirals know this. It changes nothing. The precedence is the point.

*The Union fights as one fleet pretending to be many. The Imperium fights as many fleets pretending, on feast days, to be one.*

## The Battle of Cerulyn Deep / the Emperor's Wager (30 YBP)

The war's one great fleet battle, fought in year 22, carries two names — **the Battle of Cerulyn Deep** in the Union, after the survey designation of the system's cold outer gulf, and **the Emperor's Wager** in the Imperium, a name coined by Alexios IV's enemies as a sneer and kept by his priests as a title. It is studied in both admiralties the way theologians study a shared scripture that proves opposite things.

### The Stakes

By year 18 of the war, FLEETCOM had begun assembling **Forward Anchorage 9** in the Cerulyn system: a repair yard and magazine complex just behind the sensor frontier. The threat was arithmetic, not drama. With a forward yard, Union squadrons could hold station months longer between refits; the convoy war in the middle Reach would tilt, window by window, until Imperial armies on half a dozen worlds began to wither. Every Imperial admiral read the same numbers. None would place his house's fleet under another house's banner to do anything about it, and two joint expeditions strangled in precedence committees while the anchorage grew.

So Alexios IV — then a compromise emperor of two decades' unremarkable standing — staked the one fleet that outranks all banners. He committed the Sacred Armada, and by committing it made abstention unbearable: a house that withheld its squadron while the ancestors' own fleet sailed would carry the shame for a century. Nine of the twelve great houses sent contingents. House Habsberg's squadron arrived, with exquisite timing, four days after the battle.

*The Wager was never against the Union. It was against his own houses — and he won it before a shot was fired.*

### The Battle

**Emergence.** The Armada and its house contingents — twenty-seven capital ships and better than ninety screens, the largest force ever assembled at one shoal — lit the Cerulyn threshold like a second dawn. Fleet Admiral **Odile Nakamura-Reyes**, commanding twenty-one capitals, had no orders that covered this and no way to get any: New Geneva was forty days away by courier. Her standing orders had been written for a smaller war. She convened her captains once, by tight-beam, and chose to fight for the exchange, not the ground.

**The Long Burn.** Six days. The Imperial procession came on in perfect station. The Union parade formed, dissolved, reformed — Nakamura-Reyes shuffling her real dispositions inside a shell of decoys while her strike wings went to work.

**The strike phase.** Three days of the largest strike battles in history. Osprey hands and their Fetches against the massed errantry of nine houses — the errants' great day, and their graveyard. The strikes did what strikes do: killed no capital ship outright, gutted the Imperial screen, wore the Armada's shields ragged, and burned out sensor pickets on both sides until the fleets were closing half-blind. Perhaps half the errantry of the Imperium died in those three days. The houses still call it **the Withering of the Flower**, and the lance-carriers have never carried full wings since.

**The press.** Nakamura-Reyes intended a passing engagement — one press, break contact, leave with a favorable ledger. Her fleet maneuvered as a network, and the network failed her at the worst possible moment: across the hundreds of thousands of kilometers of her formation, light-lag put her Second Squadron eleven seconds behind a turn the rest of the fleet had already made. Eleven seconds, at those velocities, is a door. The Imperial line — which does not maneuver as a network, which simply *keeps station* — lapped around Second Squadron and held it in the press.

The press at Cerulyn Deep lasted fifty-three minutes. No press before or since has lasted a quarter of that. Both gun lines hung inside shield-recharge range and unmade each other: shields bare in the first ten minutes, then armor, then structure, then crews. Jorvik drakkes took two Union battleships by boarding; a third, *UFS Quorum*, fought her boarders bulkhead to bulkhead and kept herself. The carrier *UFS Long Watch* launched strikes into the melee until her flight deck lost pressure, and after. **Megadux Isidor Kantakuzen** died on the flag bridge of the *Autokrator Nikephor* in the forty-first minute; his last recorded order was *"Keep station,"* and the Armada kept station.

**The breaking.** Nakamura-Reyes spent her carriers' last strikes as a screen and ran for the shoal with everything that could still burn. The Armada, too crippled to pursue, turned instead on Forward Anchorage 9 and burned it to slag. The Imperial fleet's own crawl back to the threshold took twelve days, under harassment the whole way, towing its dying.

### The Ledger

| | Imperium | Union |
|---|---|---|
| Capital ships engaged | 27 | 21 |
| Capitals destroyed | 9 (5 of the Sacred Armada) | 8 (5 battleships, 3 carriers) |
| Capitals crippled | 11 | 9 |
| Screens and escorts lost | 41 | 38 |
| Dead | ~61,000 | ~47,000 |

A hundred and eight thousand dead in nine days; roughly seventy thousand of them in the fifty-three minutes of the press. More human beings died in that hour than the Union navy had lost in the entire war before it.

### What Both Sides Learned

Both sides claimed victory, and both claims survive audit. The Imperium burned the anchorage, held the field, and watched the Apostate fleet run for the shoal. The Union destroyed more tonnage, extracted its fleet against odds, and noted that the anchorage — steel and stores — was replaceable in principle, while the hulls were not. Tactically the battle is a Rorschach test. Strategically it taught one lesson, and taught it to both admiralties at once: *capital fleets cannot be replaced on war-relevant timescales.* Thirty years on, neither battle fleet has returned to its year-22 strength — three Guild keels and seven Union hulls in three decades, against a morning's losses. Naval war has been chess ever since.

The people fared according to their navies. Nakamura-Reyes was relieved, inquired upon, exonerated, and eventually canonized in the Union manner — the officer who could have lost the war in an afternoon and declined to. Her written defense to the board was one sentence: *"I declined to spend what the Union cannot mint."* Kantakuzen entered the genealogies as an ancestor of the void; the Sacred Armada's watch changes to this day with his order — *keep station* — spoken as a blessing. And Alexios IV, the compromise emperor nobody feared, became the emperor who had wagered the ancestors' own fleet and not lost it. His enemies have been explaining the difference between "not lost" and "won" for thirty years.

*The Union kept its fleet by leaving. The Imperium kept its honor by staying. Neither has been able to afford either since.*

## The Convoy War

This is where fleets actually fight, year in and year out. Both sides depend on **orbital resupply windows**; the convoy war above — escorts, raiders, Ederi Khagan fleets against UFS shipping, UGF hunter squadrons against house tenders — is the void war's daily business, and its arithmetic decides campaigns that never see a gun line.

### The Arithmetic of Windows

Over a defended world, massed ground-based air defense makes low orbit lethal; ships in low orbit over a defended world are targets. Resupply therefore happens in **windows** — brief intervals when escorts have swept a corridor, local AA has been suppressed or spoofed along one approach, and the tenders can run the gauntlet down and back. The arithmetic is taught to every escort captain in both navies: an army group on a contested world consumes roughly one heavy tender's cargo every three days; a convoy sails when it can fill thirty-odd hulls; windows come six to nine weeks apart on a well-run front. Miss one window and the guns ration shells. Miss two and the hospitals start choosing. Miss three and the army begins to wither. A ground army that loses its window doesn't get defeated — it *withers*. Some of the Reach's worst tragedies are armies that won every battle and ran out of everything.

### Raiders and Escorts

The Imperium's deniable raiding arm is the Khaganate of the Ederi Drift, whose **arrow-hosts** — fast, light, shieldless by choice, ferociously handled — hunt Union shipping along the frontier while the Imperial court explains, regretfully, that it cannot control the Khagans. The Union's answer is the hunter squadrons: FLEETCOM hulls that hunt house tithe-tenders under UGF operational tasking, because interdicting ground resupply is, by a bureaucratic ruling nobody has managed to reverse in forty years, a ground-forces mission. Both services resent the arrangement with a passion otherwise reserved for the enemy. The hunter squadrons do not care; they are the war's most decorated formations and its least sentimental, and their gun camera archives are not shown to the Assembly.

### Q-Ships and Ruses

The convoy war is fought as much with lies as with guns. The Union operates Q-ships — armed hulls dressed as merchantmen — of which the most famous is the *UFS Honest Measure*, a grain hauler flying falsified Meridian Compact colors that answered a Khagan boarding hail with a battleship's secondary battery and killed three raiders before anyone understood the joke. The Compact, whose colors she wore, files a formal complaint quarterly. The Imperium's ruses run the other direction: in the incident the Union files call **the Pilgrim's Answer** (12 YBP), the corvette *UFS Tally* stopped what her charts listed as a pilgrim barque bound for Sol and was boarded and taken intact by the Jorvik drakke-nest hidden in its hold. FLEETCOM rules of engagement now permit firing on pilgrim vessels that fail to answer hails. The Imperium calls this desecration, and cites it at every prisoner exchange. Both sides cite the old Concord conventions. Both sides are describing the other.

### Famous Losses

**Convoy HW-91** (24 YBP) — thirty-four hulls and three escorts bound for Halvard's World, caught at emergence by an Ederi arrow-host and taken apart across eleven days of stern-chase. Fewer than a third of the tenders made the window. The army group below spent that season learning what withering means, and on Halvard's, in both armies' slang, **"ninety-one"** is still the word for anything promised and not delivered.

**The Burning of the Golden Train** (17 YBP) — House Ming's grand tithe-fleet, nineteen heavy tenders carrying a year's supply for two army groups, caught at a threshold by Hunter Squadron 9 with its escort still forming up. The escort was late because two houses had spent six weeks disputing whose squadron held the honor of command. The recriminations outlived the campaign; the campaign did not outlive the winter.

**The Pilgrim's Answer** — see above; famous less for the tonnage than for what it did to the rules. The void war's conventions are weak the way ice is weak. Everyone walks on them anyway.

*The gun lines are the cathedral of the void war. The convoy war is the parish where everyone actually lives and dies.*

## Life Aboard

### The Watch

Void war is years of routine bracketing minutes of annihilation. Union ships run three watches with civilian-grade rec compartments and rotation policies negotiated like labor contracts, because every crew is a constituency. Imperial ships run watch-and-watch in the old style, and one station is never empty: on every capital ship of the Imperium there is the **Vigil of Ancestors** — a crewman posted at the ship's genealogy shrine at all hours, in transit, in refit, in the press. There is a man aboard whose battle station is prayer. Imperial crews find this obvious. Union crews who learn of it find it disturbing in a way they have trouble explaining to the debriefers.

### What Shields Feel Like

From inside, a shield taking fire is weather. Impacts arrive as deep percussion through the frames — felt in the teeth before it is heard — and a shield core cycling hard puts a copper taste in the air and a wrongness in the inner ear that veterans stop noticing and new crew never quite do. Union crews watch the envelope as a percentage and call losing it **going bare**. Imperial crews call the shield **the veil**, and the cry *"the veil is torn"* appears in their hymns, which tells you what they think stands behind it. Both crews go quiet at the same telemetry.

### The Press, From Inside

No training honestly prepares anyone. The press is minutes of continuous concussion in which the ship — the only world there is — is being disassembled around you by something you will never see. Damage control in the press is fought in the dark, in vacuum, in burning compartments, by crews who know that the difference between a crippled ship and a dead one is whether they keep working. Both navies rotate press survivors out; both navies find that press survivors mostly refuse to go. The psychiatric literature of two civilizations agrees on almost nothing except this: nobody comes out of the press the same, and nobody can say what the difference is.

### The Deaths of Each Navy

Union ships die fast. The light hulls that make them quick make their deaths a cascade — containment, then atmosphere, then everything, often in seconds. A Union spacer's death is usually instant and usually total, and FLEETCOM's memorial walls carry ships' names because there is rarely anything else to recover. Imperial giants die slowly. A Kataphraktos can burn for hours, her crew fighting compartment by compartment, the Vigil still manned, the lore-keepers recording. Imperial doctrine holds that a great hull is never destroyed; she is **martyred**, and her recovered keel-fragments enter the reliquaries of her house. The Union finds this ghoulish. The Imperium finds it obvious: the ship was older than everyone aboard, and will be mourned longer.

### The Mud Below

Void crews on both sides regard the ground war with the particular horror of people whose own deaths are statistically worse but cleaner. A Union spacer's war is ninety-nine percent boredom and one percent the press; she looks down at Halvard's and cannot imagine choosing the mud. An Imperial voidsman, likely as not levy-born himself, watches the tithe-tenders offload a legion into a contested window and thanks his ancestors for the deck under his feet. The ground troops, for their part, look up at the clean quiet ships and hate them with a whole heart — right up until the window closes, and the sky that fed them goes empty, and the whole army learns to watch for drive plumes like farmers watching for rain.

*In the void you die all at once or not at all. On the ground you die by inches. Each side's soldiers pity the other, and neither would trade.*

## Open Questions

- Which houses were the three that sent no squadron to the Emperor's Wager — and what has thirty years of that stain done to them?
- The *Autokrator Nikephor* is four hundred and seventy years old and was crippled at the Wager. When the Guilds finally declare her unfit for the line, who has the authority to say so — and would the Sacred Armada obey?
- What actually happened aboard the two Union battleships the drakkes took at Cerulyn Deep — and where are those hulls, and their surviving crews, now?
- The errantry never recovered from the Withering of the Flower. What is quietly replacing minor nobility in the lances — and does the Imperium admit it?
- Nakamura-Reyes is still alive. What does FLEETCOM's living saint say, and to whom, about the hawks' plans to break the arithmetic she built her doctrine on?
- Somebody drew Hunter Squadron 9 the Golden Train's course and schedule. The Imperium executed three courier officers for it; the Union pension rolls suggest someone else. Who sold the Train?
- Both admiralties war-game a second Cerulyn constantly. What result do the war-games keep producing that neither admiralty will put in writing?
- The Ederi arrow-hosts fight shieldless by choice. Is that poverty, doctrine, or something the Khagans believe about the veil that nobody has thought to ask?

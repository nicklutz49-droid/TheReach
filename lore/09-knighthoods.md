# The Two Knighthoods

*One knighthood proves that virtue can be earned. The other proves it can still be born. The war has spent thirty years quietly blurring the difference.*

---

## 1. What Knighthood Is For

Every feudal order needs two reassurances running at once, and they contradict each other. It needs the commons to believe that the ladder is real — that extraordinary service is seen, rewarded, remembered, that a man born in a furrow or a vat can die an ancestor. And it needs the nobility to believe that blood still means something — that the sons and daughters who inherit nothing else at least inherit the right to bleed first. The Imperium solved this the way it solves everything: it built an institution for each belief and kept them scrupulously separate for nine centuries, so that neither claim would have to answer the other's evidence.

The **Paladins** are the first reassurance made flesh: power-armored shock troops elevated one at a time, by deed, regardless of birth — the single Imperial ladder that a commoner or a **Vatborn** can climb to the top rung. The **errantry** is the second: void-strike pilots drawn from the surplus of the noble houses, third sons and unlanded daughters who inherit a craft and a coat of arms in place of a title. One knighthood exists to prove the system is fair. The other exists to prove the system is still aristocratic. Both claims are true often enough to be believed, and false often enough that everyone involved has learned not to look too closely — which, as of this war, has become considerably harder to arrange.

|  | The Paladins | The Errantry |
|---|---|---|
| Domain | Ground — the breach, the boarding party | Void — the strike wave, the duel |
| Basis of entry | A witnessed deed, any birth | Cadet nobility, birth (increasingly patched) |
| Rite of entry | The **Investiture** — vigil, dawn, the Writing | The **Fledging** — arms granted, craft named, blessed |
| Typical scale | A great house fields Paladins by the dozen | A carrier's wing ran forty strong before the Wager |
| Governing culture | Breach discipline; the relic suit as inheritance | Heraldry, the duel, dying young and gorgeously |
| Touched by the Patchwork | Barely — the deed is still the deed | Down to the joints — the wound that will not close |

Militarily the two knighthoods answer opposite problems. The tempo doctrine's saturation offense needs a spearhead that can go through a breach standing up, clear a command node, and survive what kills the tide around it — that is a Paladin's whole reason for existing, and it is why they appear, doctrine officers on both sides note, exactly where the Unresting Assault has just made the enemy's network run hottest. The strike wave, by contrast, needs pilots willing to fly *individually* named craft into *individually* aimed fire, because the Imperium's entire theory of naval combat is that a duel fought by a machine isn't a duel — and an empire that cannot produce enough people willing to die that way has quietly conceded a point to the people who build the machines. Neither knighthood could do the other's job. Neither has ever tried.

Numbers make the asymmetry concrete. Even generously counted, the Imperium's entire Paladin roster does not reach a thousand serving at any one time — fewer, as is well known, than the UGF fields colonels. The errantry, for all its losses, still numbers in the low thousands across every house's wings combined, because it was never rare by design the way the Paladins are; it was only ever supposed to be brave. The war has spent thirty years teaching both institutions the same lesson from opposite directions: scarcity you engineer on purpose survives contact with a war considerably better than scarcity that finds you by surprise.

## 2. The Paladins

### The Witnessed Deed

Selection is by deed alone, and the deed must be *witnessed* — which sounds like the purest meritocracy in the Imperium until you ask who is standing close enough to see.

Every legion and every house fleet carries at least one Remembrancer attached for exactly this purpose: not a chaplain in the general sense, but an officer of record whose specific charge is to observe, corroborate, and attest. No deed reaches the Investiture without a Remembrancer's oath and at least one further corroborating witness of standing — an officer, a noble, another Paladin. The rule is meant to prevent fraud. What it has produced instead, on every front of the war, is a quiet market in *proximity*.

A Remembrancer of record can be in one place. Command elements keep Remembrancers near them; forward sectors, penal companies, and the unglamorous grind of holding a line rarely see one at all. Officers know this, and officers curate: a favored soldier gets rotated toward the sectors where witnessing is likely; a soldier with no patron performs an equally extraordinary act forty meters to the left of the Remembrancer's sightline and it simply does not happen, in the only sense that matters to scripture. Deeds get disputed, too — corroboration is sometimes thin, sometimes bought, sometimes withheld out of spite between rival officers who both wanted the same soldier for their own muster. The College maintains a standing court to adjudicate contested elevations, and its rulings are, by long institutional habit, unappealable and occasionally wrong in ways everyone privately agrees on and no one will say aloud.

This is the whole of it, stated plainly: the one truly birth-blind institution in the Holy Terran Imperium runs entirely on who happened to be looking.

### The Investiture

Elevation, when it comes, is the empire's most sacred ceremony, and the shape of it is designed to be unbearable to fake.

The candidate keeps a night's vigil beside the open genealogy of the house he will serve — the whole book, however many generations it runs, laid open at the front bench of the house's shrine, a Remembrancer seated opposite through the dark. Custom holds that the candidate reads the names aloud as the night allows: for a young house this is finished by midnight with hours of silence left over; for a house like Palaiogos or Karolus, with nine centuries of dead to get through, the vigil is a genuine trial of endurance layered on top of the deed that earned it, and more than one candidate has fainted somewhere around the fourth century and had to be revived to finish. At dawn, the Remembrancer takes up the house's own ink — every great house keeps one jar, refilled but by tradition never emptied, so that the ink which writes tonight's name is understood, in the specific mystical accounting the Imperium prefers, to be continuous with the ink that wrote the first one — and writes the candidate's name into the book. Not beside the genealogy. Into it.

When the candidate is Vatborn, the rite does not change, and this is the detail that makes Imperial theologians leave the room. A batchman has no line for the entry to extend; the Remembrancer writes it anyway, and the convention that has grown up — unlegislated, universal, never once addressed in any College ruling — is to preface such an entry with a small mark in the margin, a closed circle no wider than a fingernail, that Remembrancers call, off the record, the **Founder's Mark**. It means, without anyone having to write the sentence: *the line begins here.* Every genealogy in the Imperium that has ever elevated a Vatborn carries at least one. The College has sealed its rulings on whether a batchman has ancestors for two hundred years. Its own scribes have been quietly answering the question in ink the entire time.

### Armor as Relic

The armor is not issued. It is inherited, and a Paladin is fitted to it, never the other way round — Guild wrights spend days, sometimes weeks, coaxing a stubborn suit into accepting a new bearer, and no one in either the Guilds or the College has ever offered a mechanical explanation for why some suits take a candidate at first fitting and others fight him for a month. The suits are older than their wearers by definition and carry the names of everyone who has worn them, inscribed inside the collar where only the next bearer reads them — a genealogy within the genealogy, and the more storied suits have borne more names than most houses' entire recorded lineage.

Rejection is rare, but it is not unheard of, and when it happens the result satisfies no one. The man's name is already scripture by the time armor-fitting begins — genealogical fraud cuts both directions, and unwriting an entry is exactly the heresy that forging one is — so a candidate whose suit will not close around him remains, permanently, a named man with no armor to wear. Houses phrase this gently in official correspondence. Everyone else calls it what it is: outliving your own miracle.

Three of the oldest suits are worth naming, because between them they say most of what needs saying about the institution.

***Kurotate*** — House Yamatai's oldest war-plate, four centuries in service, forty-one names in its collar and counting. It has never once refused a properly nominated candidate, which Yamatai reads not as generosity but as evidence of how ferociously the house's own selection filters out anyone unworthy before the suit ever sees them. Its bonded blade, carried into every action for two hundred years, is called ***the Long Question*** — nobody currently serving remembers what the question was, only that the name predates every living Yamatai's memory and the house has stopped asking.

***Alevdar*** — Osmanli's, the "flame-bearer," fitted more often to Vatborn than to any noble candidate in its recorded history, a fact the house states plainly and the rest of the Imperium notes and does not repeat. Its current bearer is discussed below.

***Porphyrogene*** — Palaiogos's grandest suit, "born to the purple," and the one on this list least worth being fitted to, because Porphyrogene has stood empty in Porphyra's shrine-hall for eleven years. The house that holds the throne is also the house least willing to spend its holiest relics on a war it did not start and is trying to outlast. An empire's finest armor, standing idle because the man who might have earned it would have to earn it in a fight the house would rather someone else fought — this is Palaiogos in one sentence, and the house knows it, and considers the criticism beneath a reply.

### Training, and the Shape of the Work

Investiture makes a Paladin's name scripture; it does not make him able to fight in the suit. What follows is months of conditioning that most candidates — already survivors of the deed that earned them the armor — describe as harder than the deed itself: learning to move a mass that answers a fraction slower than instinct wants, to breathe through the suit's own weather, to fight as part of a boarding stack or a breach team rather than as the solitary hero the Investiture's ceremony implies he now is. A Paladin does not fight alone in practice, whatever the liturgy suggests in theory; he fights inside a team of Guild-trained specialists whose whole doctrine is built around getting one armored point of decision through a wall of fire intact.

What he is trained *for* is the worst ten minutes of somebody's day: spearheading through a siege-breaker's breach behind the tide, running decapitation raids on command nodes at the exact hour the tempo doctrine has the enemy's network at maximum load, or crossing the last dead vacuum of a boarding action once a warship's shields have gone bare. It is close, ugly, personal work, and it is the one place in Imperial doctrine where the aristocracy's oldest promise — *we go first* — is actually kept, by people the aristocracy did not, until recently, consider itself obligated to keep promises to.

### The Actuarial Fact

The College does not publish figures. The College does not need to; anyone who reads enough genealogies can do the arithmetic themselves, and every Remembrancer has, quietly, at some point. Of ten men and women invested in a given decade, something like six die in the armor within five years. Three more are broken badly enough to be stood down — joints that no longer answer, a mind that no longer sleeps — and are recorded as living but retired from the line. The tenth reaches something like old bones, and his is the story told at the next Investiture's vigil, because it is the only version anyone wants to keep hearing.

### Retirement: the Warden of the Book

The man who survives is, structurally, the strangest person in the Imperium: he is already an ancestor, and he is still walking around. His name has been scripture for years; his descendants, if he has any, are kin to the house forever regardless of what happens to him now — and yet here he is, ordinary, aging, present at breakfast. Houses have settled on the one solution that avoids both the embarrassment of a commoner holding land and the worse embarrassment of discarding a living saint: a retired Paladin is commonly named **Warden of the Book**, a small hereditary office bound not to a manor but to the genealogy shrine itself — he keeps the vigil now, for the next candidate, closing a loop that the Imperium finds moving and slightly uncanny in equal measure.

House Chandragupt's current Warden of the Book, **Devapal Sen**, is instructive of what the office actually does day to day, because Chandragupt cannot help auditing anything it touches: Sen does not merely sit the vigil, he formally reviews contested witness testimony before it reaches the College's court, a retired shock trooper turned the Imperium's most quietly feared examiner of other people's heroism. He has, by his own count, sat forty-one vigils since his own armor was retired to the shrine wall beside him. He has never once said which of the forty-one he thought didn't deserve the ink. Everyone assumes there was at least one.

### Two Names, and What They Cost

**Doruk Osmanoğlu** — Vatborn, and one of the few living men to have served in the **Fortieth Legion** before it was the Fortieth Legion that the Greywater swallowed. He was elevated in year 41, in the assault that followed *Katalu*'s breach at the Anders Line, for holding a shoulder of the gap alone against a counterattack long enough for the tide to pour through behind him — a deed witnessed, unusually cleanly, by three separate officers who happened, that day, to be in exactly the right place. He wears Alevdar now, the third Vatborn to do so, and House Osmanli's Investiture entry for him carries the Founder's Mark like every batchman's before him. Three years after his elevation, the legion he came from walked into the Greywater's fog and did not come out. Doruk was not with them. He is, by every account that circulates in Osmanli messes, the reason new Vatborn candidates at their own vigils sometimes hear — quietly, from the direction of the honor guard, in a language none of them were taught — a few bars of a marching song that no living person can confirm the words to. He has never been asked to explain it. He has never offered to.

**Tendai Marufu** — Mutapa-sworn, common-born, a sapper attached to the tunneling companies at the Siege of Vell. In the eleventh month, a gallery collapse trapped a wounded evacuation column below the hospital level both sides were already leaving untouched — the Quiet Floor; Marufu held the shoring alone for the better part of a night so the column could be dragged clear, and died when the gallery finished coming down around her. There was no Remembrancer within two kilometers, and the one officer close enough to have witnessed anything filed no report before he was himself killed eight months later at a different collapse entirely. Marufu's deed surfaced eleven years afterward, by accident, in the paperwork of an unrelated court-martial that happened to place a second, more junior officer at the gallery mouth that night — an officer who had said nothing for eleven years, for reasons he gave the College's court and the court declined to record. She was invested posthumously, the first such case in living Mutapa memory, the vigil kept beside a genealogy for a name that had already been dead for over a decade. Her relic suit — commissioned the same season, as custom requires — stands unworn in Dzimba's shrine-hall to this day. No candidate has yet been proposed to wear the armor of a woman who never lived to wear it herself, and Mutapa's Remembrancers have not said whether one ever will be.

## 3. The Errantry

### Who Flies, and Why

The errantry exists to give the surplus nobility something to do besides resent the ones who inherit. A house's eldest takes the seat, the fleet, the marriage contracts; the rest — third and fourth sons, daughters married off to no advantage or married off already and widowed young, cadet lines two generations from the chair and drifting further — take a craft, a coat of arms differenced from the parent house's own, and the strike wave. It is, in the driest possible reading, a policy for keeping several thousand well-armed young aristocrats loyal to a system that was never going to give them land. In the reading the errantry itself prefers, it is the last honest chivalry left in human space: a fight conducted person to person, at named range, for named stakes, by people who chose it.

Both readings are correct, which is exactly the kind of arrangement the Imperium is built to produce and never examine. Entry is marked by the **Fledging** — a shorter, brisker rite than the Investiture, befitting nobility already presumed worthy rather than birth being proven on the spot: the cadet's arms are formally differenced from the house's own, blessed by a Remembrancer, and the craft that will carry them is named in the same ceremony, usually by the cadet, occasionally by whichever relative is paying for the hull and wants the honor.

### Lance Heraldry

An eldest son inherits the house arms whole. A younger son differences them — a label across the field, a bend broken, a charge reversed, the vocabulary of an Earth heraldic tradition nobody alive has ever seen practiced anywhere but here. An unlanded daughter has, strictly, nothing to difference *from*, no inheritance whose absence needs marking, and Imperial heraldic custom has quietly conceded the point by letting her simply choose — which is why the most inventive, least ancestor-burdened, most personal heraldry in the entire Imperium belongs to the people the system gave the least to. A lance's blazon is read on sight by anyone trained to it: house colors form the field, the difference marks the branch, and the personal charge — chosen, not inherited — is the only part of the whole picture that is actually about the pilot.

Lance names follow the same logic in miniature. Landed cadets tend toward the martial and the pious. The unlanded lean wry, and the tradition of naming a craft after the inheritance one didn't get — a coin too small to matter, a toll, a leftover — runs deep enough that Hierakon hangar crews can guess a pilot's prospects from the paint before they've read the blazon.

When a lance is reassigned rather than left orphaned, custom adds a line rather than erasing the last one: a dead pilot's name, small, painted beneath the canopy rail in the new pilot's own hand. Errants call this **riding double**, and a much-inherited hull can carry six or eight names before anyone stops counting — the errantry's own answer to a suit's engraved collar, kept in paint instead of ink, by a custom nobody thought to ask the College's permission before starting.

### The Hierakon: Half Monastery, Half Stable

A lance-carrier's culture earns the description exactly. Errants keep something close to a monastic rule aboard — fixed hours, chapel services worked around flight rotations, a formality of address that persists even in the ready room — and simultaneously keep a stable culture that would be recognizable to anyone who has ever cared for a horse. Errants groom their own craft. Ground crews handle the heavy maintenance, but the custom holds that no other hand arms a lance's guns or seats its pilot's harness before a sortie, and errants who skip the ritual — wash the canopy themselves, check their own weapons, sit an hour in the hangar the evening before a strike — are considered, by the superstition of the whole culture, to be flying carelessly whatever their sortie record says. Craft are spoken of, fed offerings before hard missions in exactly the register villages use for the siege-breaker mechs, and mourned by name when they're lost even when the pilot survives — which happens more than anyone likes admitting, a lance destroyed under a pilot who ejects clear being treated, informally, as a genuine bereavement rather than a hardware loss.

### The Duel-Minded

The formal Imperial position is that sending a machine to fight is a species of cowardice. The informal position is the formal one, at volume. Traditionalist lances still open engagements by broadcasting a formal challenge on open frequency before firing — a custom with no possible answer, since Union pilots have neither the doctrine nor, generally, the patience to respond to it, and the errantry mostly knows this and does it anyway, reciting a hail into empty static as a matter of form before doing exactly the violence the hail announced.

This is not merely picturesque; it is exploitable, and FLEETCOM has built doctrine around it. A drone-first approach specifically baits the duel-minded lance into wasting its opening pass on the wrong target, or into ignoring a Fetch's fire entirely on the theory that machines don't count — a theory that has killed a great many errants who never got to revise it. Post-Withering lances, particularly the Patchwork's newer entrants, break the code far more readily than tradition approves of, and are noticeably harder for Union pilots to bait. The traditionalists call this dishonorable. The traditionalists, disproportionately, are the ones who die proving the point.

### The Withering, Thirty Years On

At Cerulyn Deep, in the three days of strike battles that opened the war's one great fleet action, perhaps half the errantry of the Imperium died — the **Withering of the Flower**, still called that by everyone, still felt everywhere thirty years later. A Hierakon at established strength carries a wing of forty lances. Most fly today at two-thirds of that, some as low as half, and the shortfall is not craft: Guild yards can build lances as freely as they build siege-breaker hulls, and have offered to. What cannot be manufactured is the specific thing the errantry requires — cadet nobility willing, or spendable, or both — and every house that lost heavily thirty years ago is still, quietly, thirty years behind on replacing what it lost.

The material residue of the Withering sits in Hierakon hangars across the Reach: **orphan lances**, craft that survived their pilots and have simply never been reassigned, because tradition holds a lance is inherited or won, not handed to whoever's available — the same logic that keeps Porphyrogene empty, applied fleet-wide. Some houses have let their orphans accumulate for a full generation rather than break the custom. The war does not care. The war has simply started finding out how much that costs.

### Three More Names

**Adelheid Habsberg**, unlanded, flew at Cerulyn Deep and survived it — one of a shrinking cohort who remembers the Withering as a battle rather than a legend. Her lance, ***Last Farthing***, is thirty-one years old and has been rebuilt around its own cockpit twice; she has refused every offer of a newer hull, on the grounds, stated once and never repeated, that a farthing that's been spent already knows what it's for. She commands training wings now aboard a Hierakon on permanent rotation to the Halvard's convoy run, and is, by informal but universal agreement among younger errants of every house, the only living person permitted to tell a traditionalist to his face that the challenge hail is theater. She is Habsberg's proof that the house's endless daughters are not only married off — some of them, it turns out, were simply saving themselves for something the marriage contracts never offered.

**Tomas fitz-Rogier** flies under House Normand's colors and a genealogy that did not exist twelve years ago. Born Tomas Vey, a common gunnery rating with an unaccountable gift for close-range deflection shooting, he was quietly furnished — the mechanism is never discussed above a whisper — with a paper parentage naming him natural son to a minor, conveniently deceased Normand marcher lord, and Fledged under the "fitz-" style Normand reserves for exactly this purpose. His lance, ***Paper Crown***, is the joke everyone in Normand's Hierakons understands and nobody says aloud. He is, by kill tally, one of the three most effective errants currently flying, which is precisely the problem the College of Remembrancers has declined for twelve years to have an opinion about.

**Xochimel**, flying out of Tenocht, was never nominated by anything resembling a genealogical committee — Aztlan's errantry has always been chosen by the same ritual calendar that chooses everything else in the house, a fact that predates the Withering and made Aztlan, alone among the twelve, essentially immune to the crisis that broke everyone else's replacement pipeline. Her lance, ***the Cut Flower***, has the highest confirmed kill count of any craft in Aztlan's wing and, per the house's own accounting, an equally notable number of engagements her gun-camera records but her after-action report does not fully explain. The Bestiary's naval counterpart has stopped asking Aztlan pilots what happens to craft they disable rather than destroy. The pattern is familiar to anyone who has read the ground war's annex on Aztlan's missing.

## 4. The Patchwork

Half the errantry died in three days thirty years ago, and the houses have spent three decades solving the resulting shortage in twelve different ways, none of them coordinated, most of them deniable, and not one of them officially acknowledged anywhere in Imperial law. The Great Council has never convened to discuss the errantry's replacement crisis as a single problem, because doing so would require admitting there is one crisis rather than twelve private embarrassments.

| House | The unofficial answer |
|---|---|
| **Palaiogos** | Flies fewer. Ancient lances stand empty rather than dilute the blood — see Porphyrogene, and the shrine-hall it stands in. |
| **Karolus** | Recruits hardest among the lesser nobility, folds errant commissions into the same total-war machine that builds its armies, and rewards ferociously — a Karolus Fledging is, by wide agreement, the fastest promotion in the Imperium for anyone with a claim to gentle blood, however thin. |
| **Habsberg** | Markets its surplus daughters as attached lances, folded into the same marriage-treaty web that supplies allied banners — an errant's service becomes, quietly, one more clause in a contract that used to be only about weddings. |
| **Yamatai** | Trains fewer, and better, and refuses dilution outright. A Yamatai wing today is smaller than before the Withering by roughly the same margin every other house is smaller — the difference is that Yamatai calls this a choice. |
| **Rurikov** | Never had much of a void arm to begin with, and has simply declined to build one now — Rurikov's treasure goes to winter levies and house screens, and its Hierakon complements are, by house admission, thin enough that the Bestiary's naval counterpart barely bothers grading them. |
| **Normand** | Buys heraldry. Commoner aces with a gift and no pedigree receive a fast-tracked or outright forged genealogy and fly under the "fitz-" style — genealogical fraud, the Imperium's gravest heresy, committed at scale, in the open, by a chaired great house. The College of Remembrancers has not ruled. What the College's continued silence is costing House Normand has never been stated where anyone could quote it. |
| **Osmanli** | Seats its own trusted Vatborn in lances openly, marked craft and all — a small stylized vessel-charge, the **Vessel Mark**, painted at the wingtip of every Vatborn errant's lance, acknowledged inside Osmanli's own chaplaincy and defended, without waiting for Elysium's permission, by house Remembrancers who have simply started treating the sealed question as answered. The rest of the Imperium has agreed, collectively and without discussion, not to look at the wingtips too closely. |
| **Ming** | Buys craft, not blood. Ming fields the smallest committed errantry of any chair and the largest checkbook, retaining spare lances from other houses' wings under contract, colors repainted, pilots paid rather than sworn — an arrangement every other house finds distasteful and every other house's cash-poor cadet branches quietly use. |
| **Chandragupt** | Opened the errantry to the same civil examinations that run the rest of the house — formally meritocratic, technically blood-neutral, and functionally indistinguishable from Normand's fraud in its result, except that it comes wrapped in enough audited legitimacy that no one has managed to call it heresy. |
| **Mutapa** | Invests in hangars and ground infrastructure rather than pilots, keeps a token wing, and has never pretended the void was its house's calling — Mutapa's boast has always been what stands, not what flies. |
| **Jorvik** | Shifted the weight sideways rather than solving the shortfall — boarding-carrier crews and drakke complements absorb the martial-glory function the errantry used to hold alone, and a Jorvik cadet with ambition increasingly chooses a drakke over a lance. |
| **Aztlan** | Barely noticed. The ritual calendar chose its errants before the Withering and chooses them the same way now — Aztlan is the one house for whom "who counts as noble enough to fly" was never really the question. |

Nothing here is admitted, anywhere, at any level the Great Council would recognize as official. It is an open secret at wildly different depths per house — Osmanli's is spoken of by its own chaplains from a pulpit; Normand's is spoken of by no one, in rooms with the door checked twice. What every house shares is the same unstated conclusion: better a patchwork the Imperium pretends not to see than an errantry so thinned it stops existing as an institution the aristocracy can point to and say, *this is what makes us worth following.*

## 5. How Each Sees the Other

Paladins consider the errantry a beautiful, doomed extravagance — knights who die for style points in a domain where the enemy mostly sends machines to be killed instead. Errants consider Paladins grim tradesmen wearing someone else's inheritance, competent, essential, and faintly embarrassing to be seen dueling beside — because a Paladin does not observe the challenge, does not care about the aesthetics of the kill, and treats a boarding action exactly like the breach it structurally is. Both assessments are accurate and both knighthoods know it, which produces less rivalry than a kind of weary mutual respect: neither would trade jobs, and both privately suspect the other has the worse one.

They meet operationally in exactly one place: the boarding action, once a warship's shields have gone bare in the press. Errant lances fly close escort for the drakkes and assault shuttles carrying Paladin boarding stacks across the last kilometers, screening the approach the way Jorvik's own craft always have; Paladins clear the breach the errantry's escort bought them. It is the one moment the two knighthoods must actually cooperate, and Imperial custom has produced a formal, rarely invoked protocol for dividing the honors afterward — who accepts a surrendering captain's blade, whose banner is planted first, whose genealogy records the taking. The protocol exists because it has been needed. It has, on at least three occasions that both knighthoods' own chroniclers will confirm off the record, produced a formal challenge between an errant and a Paladin over precisely that question, fought by the old rules, on the deck of the ship they had just taken together.

Both knighthoods will say, if pressed, that the other one doesn't understand what dying is actually for. Both have been fighting the same war from two directions that rarely look at each other — and on the one deck where they must, the protocol divides the honors, the challenge settles what the protocol cannot, and each side goes home certain the other missed the point of the ship they just took together.

## 6. What the UGF Makes of Both

The professional assessment is dry and specific, and treats the two knighthoods as entirely separate problems, because they are.

Paladins are tracked, house by house, in a classified annex to the Bestiary — *Hostile Forces Estimate 7, Annex P* — that grades them not by house but by individual suit where a suit's history is known, on the theory (correct, per UGF after-action data) that a four-century relic with forty-one names in its collar performs differently than a five-year-old one. Standing doctrine is engage at range, do not close, do not let a breach team reach hand-to-hand distance if any alternative exists — advice that UGF assault engineers, who face siege-breakers under similar instructions, find grimly familiar. FLEETCOM's own handbook on the errantry is blunter about exploitation than sentiment: drone-first tactics are written up explicitly as leveraging "documented duel-priority behavior in Imperial strike pilots," which is the driest possible way to describe watching a man choose to die for etiquette.

The Union's DIS reads the Patchwork as intelligence in its own right, whatever the Great Council declines to confirm: a Vessel Mark on a lance's wingtip or a suspiciously fresh Normand pedigree is logged, cross-referenced against the Bestiary's manpower estimates, and treated as a direct measurement of which houses are actually running short. FLEETCOM analysts have quietly concluded that the Imperium's silence on its own knighthoods is more informative than most things the Imperium says out loud — a house that has to cheat is a house that has to admit, somewhere in its own paperwork, exactly how much it needs to cheat by.

What soldiers actually say is shorter and uglier and, on the ground, has settled into a single word. UGF infantry call an incoming Paladin team "**Ink**" — because he's written in, because there's no crossing it out, because by the time you can confirm the callout it's usually too late to matter. Union naval aviators call errants "**chivs**," short for chivalry, half contempt and half the specific grudging respect reserved for an enemy who will not take the easy shot. Neither term appears in any doctrine manual. Both appear in every debrief that matters.

## Open questions

- Tendai Marufu's relic suit stands unworn in Dzimba's shrine-hall. Will Mutapa ever propose a candidate to wear the armor of a woman who died before it was forged — and what happens to whoever does?
- Who invented the Founder's Mark, and when? No Remembrancer will claim authorship of a convention every genealogy in the Imperium now uses without exception.

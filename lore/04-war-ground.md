# The War in the Reach: the Ground
*The war is planned in orbit, paid for at home, and decided in the mud.*

---

## 1. The Reach

The contested expansion zone between the powers — resources and habitable worlds both are expanding into. Both sides use the same name for it; the name is older than either of them, a Concord survey label that outlived the surveyors. In fifty-two years of war, neither power has proposed a different one. It is the only thing they have agreed on since the shooting started.

## 2. The character of the war

- **A limited war.** Decades of grinding attrition — but neither side has fully committed. The cost of total war would not outweigh the benefits of winning; the limit is cold arithmetic, not treaty or taboo. Apocalyptic for the Reach, a managed expenditure for the core worlds. *The load-bearing wall of the setting: change the math and the ceiling moves.*
- **Soft lines.** Detection networks and patrol ranges create de facto fronts. The most valuable real estate is sensor infrastructure — pickets, listening posts, deep arrays. Blinding the enemy is constant low-grade warfare even during truces; fleet battles happen where someone punches through a blind spot.
- **Attrition math.** UFS tech wins battles; Imperial crèches make battles not matter. The Union cannot attrit an enemy that manufactures people; the Imperium cannot crack Union core worlds.

The arithmetic deserves one more sentence, because everything below rests on it. On the Union side, the price of war is denominated in voters: every casualty is a family, a district, a by-election. On the Imperial side, it is denominated in prestige and treasure: houses spend levies freely and their own standing dearly, and a house that beggars itself winning the emperor's war has lost its own. Two different currencies, one identical conclusion — *keep the war the size it is.* Fifty-two years of corpses, balanced on a ledger.

## 3. Taking worlds

**Full invasions.** Vatborn legions spent by the hundred thousand, Paladins as the spear-tip — against UGF combined-arms divisions and orbital fire. The meat grinder grinds differently on each side.

An invasion has a grammar, and everyone knows it, because the physics allows no surprises. The invasion fleet crosses the shoal and burns in-system for days in full view; the defenders spend those days doing arithmetic — what can be dug, mined, dispersed, evacuated, or burned before the landings. Orbit is then contested rather than won: massed ground air defense means the invader buys *windows* over the surface, not ownership of the sky. The landings go down through those windows, seize ground the AA net can grow from, and then the war becomes what this file is about — the long middle, months or years of it, supplied convoy by convoy from above.

Worlds fall three ways: the defense is broken (rare), the defense withers when its resupply windows close (common), or the defense is *sold* — evacuated under a negotiated fiction so both sides can call it something other than surrender. The Reach's diplomats are mostly quartermasters with better manners.

## 4. Rules of war

**Weak conventions** — old Concord-era laws of war (the **Ceres Conventions**, in the usage both sides inherited) that both sides cite when accusing each other, honored when convenient or when reciprocity is feared. Prisoner exchanges happen; so do massacres. Whether Vatborn prisoners count as POWs is an unresolved legal horror for the UFS.

In practice the conventions survive where the war is old and dies where it is new. On fronts that have ground for a decade, elaborate reciprocities evolve — stretcher truces, prisoner ledgers, the unmolested water party — enforced by nothing but the certainty of tomorrow. On fresh fronts, where the units haven't met before, the war is young and vicious until it learns manners. Veterans on both sides say the same thing about it: the front has to *settle*, like a house. What it settles onto is bones.

## 5. How battles are fought

### Two doctrines, two societies

- **The Imperium** fights a WW1/WW2-style war with advanced kit: tanks, aircraft, mechanized formations, massed guns — powerful but unrefined, with orders flowing from the top. There is no single "Imperial army": it is a **feudal patchwork** of house armies with wildly uneven doctrine and quality. Crack regiments hold ridges beside inbred marcher levies; inter-house coordination is diplomacy under fire, and a lord may let a rival's flank collapse for court advantage. UGF intelligence catalogues houses like scouting reports. The Imperium's worst defeats are self-inflicted; its most feared armies are the rare true coalitions.
- **The UGF** fights modern combined-arms: delegated initiative, networked recon, drones integrated at platoon level (recon partners, not robot swarms — their edge is *informational*), precision on-call fires. Devastating when prepared.

The deeper difference is what each army does when the plan dies. A UGF captain whose orders stop making sense is trained — required — to improvise toward the intent; the network exists so that ten improvisations knit into one battle. An Imperial column whose orders stop making sense keeps executing them, magnificently, until new orders arrive from someone entitled to give them. Both behaviors are rational. One society trusts its lieutenants; the other has spent a thousand years making sure it never has to.

### Win conditions

The UGF wins battles it is prepared for. The Imperium wins battles of *commitment* — when it throws everything it has. Imperial operational art is therefore about forcing engagements the UGF cannot prepare for.

### The tempo doctrine

UGF combined arms is a symphony with a refresh rate: recon feeds targeting, targeting feeds fires, fires enable maneuver. The Imperium's great discovery, written in a million Vatborn lives: attack continuously, everywhere, without pause, and the symphony cannot keep up. The UGF kills ten for one and still loses ground because the next wave arrives while the last fire mission is still processing.

The doctrine has a cost the Imperium does not discuss and a name the UGF does not use in press briefings. Imperial staff colleges teach it as **the Unresting Assault**; UGF after-action reports call it **saturation offense**; UGF soldiers call it *the tide*, and the word appears in their sleep studies. It was proven at Serrat, in the war's ninth year — see the mythic events below — and no prepared UGF position has been considered safe since.

### Siege-breaker mechs

The Imperium's answer to the prepared position: walking fortresses (Guild-built, Guild-maintained — another leash on the houses) that wade through kill zones soaking fire that would annihilate a battalion, cracking the line so the tide pours through the breach. Inefficient, monstrously expensive, effective.

Each is unique, named, and old; houses lease them from the Guilds by campaign, at prices that have decided elections. Levies are forbidden to pray to them, which tells you what the levies do. *(The siege-breakers have their own file, and their own church.)*

### Paladins in battle

Special forces and shock elite: critical objectives, decapitation raids on command nodes and drone operations, spearheading assaults through the breach. Nasty synergy with tempo doctrine — the human waves force the UGF network to run at maximum load, and *that* is when Paladins hit the nodes running it.

### Air and orbit

Contested. Massed ground-based air defense limits both air power and orbital fire; ships in low orbit over a defended world are targets. Wars must be won in the mud — exactly where Imperial mass counts. The Imperium invests obsessively in AA as its great equalizer.

### Artillery

UGF fires arrive as single perfect answers — a drone flags a bunker; ninety seconds later it ceases to exist. Imperial fires arrive as *weather*: week-long massed barrages from thousands of Guild-forged guns, aimed at map squares. Inefficient — but saturation drowns sensors and spotting, so the dumb barrage is accidentally electronic warfare. Veterans tell whose shells are falling by rhythm alone.

### Logistics

Both sides depend on **orbital resupply windows**; the convoy war above (escorts, raiders, Ederi Khagans vs UFS shipping, UGF hunter squadrons vs house tenders) is where fleets fight most. A ground army that loses its window doesn't get defeated — it *withers*. Some of the Reach's worst tragedies are armies that won every battle and ran out of everything.

Since the war's one great fleet battle — after which both admiralties concluded that capital ships cannot be replaced on war-relevant timescales — no fleet will bleed itself to force a closed window open. The armies below know this. It is the coldest fact in a soldier's life: somewhere above him, someone precious has already decided what he is worth.

### Terrain writes each war

No two campaign-worlds fight alike: continental trench belts on open steppes, months-long arcology sieges, mountain wars where mechs can't walk and Paladins are kings, swamp wars where sensors bloom with false returns and the tide drowns platoons in fog.

## 6. The soldier's war

- *UGF:* dissonance defines the veteran. The terror of the tide — endless waves, ammunition counting, quiet worship of the network; the isolation of small prepared teams and distant fires; then rotation home to a consumer paradise where the war is a streaming category. The horror isn't losing — it's *winning*, hour after hour, until something runs out.
- *Imperial:* three armies under one banner. **Levies** — fatalism, faith, the banner, and the man from your village beside you. **Vatborn** — made for this and told so, yet developing their own quiet batch-cultures: songs, superstitions, loyalty to each other deeper than to any house. What a Vatborn believes as he walks into the guns is one of the setting's great mysteries (and what most unsettles UGF soldiers — the waves don't stop, and sometimes they're singing). **Nobles and Paladins** — a war of reputation, oaths, and court standing, where a glorious assault that wastes ten thousand levies can be a career triumph; the Paladin's honor is the one aristocratic virtue that is real, because it is paid in person.

Across the wire, the two soldiers misunderstand each other with perfect symmetry. The Imperial levy believes the UGF soldier fights for money, because he cannot imagine an army without a banner. The UGF soldier believes the levy fights because he is forced to, because she cannot imagine volunteering for *that*. Both are wrong often enough to keep the war interesting for the intelligence services.

## 7. Halvard's World

The war's Verdun — named for a forgotten settler, now a curse-word on both sides. A meat-grinder world neither power will abandon, existing partly *because* it lets both bleed each other without escalating. The pressure valve made of corpses.

### The world

Halvard's World is the central Reach's one true garden world: temperate, self-fertile, breathable without machinery — the kind of world the Reach was supposed to be full of and isn't. One great continent, named **Providence** by settlers who did not live to revise it, girdles the temperate belt between two cold oceans: steppe grain-country in the interior, drowned estuary lands in the south — **the Meanders** — and the **Kolda Massif** walling the north. The Halvard family landed some hundred and thirty years before the war; when the armies arrived, 1.2 million people were farming Providence and exporting food to half the central Reach.

That is precisely why it matters. Every other battlefield in the Reach runs on the orbital window; Halvard's World *feeds armies*. It is the only world in the theater where the logistics arithmetic relaxes — where a garrison can eat off the land, and a lost convoy means hardship instead of withering. Both powers seized a hemisphere of it early (Union landings in the war's sixth year; continuous front since the eighth) and neither has left, because whoever held it entire would own the Reach's only self-supplying staging world. The one world where an army cannot starve is the world armies go to die on.

Its second virtue is geometric. Halvard's sits deep inside both powers' overlapping sensor umbrellas, under two of the densest AA nets ever built — the northern net is Mutapa's masterwork; the southern is the UGF's. Nothing approaches unseen; orbit is mutually and permanently denied; no offensive there can produce decisive surprise. A war that cannot be suddenly lost is a war that can be safely continued. The staffs on both sides understood this years before they admitted it to themselves.

### The fronts

- **The Long Front** — nineteen hundred kilometers of trench belt across the interior steppe, the oldest continuously fought line in human history. Field fortifications have evolved here the way cathedrals once did: by generations of anonymous labor. Rurikov holds the Imperial winter sectors by ancient arrangement; winter is when they start.
- **The Wet Flank** — the Meanders: tidal channels, fog, false sensor returns, platoon-sized wars fought by boat and rumor. Drone loss rates here are the worst in the theater; the fog is credited with opinions.
- **The Roof** — the Kolda Massif, where mechs can't walk and Paladins are kings. The high passes change hands in company-strength epics nobody at headquarters can see. UGF mountain troops and house retainers have fought up there so long they share a dialect of hand signs.
- **The Glass Country** — what remains of **Halvardstead**, the settlers' capital, fused by years of incendiary barrages into a plain of black glass and cellar-holes. Both sides blame the other; the barrage logs of both, veterans note, are missing the same three months.

Behind the fronts, two shore-cities anchor the war: **Fairlanding**, the Union port complex in the south, now the largest UGF installation in the Reach; and the **Kostantine Landing** in the north, the Imperial depot-city consecrated to the first emperor, whose warehouse-cathedrals serve both functions without embarrassment.

### The rhythm

Offensives come in **pushing seasons** — roughly twice a year, when the convoy cycles of both powers converge and each side has banked enough shells to spend. Everyone knows the calendar; the barrages begin within days of each other like a liturgical observance. In between: raids, sniping, wire-farming, and the sensor war, which never pauses for anything.

The soldiers do not number the years; they name them. **The Year of Mud.** **The Year of the Bells** (an Imperial push preceded, inexplicably, by every church bell in the northern zone ringing for a day — the UGF still doesn't know why, and the levies won't say). **The Year Without a Push**, which no one trusted and which produced, through sheer nerves, nearly the casualties of an offensive. The year-names are the only history of Halvard's World its veterans recognize. The official histories use dates, and are read by no one who was there.

### Life in the grinder

A UGF tour on Halvard's is fourteen months — *fourteen and home* — rotated through line, reserve, and Fairlanding leave in a cycle so fixed that soldiers describe their whole war by where the cycle caught them. Imperial contingents rotate by court politics instead: a house buys sectors of the line the way it buys precedence at Mars, holds them for glory or penance, and passes them on. Only Rurikov never rotates out. Some Vatborn legions have been on Halvard's so long they are effectively *from* there — batch-cultures with local roots, who tend graves of batches before them.

And in between the lines, astonishing everyone, live the **Remainders** — some sixty thousand descendants of settlers who never left. They farm the shell-swept country between and behind the fronts under tacit license from both armies, because both armies eat. They sell eggs at a fair price, in either currency, and are the only people on the world both sides feel vaguely ashamed in front of. Killing a Remainder is the one atrocity Halvard's World does not forgive; units on both sides have handed their own men over rather than carry the luck of it.

Nobody knows the world's true casualty total. The number soldiers use is three million. Nobody can say three million *what*.

### The mythology

Fifty years of grinding have produced a folklore thicker than either army's doctrine, and it does not respect the wire.

- **Old Halvard.** The settler himself, walking the lines before an offensive — a tall figure in farm clothes, seen at the wire at dusk, counting. Both armies see him. Seeing him means the push comes at dawn; some units hold that the man who sees him is exempt, others that he is already spent. Men argue the point in two languages. Offerings are universal: the last swallow of anything, poured out. Quartermasters on both sides quietly over-issue by a ration they call, in writing, *evaporation*.
- **The phrases.** Both languages have absorbed the world. *Gone to Halvard* — dead, in Union barracks slang and Imperial levy-speech alike. *Paying Halvard's rent* — holding a quiet sector, losing men to no purpose anyone can name. *Green again* — never, or eventually, as in *when Halvard's is green again*: the toast veterans of both armies drink without translating, because it doesn't need it.
- **The song.** The settlers left behind a planting song called **"Green Again,"** and the world taught it to everyone. The UGF sings it with barrack-room verses; the levies sing it as a hymn; the Vatborn sing it with verses no one taught them and no one outside a batch quite catches. It is canon on both sides of the wire that you do not fire on singing. This is honored irregularly — which is to say, it is honored. The worst thing a UGF veteran of Halvard's can tell you, and the thing they tell you last: sometimes the tide comes in singing it, and the guns answer anyway, and afterward the defenders' own version doesn't taste right for a while.
- **The luck.** A blade of Halvard grass in the helmet band, on both sides. Never say aloud the days left in your tour. Never rebuild the same bunker twice on the same footings — the third one always dies. The superstitions cross the lines faster than the diseases do.

### Why neither side leaves

The official answers: strategy (the staging world), honor (the graves), politics (no government survives abandoning the ground three million died for — sunk cost fossilized into policy). The real answer is the valve. Halvard's World absorbs offensives that would otherwise seek decision somewhere dangerous. It is the one arena where both powers can demonstrate resolve, bleed the enemy, feed the newsfeeds and the chronicles, and be *certain* nothing decisive will happen — the geometry forbids it. Both general staffs budget for Halvard's the way cities budget for fire departments. Both have commissioned withdrawal studies. Both keep the studies classified — not from the enemy, from their own governments.

The war needs a place to happen. Halvard's World is where the war is kept.

## 8. The scouting report

UGF military intelligence publishes an annual assessment of Imperial house ground forces — formally *Hostile Forces Estimate 7: House Armies*, universally called **the Bestiary**. It grades each house by **Contact Grade**: Grade One, *decline engagement absent overmatch*; Grade Two, *engage with fires advantage*; Grade Three, *engage with standard preparation*; Grade Four, *engage at will*. The grades are dry. The annexes are not. Excerpts, current revision:

### House Palaiogos — Grade Three; Porphyry Guard formations, Grade Two
Ceremonial armies, rarely deployed forward; parade doctrine, antique equipment — Guild-maintained, and therefore not actually antique. The **Porphyry Guard** appears only where the throne's honor is staked, and fights accordingly. *Assessment: an army built to be seen. Do not volunteer to test whether it can also fight.*

### House Karolus — Grade Two, trending One
The war party's house. Largest committed house army in the Reach; aggressive, orthodox, and — uniquely dangerous — genuinely cooperative with allied houses, because it wants the throne and needs coalition victories to claim it. Offensive timing correlates with the court calendar; expect maximum effort as the election approaches. *They are not fighting us. They are campaigning.*

### House Habsberg — Grade Four; see Annex C before acting on this grade
A small, indifferent army that has not won an unassisted battle in living memory. Annex C: Habsberg banners never come alone. Their marriages are mutual-defense instruments; strike a Habsberg weakness and treaty obligations drag crack allied formations onto the field. Three UGF commanders have attacked Habsberg and met Yamatai. *The banner is bait. It does not know it is bait, which is why it works.*

### House Yamatai — Grade One
The finest infantry in the Imperium: small, superb, and the only Imperial force that attacks *silence-first* — night assaults without preparatory fires, which defeats a doctrine built on answering fires. They observe the Ceres Conventions to the letter and return prisoners with ceremony. They will retreat, but only on orders written before the battle; nothing that happens during one changes their minds. *Pray for daylight.*

### House Rurikov — Grade Two
The tempo doctrine's true believers. Levy quality poor, levy quantity decisive, staff work patient and glacial. They attack in conditions that ground our drones and thicken the sensor fog — which is to say, they attack in winter, on worlds where winter is worth attacking in. Holding a line against Rurikov is a mathematics examination that lasts until spring. *They do not stop in winter. Winter is when they start.*

### House Normand — Grade One for the first two hundred hours; Grade Three thereafter
The best mechanized assault formations in the Imperium; breakthrough artists; chronically overextended, historically and constitutionally. Their spearheads at hour two hundred are out of fuel, out of shells, and out of friends. Doctrine note: trade space for time and let the offensive exhaust itself. *Defeat House Normand by losing to it slowly.*

### House Osmanli — Grade One
The guns and the Vatborn corps. The heaviest, best-served artillery in the Imperium — their barrages are the weather the others merely forecast. Their Vatborn are the theater anomaly: long-service, promoted on merit, proud, and *retained* — they fight like professionals, not like a tide, and they exercise initiative. Troops find this more disturbing than the waves. *The waves that think.*

### House Ming — Grade Four in contact; Priority One in effect
Garrison formations: well-equipped, competently led, visibly uninterested in dying for anyone's precedence. Irrelevant in the field and decisive around it — where Ming administers, every neighboring house formation is fed, fueled, paid, and at establishment strength. Ming presence is the single best predictor of enemy offensive sustainability we have. *You do not fight House Ming. You fight everyone House Ming has fed.*

### House Chandragupt — Grade Two
The vast levies, run by a bureaucracy that audits attrition. Slow to arrive, methodical in the assault, and terrifyingly sustainable: their staff calculates what a position is worth in lives, pays exactly that, and stops — then returns next season with a corrected estimate. The worst possible opponent for a fixed position you must hold indefinitely. *They know what your ridge costs to the decimal, and they have already decided to pay it.*

### House Mutapa — Grade Two in the open; Grade One dug in, besieging, or beneath you
The Imperium's siege engineers and air-defense masters — the reason the sky stays shut. A month of Mutapa occupation closes the orbit and honeycombs the ground; their reduction of the Vell arcology is taught at our own staff college, through gritted teeth. *If Mutapa is digging, the world is already half theirs — the half underneath.*

### House Jorvik — Grade Two, raiding; not assessed for line combat
Descent-raiders: fast landings against ports, depots, and orbit-heads, frequently in company with Ederi wings — the Estimate has given up determining who escorts whom. They do not hold ground and do not want it. *They come down like weather and leave like a bill.*

### House Aztlan — Grade One, annotated
Shock infantry beyond rational doctrine: assaults pressed home through fires that break every other house, on a ritual calendar our analysts can partially predict and not at all understand. They take prisoners by strong preference. Sectors opposite Aztlan report fewer killed and more missing than any other frontage in the theater. The Estimate declines to speculate regarding the missing. The soldiers do not. *Pray for Yamatai.*

### The prayer list

Enlisted usage compresses the Estimate into liturgy. UGF soldiers pray not to face: Yamatai at night, Osmanli's guns, Mutapa when the digging has started, and Aztlan under any circumstances whatsoever. They hope for: Ming garrisons, Habsberg banners (the new men hope for this; the old men read Annex C), Palaiogos parade formations, and any marcher levy whose lord is feuding with his neighbor — which the Bestiary tracks in a court-gossip annex that is, by internal audit, the most-read intelligence product in the Union.

The Imperium certainly keeps an equivalent volume on us. No copy has ever been taken. That fact has its own annex.

## 9. Mythic events of the ground war

Every long war keeps a private calendar. These are the dates on it — the events soldiers on both sides know, whatever the official histories say.

### The Vigil of Guns — Serrat, war year 9
The proving of the tempo doctrine. On the steppe world of Serrat, the UGF had built the perfect defense — the war-college position, layered, networked, rehearsed. A Rurikov-led coalition attacked it continuously for thirty-one days, through its own high holy days — the chaplains ruled the assault itself an act of devotion, hence the name — and the perfect defense performed perfectly and lost. Kill ratios exceeded ten to one throughout; the line moved anyway, because the next wave always arrived while the last fire mission was still processing. UGF survivors called it **the Loud Month** and the name stuck to the whole class of horror. After Serrat, the Union wrote a new field manual. The Imperium wrote a liturgy.

### The Spite — Maren Col, war year 17
At the Maren Col, a Novgoroy auxiliary corps — fighting, as Novgoroy always fights, for the elevation its Prince covets — held the flank of a Rurikov advance. When UGF **Operation LEVER** struck the pass, Rurikov's guns were registered, in range, and silent for six hours. Some forty thousand auxiliaries died in a pocket the guns could have opened. The Rurikov marshal was censured in public and congratulated in private; his house's client-rival had been humbled at wholesale prices. Union staff colleges teach LEVER as the model exploitation; the honest instructors add that the model requires an enemy that hates itself. Ask an Imperial veteran about Maren Col and he will call it the Spite, and will not say whose.

### The Corvant Pocket — war years 31–32
The proverbial army that won everything and ran out of everything. When the convoy war closed the resupply window over Corvant, FLEETCOM — nine years after the great fleet battle, husbanding hulls it could never replace — declined to force it open. The **Ninth Expeditionary Army**, ninety thousand strong under **General Ansa Reyes-Okafor**, fought on alone for thirteen months and won twenty-nine consecutive engagements while it starved. Doctrine said keep the network fed before the men, and they did, and the reports call the choice correct, and the reports are right, and no one who reads them is glad. Eleven thousand were still standing when Reyes-Okafor capitulated — to the Yamatai marshal **Lord Benkei Ashiwara**, who granted full honors of war, paraded his own regiments to salute the Ninth as it marched out, and returned the general her sidearm. Imperial chaplains preach the campaign as **the Fast of the Apostates** — heaven withholding bread from the faithless. Imperial soldiers who were at Corvant do not preach about it at all. The Ninth won twenty-nine battles. The thirtieth was against arithmetic.

### The Siege of Vell — war years 36–38
Nineteen months to reduce one arcology. Mutapa closed the sky over Vell in the first six weeks, then took the city the only way a city like that can be taken: from below, gallery by counter-sunk gallery, level by level, at a pace their engineers published internally in advance and met. What the war remembers is Level 40 — **the Quiet Floor** — the hospital level, which no weapon touched in nineteen months, by no agreement anyone signed. Wounded of both sides were carried up and down; both commands denied the arrangement existed; both quartermasters' ledgers show it being provisioned. When Vell fell, the Quiet Floor kept working under Mutapa administration for nine further days as though nothing in particular had happened — the only institution on Vell to survive the siege. No one negotiated the Quiet Floor. That is the only reason it held.

### The Greywater — war year 44
On the tide-world the Union charts call Vasseur and everyone else calls the Greywater, the **Fortieth Legion** — ten thousand long-service Vatborn under Osmanli colors, among the best the crèches ever produced — advanced into the estuary fog and did not come out. No bodies, no wreckage, no distress traffic. Accused of a massacre, the UGF did something unprecedented: it published its fire logs for the sector, complete. They show nothing. No mission was fired into the Greywater that day. The Guilds surveyed the estuary once, the following year, and sealed the survey. Since then, pickets on both sides report legion marching songs on dead frequencies when the fog is high — always the same songs, always slightly wrong. Ten thousand men walked into the fog. Sometimes, on the right frequency, it sings.

## 10. The cracks in the wall

The limited war holds because the arithmetic holds: Union casualties are priced in voters, Imperial commitment is priced in house treasure and standing, and neither price is currently worth paying at total-war scale. That is the whole wall. These are the visible cracks.

- **The election.** The Imperium's ceiling is the houses' unwillingness to spend, and an emperor is what could overrule them. A war candidate on the throne — and House Karolus has spent a generation becoming exactly that — converts fifty-two years of managed expenditure into mandate overnight. The opposite failure is as dangerous: a disputed election that recalls house armies from the Reach hands the UGF a windfall of ground it cannot digest, and nothing escalates a limited war like one side suddenly, accidentally winning it.
- **The Union braid.** The Union's half of the arithmetic assumes every casualty votes. Autonomous weapons remove the voter from the casualty; a Union cloning program would do the same and salt the wound with everything the crusade claims to be against. The Union's war party understands this perfectly — that is precisely the argument for both. The day dying becomes cheap for the Union is the day the ceiling moves, and the Union will have paid for it with the thing the war was supposedly about.
- **A Guild interdict.** The house war runs on Guild forges: the guns, the mechs, the crèches, the keels. An interdicted house's front does not lose; it switches *off*. The UGF surging into such a silence could take something the Imperium cannot account as a limited loss — and a Great Council convinced the Union had *procured* the interdict would finally have a grievance worth total war. The third superpower has never picked a side. The wall assumes it never will.
- **A decided sea.** The arithmetic assumes contested orbit and fleets too precious to spend — the lesson both admiralties bled for at the war's one great fleet battle. If someone ever wins the second wager outright, the withering becomes general across an entire theater, and the losing side chooses between escalation and collapse. Armies are hostages to a chess game they cannot see. *(The void war is its own file, and its own nightmare.)*
- **The valve jams.** If either side actually won Halvard's World, the pressure it absorbs would have to go somewhere. Both staffs know this. Cynics in both staffs note the curious durability of certain stalemates, and are careful where they note it.
- **The Vatborn variable.** The Imperial half of the math prices a Vatborn at the cost of decanting him. Anything that reprices him — a batch that refuses, a religious ruling on Vatborn souls, a Union policy that turns prisoner batches into citizen-soldiers at scale — moves the ceiling in a direction no staff study has modeled honestly, because modeling it honestly means writing down what a Vatborn is, and nobody's forms have a field for that.

The wall has held for fifty-two years because everyone leans on it. No one has ever tested a wall that way and enjoyed the result.

## Open questions

- Both staffs keep their Halvard's World withdrawal studies classified from their own governments. What do the studies conclude — and given how the powers talk unofficially, has each side already read the other's?
- Who or what walks the wire as Old Halvard — grief, a psychological operation, or both sides running the same operation on each other without either knowing?
- What happened to the Fortieth Legion — and why did the Artificer Guilds survey the Greywater once, seal the survey, and never go back?
- Where do Aztlan's missing go, and why has the Bestiary "declined to speculate" through eleven consecutive annual revisions — is the silence ignorance, or policy?
- What did the honors of Corvant cost Lord Benkei Ashiwara at the Imperial court — and why does House Yamatai's own chronicle strike the episode it should be proudest of?
- Both quartermasters' ledgers show the Quiet Floor being provisioned for nineteen months. Neither ledger shows an order. Who signed nothing, twice?

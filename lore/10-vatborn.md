# The Vatborn
*Assembled entirely from outside. No archive in either empire records what a batchman believes walking into the guns, and this file does not pretend to be the first to fail at it.*

---

## 1. No One Holds the Whole File

Ask the Imperium what a Vatborn is and you get the College of Remembrancers' silence, sealed two centuries deep. Ask the Union and you get a filing problem: the Directorate of Colonial and Transitional Administration, the Directorate of Intelligence and Survey, and the Directorate of Justice and Charter each keep a partial record, in different formats, under different legal theories, and none of the three has ever formally reconciled its numbers with the other two. Ask a house quartermaster and you get a muster roll, current only until the next casualty report. Ask the Guilds, who alone run the crèches and alone could actually answer, and you get an enclave gate, closed the way it is always closed.

What follows is what remains when none of those offices will show their whole ledger: field interrogation transcripts, Remembrancer chaplains' reports from the line, PALT census annexes trying to count a population no law anticipated, muster and pay ledgers that changed hands as war booty or prize-court evidence, folklorists' transcriptions of songs nobody taught anybody, and the UGF's own *Hostile Forces Estimate 7* annexes on Vatborn formations. These documents were never meant to sit next to each other. They contradict on names, numbers, dates, and the exact wording of the same song sung on the same night by soldiers who were, by every account, standing close enough to hear each other clearly.

The one figure everyone quietly uses and nobody will formally defend is DIS's rough estimate that something like one soldier in five under Imperial colors was decanted rather than born — a number derived not from any house's own audit (no house has one; the crèches are Guild property, and Guild property is not audited by tenants) but from years of comparing muster intercepts, captured pay ledgers, and the simple fact that Vatborn units, once you know what to look for in the paperwork, look different on paper before they ever look different in the field. DIS revises the estimate every few years. It has never moved by more than a point in either direction, which the analysts who maintain it find, they will tell you privately, more unsettling than reassuring — a number that stable ought to be coming from somewhere with better bookkeeping than a guess.

This file is that guess, extended.

## 2. Decanting, As Far As the Records Go

The Guilds' own crèche registers have never been read by anyone outside the Guilds. What survives are the records that changed hands — captured supply manifests, prize-court exhibits, the paperwork a house keeps once a batch is delivered into its custody, which is to say: everything downstream of the part nobody will discuss.

> *"Decant date entered per crèche schedule, not independently verified. Tender assigned: employee code G-1141. Naming rite conducted, batch strength at rite two hundred and six. Muster-ready declared, batch strength at muster one hundred ninety-one. Deficit noted, not explained. No further entry required by this office."*
> — Crèche-adjacent supply ledger, House Chandragupt provincial archive, entered as an exhibit in a Union prize-court hearing on a captured tender's cargo manifest, war year 34.

Fifteen soldiers, gone between a naming rite and a first muster, in a document that does not consider the gap worth a sentence. This is the characteristic gesture of every crèche-adjacent record this file has been able to locate: administrative precision about the milestones — decant date, naming rite, muster-ready — and total silence about everything between them. What actually happens to two hundred children in the years the ledger compresses into a single line is not recorded anywhere a house, a chaplain, or a UGF prize court has ever been allowed to look. The Imperium calls this discretion. It has the same shape as a secret, and functions identically.

The one figure who appears in nearly every batch's early paperwork and then vanishes from it entirely is the **tender** — a single Guild-assigned caretaker, identified in house ledgers by employee code alone, who by all surviving accounts raises a batch through its accelerated childhood and is never named again once the batch reaches muster-ready. Houses do not record what becomes of a tender afterward; it is, by long practice, not a house's business. What the ledgers erase, the songs sometimes keep — see below.

## 3. Three Names for Every Soldier

Every disagreement in this file about a given Vatborn's *name* — and there are dozens — comes from the same cause: there are three of them, kept by three different institutions, none of which defers to the others.

| Layer | Assigned by | Typical form | Who actually uses it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Decant-code** | The Guild crèche register | `OSM/C-3/1112/B-204` — house, crèche number, year A.M., batch letter, sequence | Guild ledgers, prize courts, and nobody who has ever met the man |
| **Call-name** | The naming rite, drawn from a fixed Guild list | *Anchor, Bright, Latch, Ember, Second Watch* | House muster rolls, Remembrancer field reports, official correspondence |
| **Earned name** | The batch itself, within days of first muster | *Old Anchor, Tuesday, Nine-Finger, Long Walk* | The batch. Only the batch. Until the batch is gone. |

The call-names come from a Guild namebook the College's own clerks call, with weary affection, **the Common Roster** — a list of some four hundred words in garbled Concordant, unrevised in longer than any living Remembrancer can date, and reused without end across every house and every crèche in the Imperium. It is not unusual for three unrelated soldiers named *Ember* to be serving on three different fronts in the same season, known to their own units by entirely different names, indistinguishable to a clerk cross-referencing muster rolls, and functionally strangers to one another in every sense but the one line of paperwork that makes them look identical. Whether the Guilds have simply never bothered to lengthen the list, or find the interchangeability useful, is a question no house has ever had standing to ask.

The earned name is the only one of the three that means anything, and it is the only one that is never officially recorded, never survives a batch's death, and appears in the archive only by accident — a slip in an interrogation transcript, a Remembrancer's private notation, a UGF chaplain's field diary. This file quotes several. There is no complete list anywhere, of anyone, and there never will be.

## 4. The Law, Unharmonized

The Imperium's civil status for the Vatborn was never legislated once, from the center, for the whole empire. It accreted, house by house, into a patchwork the Great Council has never proposed reconciling — for the same reason it has never proposed a standard rifle bore: reconciliation would mean somebody's version winning.

| House | Vatborn status in house law | Working detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Normand** | Chattel of the marcher estate | Transferable with the land title itself; a Vatborn legion can be inherited, mortgaged, or divided in a marcher lord's will exactly like a herd |
| **Rurikov** | Bound to the levy rolls, undistinguished | No formal legal difference from an indentured hearth-born peasant conscript; the same polk levies both without separate paperwork |
| **Chandragupt** | An audited class | Individually tracked, deficits filed rather than mourned, and — uniquely — a published manumission price a batchman or his kin may pay to clear |
| **Osmanli** | Hearth-subject, promotable | The one house whose Vatborn rise through the same ranks as the freeborn, by the same merit boards |
| **Yamatai** | Almost never fielded | The house keeps no mass levy of any kind; the rare Vatborn taken into a Yashima banner is absorbed, quietly, as an adopted retainer, and the house's own genealogists have never been asked to explain the adoption rite in writing |
| **Aztlan** | Unpublished | Not written down anywhere the Council, the College, or this office has ever been shown — consistent with the house's general silence on everything downstream of a battle |

**Hearth-subject** is the term most houses actually use in daily law — a status between property and citizen, owing service, entitled to shelter, unentitled to a genealogy of one's own unless elevated. What it does not resolve, anywhere, is the theological question sitting underneath all of it: whether a decanted man has ancestors, and therefore whether he has a soul with anywhere to go. The College of Remembrancers drafted its rulings on this two hundred years ago and has kept them sealed ever since, a fact this file will not attempt to improve on. The one place the sealed question gets a quiet, unofficial answer in ink rather than doctrine belongs to a different institution's file, not this one.

What Remembrancer chaplains attached to legions do in the meantime, on the ground, without waiting for Elysium, is discussed next.

## 5. Pour and Grave

Batch-culture has its own kinship vocabulary, none of it licensed by any house, all of it in universal informal use.

A cohort decanted together is a **pour** — "first pour" for a batch's founding members, and the word has migrated, the way words do, into a general term for one's closest people: *my pour* can mean the six soldiers you trained with or, loosened further, anyone who would carry you. Batchmates address each other as **kin**, occasionally sharpened to **kin-drawn** when the speaker wants to distinguish it, pointedly, from a house's hearth-kin. A replacement posted in from an unrelated batch to fill losses is a **graft** — tolerated, fed, armed, and not fully trusted until "he's taken root," a phrase every UGF liaison who has debriefed a Vatborn prisoner has heard at least once without quite being able to translate the weight of it.

Funerary practice, as observed — never as explained, since no batchman has ever explained it to an interrogator who asked directly — centers on two customs recorded independently by multiple sources on both sides of the wire.

The first is **the Roll**: at a batch burial, the names of the batch's dead are recited aloud, in the order they died, not the order they were decanted. A Remembrancer field report from the Long Front describes it this way:

> *"The rite has no sanction from this office and I have not attempted to stop it. They stand the newly dead beside the older graves and one of them speaks names — call-names, mostly, though I have twice heard names I do not recognize from any muster roll, which I take to be the other kind. No prayer is offered to any ancestor. When asked, the soldier conducting the rite told me it was 'just the count.' I have filed this as an unlicensed devotional practice, tolerated pending guidance. Guidance has not arrived in eleven years of my asking."*
> — Field report, unnamed Remembrancer attached to a Mutapa-sworn legion, Halvard's World, filed to the College, marked *pending*

A UGF liaison officer, watching the identical practice from the opposite trench line during a stretcher truce, filed it as "superstitious roll call, morale-related, non-hostile." Same rite, same night in all likelihood, two institutions, two vocabularies — and the one officer who asked was given the word, wrote down "just the count," and filed it under a heading of his own invention. Neither archive uses the soldiers' name for it. One of them was told it.

The second is **planting**: a cutting or a handful of seed set into freshly turned earth over a batch grave, wherever the ground will take it. On worlds where nothing grows, soldiers on both sides report the gesture performed anyway, with whatever is available — a splinter of wood, a green rag. Halvard's World, where the custom of a blade of grass in the helmet band is already universal, has made this indistinguishable from local superstition; on fronts with no folk custom of their own to borrow, the Vatborn practice stands out sharply, unexplained, and is the detail UGF veterans most often mention when asked what unsettled them most about facing a Vatborn unit that had just taken losses. Not the attack. The gardening, afterward, under fire, before the position was even secured.

## 6. What They Sing

**"Green Again"** — the Halvard's World planting song, sung by every army that has ever held that front — is, in Vatborn mouths, not the same song. Levies sing it as a hymn; the UGF sings barrack-room verses; and the Vatborn sing verses no crèche curriculum, no muster-roll songbook, and no chaplain of any house has ever admitted teaching them. Two transcriptions, from two institutions that have never compared notes:

> *"...counted twice and never lost, / the seed knows the frost it cost..."*
> — College of Remembrancers, transcribed from an Osmanli legion's evening muster, marked *liturgically significant, provenance unconfirmed*

> *"...twice-counted, never lost — / the green don't care what the digging cost..."*
> — UGF chaplain's field notebook, Wet Flank sector, transcribed independently the same war year

The College reads "counted twice" as doctrine in embryo — a claim, however folk in origin, that the dead are numbered somewhere. The UGF chaplain who wrote the second version down noted only that it was "the verse that made the company stop talking," and moved on to the next entry in his book. Neither institution has ever compared its transcription to the other's. This file is, as far as its author can determine, the first document to place them side by side.

The other song this file can canonize is **"the Counting,"** taught in the crèche years — the one piece of batch-culture with a documented educational function, since it doubles as a formation drill: children chant numbers while forming and re-forming ranks, learning to hold a line before they are old enough to be told why. A fragment, preserved (again) only by accident, in a captured training aid recovered from a Rurikov crèche-adjacent depot:

> *"One for the pour, and one to grow, / one for the ones who don't come home..."*

No verse of the Counting has ever been recovered complete. What is notable is the word *home* appearing in a rhyme taught to children who, by every legal and theological account this Imperium has produced, have none. Whether the word survives from the tender's own vocabulary — the one adult a batch actually has, however briefly, however unrecorded — or from somewhere else entirely, nobody who could answer has ever been asked in a way that produced an answer.

## 7. The Paperwork of an Unresolved Soul

Whether a captured Vatborn counts as a prisoner of war is not a philosophical question inside the UGF. It is a form that had to be invented because no existing one fit, and its invention is the clearest surviving evidence of how badly the question sits with the people obligated to process it.

**DIS Form 12-V — "Field Interrogation: Enemy Decanted Personnel"** — exists precisely because the standard prisoner intake form assumes a combatant with a state, a home address, and next of kin, and a decanted soldier has, in the form's own dry phrasing, "no box for the first two and a contested claim on the third." Two transcripts, chosen from many that disagree with each other in exactly this way:

> *"Q: State your name for the record. A: [Given as call-name, per muster intercept.] Q: Full name and place of registry. A: [No response.] Note: subject repeated call-name three times when pressed, appeared confused by the request rather than evasive. Marked non-hostile, non-compliant with Section 3."*
> — DIS Form 12-V, Halvard's World intake, war year 39

> *"Q: State your name for the record. A: [A name not matching any known call-name convention or muster roll for the identified unit.] Note: subject volunteered this name without prompting and would not be moved from it under three separate lines of questioning. Cross-reference to unit muster roll returns negative. Recommend: do not press further; subject grows agitated when the 'other' name is used."*
> — DIS Form 12-V, Wet Flank intake, war year 45

The first prisoner gave the institutional name — the interrogator's own note reads confused, not evasive, as though it were the only name the question could possibly be about. The second gave the earned name, because to him it was evidently the only real one, and no amount of DIS's institutional patience could locate it on any roll DIS had access to — because it was never on a roll to begin with. Both transcripts are marked, in different analysts' handwriting, with the same word in the margin. That word is discussed below, once its full pattern can be shown.

## 8. Populations No One Planned

### PALT's Ledger With No Box

The Provisional Authority for Liberated Territories inherits, on every world it administers, an unplanned population problem the Directorate of Colonial and Transitional Administration's own forms were not written for: not only surrendered or captured Vatborn soldiers, but generations of villagers descended from vat-seeded settlement stock, some of whom claim it, most of whom have never had reason to ask, and none of whom could prove it either way, or fit any residency category on the standard intake schedule.

> *"Category 4 (Vatborn, formerly under arms): 340. Category 4A (Vatborn, non-combatant, crèche-documented): 12. Category [handwritten, no number assigned] ('villages, undetermined origin, batch-descended per local rumor, no documentary basis for classification'): population uncertain, estimate withdrawn pending further study. Estimate has been pending further study for six census cycles."*
> — PALT Residency Determination schedule, liberated world file, DCTA archive extract

The margin note — "no documentary basis for classification" — is doing enormous work for six words. A world's own inhabitants may not know which of their grandparents began in a vat; PALT's clerks certainly do not, and the schedule's design assumes they will find out eventually, which they have not, six census cycles running. The category with no number assigned is, on some liberated worlds, the largest one on the form.

### Osmanli, From the Inside

Set against every other house's version of this story is Osmanli's, which the Bestiary's Vatborn annex treats, with visible professional discomfort, as the theater's genuine anomaly:

> *"Osmanli Vatborn serve long, promote on documented merit, and — this is the detail that unsettles line officers more than any tactic — exercise independent initiative without apparent institutional anxiety about doing so. They are not, functionally, a tide. They are professionals who happen to have been decanted rather than born, and Osmanli's own quartermaster ledgers treat the distinction as beneath comment."*
> — *Hostile Forces Estimate 7*, Annex V ("Vatborn Formations")

One illustrative service record, recovered from a captured Osmanli pay ledger and cross-referenced against a Bestiary prisoner interview: decant-code **OSM/C-3/1112/B-204**; call-name, per the Common Roster, **Anchor**; and by the time the ledger was captured, three decades into Osmanli service, referred to by every entry after the first few years — by his own officers, in official correspondence, without apparent self-consciousness — as **Old Anchor**. An earned name, promoted, by long use, into the institutional record that is supposed to belong only to the call-name. Nowhere else in this file's sources does that migration happen. Osmanli is, on the evidence, the one house where the batch's own name for a man occasionally outlives the paperwork's insistence on its own.

### Halvard's Own

On Halvard's World, where units rotate by court politics and Rurikov never rotates out at all, at least one Vatborn legion has held the same stretch of the Long Front long enough that its own soldiers have stopped using its house-issued number and call it, among themselves, **the Loam Legion** — a name that appears nowhere in any Imperial order of battle, and everywhere in the informal record of everyone who has fought beside or against it for more than a season. Replacement batches arriving to top up the Loam Legion's losses inherit, along with a graft's usual probation, an existing set of graves to tend — batches before them, tended by batches before that. Nobody currently serving remembers whose decision it originally was that this duty would pass down. The graves are tended regardless.

### The Number Nobody Files

Bestiary analysts, cross-referencing capture rates, casualty ratios, and captured muster returns across a dozen fronts and three decades, have never located a category for Vatborn desertion — and have eventually concluded this is not a gap in what they've been able to collect, but a gap in what was ever recorded:

> *"Desertion, Vatborn formations: no comparable metric exists in any captured muster document reviewed for this annex. Absence may reflect (a) genuine near-zero incidence, (b) house reporting conventions that fold the missing into casualty or 'unaccounted' returns without distinction, or (c) both. Analysts are not agreed which."*
> — *Hostile Forces Estimate 7*, Annex V, marginal note

Whichever it is, the number does not exist on paper, in a war otherwise audited to the sundry ledger and the marker stone. A house that tracks cloth by the length has never once, on the evidence available to this file, tracked a batchman walking away.

## 9. What Pay Buys

A Vatborn draws pay, house-set and modest, and what the surviving quartermaster ledgers show him spending it on is, in aggregate, a more honest self-portrait than any interrogation transcript.

> *"Sundries, Legion account, week ending [date redacted]: cloth, undyed, 40 lengths. Song-sheets, printed (unofficial vendor, tolerated), 6. Seed, mixed, agricultural surplus, 1 sack. Marker stones, quarried, 9. Contribution to battalion manumission fund, forwarded per standing request, sum as noted."*
> — Legion sundries ledger, house unspecified, captured supply record, war year 41

The **batch chest** — a pooled, informal fund, unrecognized in any house's official accounting but visible in nearly every captured ledger this file has reviewed as a recurring line item under some euphemism ("contribution," "forwarded per standing request," "battalion fund") — is where Vatborn pay disproportionately goes: toward a comrade's Chandragupt-style manumission price where the law allows one to be bought; toward proper stone markers where the house issues none; toward seed, cloth, and unofficial song-sheets where nothing else on the ledger explains why a legion at the front needs cloth undyed by the bolt. No house quartermaster has ever formally acknowledged the batch chest's existence. Every one of them has, evidently, learned to stop asking what the cloth is for.

## 10. The One Constant

Across every DIS Form 12-V this file's sources could locate — different houses, different decades, different fronts, different interrogating officers who have never met each other and could not have coordinated an answer — one question produces an answer with a consistency nothing else on the form comes close to matching. The question, in its various phrasings, amounts to: *what will you do when this is over.*

The overwhelming majority of recorded answers, across the entire span of the war, use some form of the word *home*, or *go back*. This is worth sitting with for exactly as long as it takes to notice the problem: a Vatborn, by every legal and theological account this Imperium has produced, has no home to go back to. He was made for the front he is standing on. And prisoner after prisoner, house after house, decade after decade, answers the question as though the sentence makes perfect sense.

DIS analysts, independently, across a documented span of at least thirty years, and without any evidence any of them ever read another's transcript before filing their own, have annotated this answer in the margin with the identical two words: **"referent unclear."**

Nobody at DIS coordinated that phrase. Nobody could have; the transcripts sit in different files, filed by different officers, years and fronts apart, and DIS has no institutional practice of circulating interrogation marginalia for style consistency. It is, on the evidence, simply the phrase that the same question produces in the same institution's vocabulary, independently, every time — which either means nothing at all, or means that whatever the Vatborn mean by *home* has been sitting in the same unmarked place for thirty years, waiting for someone with the standing to ask a follow-up question nobody has yet thought to write on the form.

## 11. The Fortieth Legion

The mosaic's last artifact is also its most complete, because the record simply stops.

The Fortieth Legion — ten thousand long-service Vatborn under Osmanli colors, among the finest the crèches are known to have produced — held a sector of the estuary front on the tide-world the Union charts call Vasseur for six years before the events of war year 44, the same front the Anders Line campaign had already broken open three years earlier. Its quartermaster ledger, recovered afterward from a rear depot and never formally closed, records nothing unusual in its final entries: cloth, seed, song-sheets, nine marker stones ordered the week before, a batch-chest contribution forwarded per standing request, same as any other week in six years of them. Nothing in the paperwork anticipates anything. That is what makes it the paperwork of a legion that did not know either.

The Fortieth advanced into the estuary fog and did not come out. No bodies, no wreckage, no distress traffic — the Guilds surveyed the ground once, the following year, and sealed the survey, and no explanation has ever crossed into any record this file has been able to reach. What has crossed, reliably, on both sides of the wire, every season the fog runs high, are marching songs on dead frequencies — always the legion's own songs, always slightly wrong, in the specific way this file has now spent ten sections documenting every Vatborn song is *always* slightly wrong, depending on who is doing the transcribing and what they were taught to hear.

The quartermaster's ledger for the Fortieth Legion has never been formally closed. Eight years on, whoever holds the authority to close it has not done so, and — six census cycles of PALT paperwork later, thirty years of "referent unclear" later, one sealed College ruling and one sealed Guild survey later — does not appear, on the evidence available to this file, to be looking.

## Open questions

- The Common Roster has stayed roughly four hundred words deep for longer than any Remembrancer can date. Has anyone with Guild access ever asked why it has never been lengthened — and would a longer list even be technically possible, or is four hundred a limit rather than a habit?
- Tenders vanish from every record the moment a batch reaches muster-ready. Are they reassigned to new batches, retired, or is "employee code" doing work that a name would make harder to erase?
- Chandragupt publishes a manumission price for its Vatborn. Has this file's author — or anyone — located a single documented case of a batchman actually bought free, and what happened to him afterward?
- Two independent transcriptions of "Green Again" agree on the count and on nothing after it — and the College reads doctrine into word order alone. Has any Remembrancer ever tried to establish which version is older, or is the argument itself the point?
- The batch chest appears, disguised, in captured ledgers from at least four houses. Has any house quartermaster ever formally investigated where the money actually goes, or is not asking the arrangement everyone involved prefers?
- If "referent unclear" has been written, independently, by DIS analysts for thirty years without any of them comparing notes, has DIS's own leadership ever noticed the pattern in its own archive — and if it has, why has no one published it?
- The Loam Legion's grave-tending duty passes down through replacement batches with no one able to say who started it. Is there a first grave, and does anything in any surviving record — Guild, house, or UGF — mark who is in it?
- PALT's undetermined-origin category is, on some worlds, the largest population on the residency schedule. What happens the day a liberated world's own government decides to answer the question PALT has left blank for six census cycles — and who would want that answer public first?

# The Union of Free Systems
*The freest society in human space, as certified by its own Directorate of Public Information.*

---

## 1. Structure & Civic Life

**Citizenship must be earned through service.** This is the Union's founding sentence, carved over the Assembly doors on New Geneva, printed on the currency, recited by schoolchildren who will spend their adult lives deciding what it costs them. It produces two civic tiers: **Citizens**, who hold full political rights, and **Residents** — comfortable, prosperous, and disenfranchised.

Everything else about the Union follows from those two words and the gap between them.

The institutional language is deliberately clean: directorates, acronyms, plain words. Where the Imperium gilds, the Union laminates. A thing in the Union is named for what it does, by a clerk, and the name sticks for three hundred years.

The capital world is **New Geneva** — an idealistic founding name, now the seat of a war bureaucracy. (The Imperium refuses the name in official documents, using its old Concord-era catalog designation instead.) The city itself is genuinely lovely: lake districts, arcology terraces, the white **Rotunda** where the Assembly sits and the low gray slab of **Charter House** where the Directory does. Visitors always remark on the light. The city is very beautiful. The budget is mostly war.

### The two tiers

A Resident's life is good. Every Resident receives **the Provision** — the universal service basket: a housing lease, medical care, transit, terminal access, education through accreditation age. Residents work, own businesses, get rich, get famous. They pay the **Charter Levy** at the same rate Citizens do. What they cannot do is vote, hold office, sit on a jury, own land outright, or hold a broadcast or courier license. A Resident leases; a Citizen holds freehold. A Resident's dispute goes to an arbitration panel; a Citizen's goes to a jury — and the arbitration panels are staffed by Citizens too. A Resident may petition the Assembly, but every petition needs a Citizen sponsor. *Every petition needs a pin.*

Crucially — and this is the Union's whole theory of itself — **citizenship is not hereditary.** A Citizen's children are born Residents and must earn the tier themselves. The First Director's own daughter, **Ilsabet Voss**, has never served and remains a Resident; the pamphlets on all three sides of Union politics use this fact, for opposite purposes. This is the system working, say the pamphlets. They do not agree on what the system is.

Roughly one adult in six is a Citizen. The Union will give a Resident everything except a say.

### Accredited service

Citizenship is earned by completing a term of **accredited service** in one of four routes recognized in law, certified by the Directorate of Accreditation and Civic Records:

| Route | Term | In practice |
|---|---|---|
| **Military** | Six years | The door that is actually open. Walk in at eighteen, walk out at twenty-four with the franchise — if you walk out. |
| **Colonial** | Ten years | Postings are capped, competitive, and mysteriously full of Citizens' children. The waiting list is measured in years. |
| **Hazardous-industrial** | Twelve years | Only work in DACR-rated Hazard Classes counts, and the rating boards have spent two generations rating hazards downward. The classes shrink; the factories don't. |
| **Medical** | Eight years, two forward | Requires credentials most Residents cannot afford to acquire; the route effectively begins with a Citizen-sponsored scholarship. |

In peacetime the four routes were a genuine spread. Fifty-two years into the war, accredited service means the military in the same way that weather means rain. The recruiting office is the one civic door guaranteed to be unlocked, and it opens onto the Reach. This is not a flaw the Union's critics discovered; every Resident knows it by age twelve, and it is the live wire under all Union politics.

The law offers four doors. The war keeps three of them shut.

### The pin

Citizens wear the **Charter pin** — a small sunburst-and-star, four grams of alloy, worn at the collar. It is not required. It is universal. It is the first thing anyone in the Union reads on anyone else, the way an Imperial reads heraldry. In a courtroom, a land office, a zoning hearing, a school career fair, the pins cluster at the front of the room and the decisions follow the cluster.

There is one exception, and it is political: **the Unpinned** — Citizens, many of them veterans, who refuse to wear the pin until everyone can vote. Going unpinned is legal, conspicuous, and infuriates almost everyone, which is the point.

The pin weighs four grams. Ask a Resident what it weighs.

## 2. Government

### The Directory

Executive power sits with **the Directory**: eleven Directors-General, one per directorate, confirmed by the Citizens' Assembly to five-year terms — plus a twelfth chair for the **First Director**, whom the Directory elects from among its own sitting members. On election, a First Director surrenders their portfolio. Ties in the Directory are broken by the First Director, which is to say there are no ties.

The current First Director is **Halima Voss**, sixty-eight, in her third term. She came up through the Directorate of Industrial Mobilization — twenty-six years, ending as its Director-General — and it shows: she thinks in throughput, speaks in schedules, and has never in her public life used the word "victory" without a qualifier. She is a coalition-broker of genius, and she is hated by the hawks and the reformers alike for exactly the same reason: **she keeps the war exactly the size it is.**

Her critics call this cowardice, cynicism, or drift, depending on the critic. Her defenders — fewer, quieter, better informed — note that the war's size is the one thing in human space that has stayed fixed for a generation, and wonder aloud who they think has been holding it.

### The directorates

Eleven directorates, eleven acronyms, assigned by different generations of clerks and answering to no common scheme. This is the closest thing the Union has to heraldry.

| Directorate | Acronym | Character |
|---|---|---|
| Directorate of War | **DIRWAR** | Strategy and procurement; the largest budget in human history, audited quarterly. Speaks of oceans of blood in fiscal quarters. |
| Directorate of Intelligence and Survey | **DIS** | Spies and cartographers in one building, because in this war sensor coverage *is* intelligence. The people who decide where the war is. Its Director-General is by long custom referred to only as **the Surveyor**. |
| Directorate of Colonial and Transitional Administration | **DCTA** | Settlement, development, and oversight of the provisional authority that administers liberated worlds (see the client-states file). The crusade's landlord. |
| Directorate of Public Information | **DPI** | Does not censor; it *accredits*. Owns the liberation-documentary pipeline and, more importantly, the courier manifests. |
| Directorate of Industrial Mobilization | **INDMOB** | The arsenal. Voss's old chair, now held by her protégé; runs on schedules and hates surprises. |
| Directorate of Accreditation and Civic Records | **DACR** | Keeps the ledger of service — who has earned what, and what counts as earning. The smallest public profile and the most feared building on New Geneva. It decides what counts. |
| Directorate of Finance and Treasury | **DFT** | Sells the bonds, prices the war, tells the Directory what it can afford. The war's actual general. |
| Directorate of Health and Repatriation | **DHR** | Hospital ships, prosthetics, reintegration. Where the war comes home first. |
| Directorate of Justice and Charter | **DJC** | Courts and Charter law. The smallest budget and the longest memory. |
| Directorate of Transit and Lanes | **DTL** | Shipping registry, convoy scheduling, the civilian half of logistics (much of it contracted out to Partner Systems). Fights the war with timetables. |
| Directorate of Research and Standards | **DRS** | Weights, measures, patents — and the **Deferred Systems Annex**, the drawer where the Union keeps what it will not yet build. |

Nobody has ever accused the Union of poetry.

### The Citizens' Assembly

The legislature: **nine hundred seats**, elected by the Citizen rolls, apportioned across worlds by Citizen population. Read that sentence again slowly, because it contains a machine: Citizen population concentrates where service concentrates — garrison worlds, veteran colonies, the naval-yard systems — so the electorate that oversees the war is disproportionately made of people the war produced. The Assembly does not merely fund the war. In a real demographic sense, the war elects the Assembly.

The Assembly confirms Directors-General, passes budgets, declares (and could in principle end) the war, and reads the names of the newly accredited into the record each session — including the posthumous ones. Residents killed in accredited service are enrolled as Citizens by **posthumous accreditation**: the franchise, conferred on the dead, heritable by no one, exercisable never. Their names are read aloud with all the others. The frank ones in the gallery note what this says about the going price of a vote.

The Union enfranchises its dead. It is the only route with no waiting list.

## 3. The Braid

Union politics is a three-way argument about what the war is for, conducted by three movements that despise each other and cannot survive without each other. Insiders call it **the braid**. Voss has never used the word in public. She did, according to three separate memoirs, coin it.

### The Full Franchise movement

**What it wants:** the vote for every adult, service or no service. Its bill — the **Whole House Amendment** — has been introduced in the Assembly every session for eleven years and has grown from a gesture into a bloc.

**Who leads it:** Assemblywoman **Coretta Nwosu-Lindh**, a medical-route Citizen who earned her pin as a triage surgeon on a hospital ship and wears it anyway — "so they have to look at where it came from." Her stock line is the movement's best argument in nine words: *"I earned my vote. That is the problem."*

**Its best argument:** a republic where the franchise runs through the recruiting office is not a republic; it is an army with elections. The Residents are asked to fund, staff, and mourn a war they cannot vote on — accreditation without representation.

**Its ugliest edge:** the movement cannot quite disown its arithmetic. A full franchise means the Resident majority — the people who never served — could vote to abandon the people who did, and with them the actual serfs of the actual Imperium. Its radical wing organizes **rate strikes** in war industries and has twice been caught corrupting DACR service records, and some of its pamphleteers have begun to say *the liberation was never ours* in a tone the movement's enemies record and replay.

### The Resolution Bloc

**What it wants:** to end the war by winning it — to break the limited-war arithmetic on purpose. Its platform, refined over twenty years from a fringe position into the second force in the Assembly: deep strikes on the Imperial core's yards and crèche-worlds, autonomous weapons released from human veto, and — never in the platform, always in the room — a Union answer to the crèches themselves. Its members are called **Resolutionists**. Their slogan is a masterpiece of Union plainness: **"One more decade is ten more decades."**

**Who leads it:** **Corin Adeyemi-Strand**, retired FLEETCOM flag officer — and, in Voss's most quoted appointment, the sitting Director-General of War. Voss elevated the hawks' leader to the one chair where wanting more war means owning its costs, and he has spent six years discovering what the chair does to the wanting. He is grayer now. The Bloc's younger members have started to say so.

**Its best argument:** the arithmetic is not stable, merely slow. Every year of limited war teaches the Imperium how to win a limited war; the crèches mean time is a weapon that only works for the enemy. The Union cannot attrit an opponent that manufactures people — so the only moral end is a fast one, and every year of Voss's equilibrium is a subscription payment on other people's children. *A limited war is a subscription. We propose to buy.*

**Its ugliest edge:** what buying costs. Deep strikes on crèche-worlds mean killing the Vatborn in the vat — bombing, as one DJC memorandum put it before it was classified, *the only munitions plant that screams.* And the machines and the batches are discussed below, under the Union's soul, where they belong.

### The Quiet Border

**What it wants:** a negotiated freeze. Fix the line where it stands, fortify it, trade across it where profitable, and let the Imperium rot on its own thousand-year timescale. The movement grew out of a book — ***A Decent Distance***, by **Yusuf Grieg-Andrade**, a former senior colonial administrator who spent nineteen years on liberated worlds and came home to write four hundred pages of quiet, documented despair about what liberation looks like from underneath. Its adherents are called **Quietists**, a name coined by their enemies that they have adopted with a shrug.

**Its best argument:** the liberation isn't liberating. Deprogramming an ancestor cult takes generations; every freed world is a decades-long occupation run by a provisional authority that is forever "transitioning to self-rule"; the Union is spending its young to annex worlds that did not ask. Freeze the line and win the long way — by being, visibly, across a quiet border, the thing worth defecting to. *Let the Imperium besiege a better idea.*

**Its ugliest edge:** the freeze writes off the serfs — hundreds of worlds of hereditary bondage, reclassified as sunk cost by people who will never meet them. And beneath the war-weariness there is a colder current the movement prefers not to audit: a frozen war freezes the franchise ladder. Some of the Quiet Border's most generous funders are establishment Citizens who like the electorate exactly its current size. Peace, for them, is a gated community.

### How Voss rules

She quotes each of them to the others, accurately, at the worst possible moment.

To the Resolutionists she is the only thing standing between them and a home front that the Quietists prove is already tired — escalate, and the bond drives don't underperform, they *fail*. To the Quietists she is the only thing standing between them and Adeyemi-Strand — freeze the war on her watch and the next government is his. To the Full Franchise she is the drip: every year, a few more accredited categories, a few thousand more colonial and medical slots, reform at a rate calibrated to be real enough to point to and slow enough to change nothing structural. Each movement believes it is one Voss-failure away from power. Each therefore props her up against the other two, and calls it patience.

And beneath the brokering there is the fact none of the three movements can say aloud, because each is differently indicted by it: the war, at exactly its current size, is the Union's only functioning engine of mass enfranchisement. Victory closes the factory. A freeze closes the factory. Full franchise makes the factory meaningless. Only the grinding middle keeps minting Citizens at scale — tens of thousands a year, six years at a time, plus the posthumous rolls.

Voss has never said this aloud. She has never needed to.

## 4. Daily Life

A prosperous consumer society funding a distant war. Most people in the Union — Citizen and Resident alike — have never seen the Reach and never will: it arrives as war bonds, patriotic media, liberation documentaries, and veterans who come home strange.

There is no faster-than-light communication, so the war arrives by ship. The DPI courier packets reach New Geneva on a standing schedule, and the casualty notifications travel in them, hand-carried the last mile by DHR officers in gray. On the capital the packets land Thursdays; every world knows its own day. Families of the deployed learn the convoy schedules the way farmers learn weather. The war is always weeks old by the time anyone sees it. Nobody in the Union has ever watched it live.

The war arrives on Thursdays. Grief keeps office hours.

### The war on a screen

On every major platform the Reach is a streaming category — officially "Current Affairs: The Reach," universally just **Frontline**. Its grammar is fixed:

- **Liberation documentaries**, the prestige tier. The flagship is ***First Light***, thirty-one seasons: each episode ends on a freed world's first free sunrise, scored, drone-shot, devastating. The genre's open secret is that the sunrises are frequently reshot months later, once the world is pacified enough to film. The sunrise is real. The *first* is editorial.
- **Raw-feeds** — helmet-camera compilations, gray-market, technically unaccredited, enormously popular. The single most-streamed item in the category last year was eleven hours of rain on a tarp at a firebase, no commentary. Four hundred million streams. Nobody at DPI can explain it. Every veteran can.
- **Feedjocks** — commentators who narrate week-old combat footage live, with the urgency of sport. Since all footage is weeks old, liveness is a genre convention, not a fact. The audience knows. The audience does not care.

DPI's control is elegant because it is physical. It does not censor; it **accredits** — and accreditation means priority on the courier manifests. Unaccredited reporting is perfectly legal and arrives three months late, which in a news economy is the same as never. Whoever schedules the couriers edits reality. Censorship by freight.

### Bond drives

The war is financed by the **Liberation Loans**, numbered series sold with festivals, celebrity ambassadors, and school competitions. For fifty years they oversubscribed. The **Ninth Liberation Loan** was the first to fall short; DFT called it a "reallocation of household patriotism" and DPI made the phrase stick for almost a month. The Tenth was renamed the Victory Loan. It sold worse.

Recruitment has gone soft the same way — targets met, but later each year, with better bonuses. Nothing is failing. Everything is slower. The Directory reads these numbers the way DIS reads sensor ghosts: as the shape of something large, still distant, moving.

### The Returned

Veterans come home through **Repatriation Processing** — a mandatory decompression interval at the transit stations, weeks of medical checks, debriefs, and "civic reorientation." Every soldier in the Union calls it **the Airlock**, because that is what it is for: you do not open the inner door until the pressure matches.

It never quite matches. The Returned are easy to spot if you know the tells, and everyone in the Union now knows the tells: they sit facing exits; they count things — rounds of applause, streetlights, days; they go silent at fireworks and flinch at nothing at all, which is worse. Resident veterans come home *as Citizens* — the franchise is conferred at demobilization, so the Union's newest voters are twenty-four-year-olds voting for the first time with a war in their hands. Veterans are consequently the braid's shared currency: the Full Franchise runs them for the Assembly, the Resolutionists put them on stages, the Quietists publish their memoirs. Every movement wants the Returned standing behind its podium. Very few ask them to speak.

Among themselves, the Returned have a name for the core worlds: **the Aquarium**. Clean. Lit. Sealed. Everyone swimming in circles, beautifully fed, sincerely convinced the glass is the world. Most veterans re-enter the Aquarium and, in time, swim. The ones who can't tend to go back out — to the colonial service, the transit lines, the Reach itself — and the Union has learned not to look too hard at how much of its frontier is staffed by people who could not come home.

Ask one of the Returned what home is like. *"Clean. Lit. Sealed."*

## 5. Ideology & the View of the Imperium

The Union's cause is a **moral crusade: "liberate the serfs."** It is sincere — genuinely, movingly sincere — for the true believers, and comfortably abstract for everyone else. The Imperium is not a rival power in Union rhetoric; it is a crime scene the size of half of human space. Hereditary rule, the ancestor cult, the crèches: the Union's schoolbooks present these not as a culture but as a hostage situation a thousand years long.

And the crusade happens to be expansion. Every liberated world becomes UFS territory, administered by the provisional authority, transitioning to self-rule on a schedule that has never once completed. The Union notices this. The Union is not stupid. It has simply decided, year after year, that the noticing changes nothing about the serfs.

The Union rejects the Concord of Man outright — not fallen scripture but a failed model, deliberately evolved beyond. (Union historians still call its dissolution the Sundering like everyone else, and find the shared vocabulary quietly embarrassing.)

### The hypocrisy, from inside

The Imperial line that lands hardest is the cheapest: **"You have castes too. You just call them Residents."**

Every Union schoolchild learns the three rebuttals, and every honest Union adult knows the weight each one actually bears:

1. **"Our tiers are not hereditary."** True in law — and in practice a Citizen's children inherit the map: which routes are safe, which recruiters to see, how to convert six years into an officer track instead of a rifle. The ladder is open. The rungs are priced.
2. **"The door is open to everyone."** One door. It opens onto the Reach.
3. **"A Resident lives better than an Imperial duke's subjects."** True, provably, materially true — and the most corrosive sentence in the Union's mouth, because *they are better off under us* is the argument of every comfortable aristocracy that ever was. The Union hears its own voice using the Imperium's grammar, and changes the subject.

What the Union has that the Imperium does not is the argument itself. The Whole House Amendment gets read into the record every session. The Unpinned walk bare-collared through New Geneva unarrested. The hypocrisy is real, and it is *litigated*, in public, forever — which is either the system's redemption or its anesthetic, depending on which pamphlet you read.

The Union's answer to "you have castes too" has never been a denial. It has been: *ours argue back.*

## 6. The Military

Two services: **FLEETCOM** (Fleet Command) and the **UGF** (Union Ground Forces). Their shared foundation is technological superiority — drones, orbital fire discipline, networked combined arms — and their shared constraint is arithmetic with a face on it: every casualty is a voter, a family, a news story. Expensive soldiers, careful doctrine. (For what that doctrine is and how it fights, see the war files. This section is about what the services *are*.)

### FLEETCOM

A service of curators. Fleets are small and precious; a capital hull is a decade of budget and a political event, and losing one is a national tragedy with a commission of inquiry attached. FLEETCOM's institutional culture follows: meticulous, understated, obsessed with readiness and maintenance, allergic to risk in a way its own young officers find suffocating. Ships have biographies. Crews keep them like arguments for their own existence.

The service has not fought a full fleet action in a generation — not since Cerulyn Deep, whose lesson (capital fleets cannot be replaced on war-relevant timescales) is FLEETCOM's founding trauma and standing excuse. Its daily war is convoys, pickets, and patience; its promotion boards famously favor officers who have never lost anything, which selects, year by year, for officers who have never risked anything. The admiralty knows this. The admiralty has commissioned studies. The studies are very careful.

A service of curators, praying it never has to spend the collection.

### The UGF

An army of sergeants. Delegated initiative isn't just the UGF's doctrine, it is its personality: the service runs on a deep, professional, aggressively unglamorous NCO cadre, and its culture treats competence the way the Imperium treats lineage. Its soldiers' quiet worship of the network is real and earned — the network is the reason the tide doesn't reach you — and the human beings inside that network are the object of the worship, a point that matters enormously below.

Rotation is law: fourteen-month combat tours, hard-capped, honored even mid-campaign, because the Union relieves its soldiers on schedule for the same reason it pays its bonds on schedule — the whole system runs on the promise. The class texture is unmissable: the officer corps skews toward Citizens' children on sponsored academy tracks; the rifle companies are overwhelmingly Residents earning the pin. The UGF is, among everything else it is, the Union's franchise factory — its raw material is Residents, its product is Citizens, and the conversion rate is posted weekly outside every DACR office.

The two services share a war and a mutual grievance older than most of their personnel: FLEETCOM decides the resupply windows, the UGF pays for them. FLEETCOM delivers the army to the war and the war to the army, and each service privately believes it is the one doing the delivering.

## 7. The Union's Soul

The standing temptation, as the war grinds on: autonomous weapons, or even a cloning program of the Union's own. What the war does to the Union's soul is not a subplot. It is the plot.

### The machines

The Union's drones spot, fly, fuze, and range. They do everything but decide. The decision — the kill — is reserved to a human being by Charter law, DJC precedent, and seventy years of doctrine, and the Resolutionist critique of this arrangement has the horrible virtue of being accurate: *we keep a man in the loop the way the Imperium keeps a priest at the launch rail.* The human veto is no longer an engineering necessity. It is a ritual. And the ritual is the casualty — the voter, the family, the news story — which means the ritual is the arithmetic, which means removing it changes the war's size.

That is the real reason the Directory refuses, and Voss has come closer than anyone to saying it plainly: the machines would not end the war. They would end the *limit*. An enemy that manufactures people, fought by a Union that manufactures deciders, is the load-bearing wall of the entire settled peace-that-isn't, removed on purpose.

The Union's compromise is housed in the DRS Deferred Systems Annex and known informally as **the Eighteen-Month Doctrine**: the Union does not build autonomous weapons, and maintains at all times the proven, tested, funded ability to be fielding them eighteen months after a Directory vote. Never build it; always be ready to have been wrong. The Annex's engineers are the best-paid people in the Union who have never shipped anything.

The Union knows exactly what it would take to win. That is what the drawer is for.

### The whisper

The other temptation is quieter, because everyone involved understands what it would mean. Deep in the DHR budget — the *health* budget, a placement nobody finds accidental — sits a line item called **Workforce Continuity Studies**. It is small. It has been renewed for nineteen consecutive years. No facility is named. No facility has ever been named.

The advocates — some Resolutionist planners, some DIRWAR actuaries, never in public and never using the word — speak of "regenerative force structure" and point at the one unanswerable column in the ledger: the Union cannot attrit an enemy that manufactures people. Every year the numbers are re-run, and every year the numbers return the same three exits: the machines, the batches, or defeat by subscription.

The horrified are almost everyone else, and their objection is not squeamishness — the Union has no church to be squeamish for it; its horror is outsourced to the veterans' associations and the DJC. The objection is a fork, and it cuts on both tines:

- If a decanted soldier can earn the franchise, then the Assembly will one day be elected by batches raised, from the vat up, to want what the state wants. The Union becomes a machine for manufacturing its own consent.
- If he cannot, then the Union has built the Vatborn with a cleaner font — service without franchise, the caste it crusades against, printed at home under a health-directorate line item.

There is no third tine. That is why the line item is small, and why it is never zero. The same Union that cannot decide whether Vatborn prisoners count as POWs is keeping, at minimum, the paperwork current on making some of its own.

The whisper never gets louder. It also never stops.

## Open questions

- Does Voss have a theory of victory at all, or is the equilibrium the entire doctrine? And what happens to the braid when she goes — retirement, death, or one lost vote in a room of eleven?
- What does the Charter actually say about the franchise? Is the Whole House Amendment a constitutional revolution or a reinterpretation the DJC courts could deliver quietly — and who has standing to bring the case?
- Has DACR ever *de-accredited* Citizens at scale? What does revocation look like, what earns it, and is there a list?
- Has anything ever left the Deferred Systems Annex? Is eighteen months the real number — and who, outside the Directory, would know if it had already been spent?
- Resident-majority worlds run their own municipal life. Are there worlds effectively governed by Residents in everything but the vote that matters — and what happens the first time one of them says so in those words?
- What does Ilsabet Voss do? The First Director's Resident daughter is the most politically legible private person in the Union — what does it mean the day she enlists, or the day she pointedly doesn't?
- Where do Workforce Continuity Studies physically live — a filing cabinet, a tissue bank, or something with a heartbeat?
- Who is the Surveyor — and does the Assembly that confirmed the appointment actually know?

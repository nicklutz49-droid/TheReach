# Client States and the Fringe

*The powers that orbit the powers.*

---

## 1. The Client System

Each superpower owns a stable of clients; territory changes hands through client defections as much as battles.

The Imperium calls its stable **the Sworn Marches** — personal oaths, sworn to the throne or to a sponsoring great house, sealed with genealogies and hostage-marriages. The UFS calls its own **Partner Systems** — institutional agreements, renewable, audited, festooned with annexes. One side's euphemism is medieval, the other's is corporate. The relationship underneath is the same shape: protection, obligation, and a border drawn through someone else's home.

Clients exist because the war is limited and the frontier is not. Along most of its length, the two giants do not touch each other directly — they touch clients, who absorb the raids, host the pickets, feed the fleets, and take the blame. A client's first duty, on either side, is to be *between*.

Fifty-two years of war look different from inside a client state than from either capital. The core worlds experience the war as expenditure. The clients experience it as weather that never breaks.

## 2. Imperial Clients — "the Sworn Marches"

| State | Character |
|---|---|
| **Principality of Novgoroy** | Rus-pastiche border princedom; oldest, hungriest client. Fierce auxiliaries; its Prince campaigns openly to be elevated to a great house. |
| **Kingdom of Aldemar** | The model vassal. Franco-courtly, prosperous, loyal, hosts a Guild enclave. Paraded at negotiations: *this could be you.* |
| **Khaganate of the Ederi Drift** | Void-nomad fleets, garbled steppe identity, bound by tribute not oath. Deniable raiders — "we cannot control the Khagans." |
| **Free Duchy of Skarn** | Client on paper, smuggler-state in practice; trades with both sides. Tolerated because Skarn's back channels are how the superpowers talk unofficially. |

### Principality of Novgoroy

No polity in human space has spent more of itself on the war per head than Novgoroy, and no polity keeps better records of the fact. Prince **Vsevolot IV** maintains the **Ledger of Sons** — a casualty roll formatted as a credential, bound in white leather, carried to Mars by his envoys at every session of the Great Council. Novgoroy regiments have died in every year of the war; the Prince's argument is arithmetic: no oath-list of any great house shows more blood given for the throne. What Novgoroy wants at the war's end is simple and enormous — a thirteenth chair. Elevation. The word *client* struck from its name.

The obstacle is House Rurikov, and everyone knows it. Rurikov blocks the elevation at every turn — publicly on procedural grounds, actually because two Rus-flavored powers would split one inheritance of cultural authority, and Rurikov did not spend a thousand years fossilizing its misrememberings to share them with the help. The looming election has made Novgoroy frantic: an elevation is exactly the kind of bribe a desperate candidate might pay, and exactly the kind Rurikov exists to veto. The Prince courts Karolus, flatters Habsberg, and bleeds on schedule.

Novgoroy's application is written in blood, and Rurikov keeps returning it unread.

### Kingdom of Aldemar

Aldemar is what happens when a client wins. Prosperous, courtly, untouched by raiding for forty years, its harvests tithed but never requisitioned, its heirs educated on Mars, its Guild enclave humming behind walls no house army would dare approach. King **Thibaut** presides over the most pleasant polity in the Sworn Marches, and Imperial diplomats present it at every negotiation like a brochure. Aldemar's function is to be shown. Its court has refined being-shown into statecraft: the pageantry is impeccable, the loyalty is real, and the terror underneath is very quiet.

Because the model vassal has done the math. Aldemar is useful precisely while the war needs a *this could be you* — and Aldemar has watched what the Imperium does with things it is finished with. Elevation will never come; Novgoroy's centuries of blood haven't bought a chair, and Aldemar's centuries of comfort certainly won't. Absorption is likelier, some future emperor's wedding gift to a favored house. So Aldemar wants the war to end slowly, incompletely, in a way that keeps brochures necessary — and it has learned to be quietly indispensable to the Guild enclave it hosts, on the theory that the Guilds outlast emperors. Aldemar's great fear is not conquest.

It is being finished with.

### Khaganate of the Ederi Drift

The Khaganate is not a nation; it is an agreement that occasionally holds. The Ederi are migratory fleet-hordes — clan-flotillas of a few hundred hulls each, orbiting the war the way scavengers orbit a herd. At last count, five Khagans claim the title simultaneously, which is why "we cannot control the Khagans" is the rare Imperial diplomatic statement that is technically true. Their identity is a garbled steppe inheritance a thousand years removed from any steppe: their small craft are called **ponies**, and no Ederi has ever seen a horse.

Raiding is not the Ederi's military policy; it is their economy, their marriage market, and their calendar. Bride-price is paid in **prize-shares**; a clan's standing is counted in the captured hulls towed behind its motherships; the great seasonal musters where the hordes meet to trade, marry, and settle feuds are scheduled around the plunder cycle. What they take, they sell — much of it through Skarn, whose fences ask no questions. As for the tribute that binds them to the Imperium: whether it flows to Mars or from it depends on which ledger you read. The Imperium books it as the Khagans' homage. The Khagans book it as the Imperium's rent. Their oldest human relationship runs through House Jorvik, whose void-raider tradition the Ederi regard with the affection one keeps for a talented cousin who joined the police.

What the Ederi want at the war's end is for there to be no end. Peace is the one weapon that could actually destroy them — a raiding economy with nothing to raid is a famine with extra steps — and the older Khagans understand this perfectly. The younger ones have begun hiring out as escorts as readily as raiders, learning the other side of the convoy ledger. The Imperium finds this ominous, and is right to.

The Ederi do not fear losing the war. They fear it ending.

### Free Duchy of Skarn

Skarn's constitution is a masterpiece of licensed hypocrisy: a sworn Imperial client whose sworn duty, by centuries of careful precedent, includes trading with anyone. The Duchy sells flags of convenience, bonded warehousing, unregistered berths, and above all *deniability* — the commodity both superpowers consume in bulk and neither can produce domestically. In the **Glass Quarter** of Skarn's capital station, Imperial and UFS legates dine in the same restaurants and pretend not to; the two intelligence services keep offices on the same street, which everyone agrees is more convenient. Prisoner exchanges are negotiated over Skarnish tables. So, occasionally, are things neither government will ever admit were negotiated at all.

Fifty-two years of war have made Skarn rich beyond the dreams of its founders, and the Duchy has responded with the discipline of a professional: it keeps its prices fair, its secrets sealed, and its usefulness perfectly balanced. The current Duke is affable and unmemorable — Dukes of Skarn are interchangeable by design, since a memorable one would be a liability. What Skarn wants at the war's end is not peace but *peace process*: decades of talks, summits, verification regimes, and confidence-building measures, ideally all hosted at Skarn, at standard rates.

Skarn's export is deniability, and business has never been better.

## 3. UFS Clients — "Partner Systems"

| State | Character |
|---|---|
| **Meridian Compact** | League of station-states joined for market access. Runs FLEETCOM's civilian logistics; fights with invoices. Loyalty measured quarterly. |
| **Republic of New Tyre** | Liberated two generations ago; now more Unionist than the Union. Zealous volunteer brigades that unsettle UGF regulars. |
| **Osei Federation** | Proud technocrats who joined for protection and chafe under "advisory oversight." Hosts the biggest FLEETCOM anchorage in the Reach — and resents it. Most likely to flip. |
| **PALT — Provisional Authority for Liberated Territories** | UFS administration of captured imperial worlds: deprogramming ancestor cults, managing unplanned Vatborn populations, forever "transitioning to self-rule." The crusade's moral wound, wearing a lanyard. |

### Meridian Compact

The Compact's governing organ is called, with disarming honesty, **the Board**, and the Board discusses the war the way it discusses everything: as exposure. Fifty-two years of conflict have been the best thing that ever happened to Meridian's balance sheets — the Compact runs FLEETCOM's civilian logistics under a master charter renegotiated every five years, and by some coincidence every renegotiation has landed during a crisis. FLEETCOM's quartermasters despise depending on them and have audited them into glass; the audits always come back clean, which is somehow worse. Meridian's ships arrive with contractual exactness. Its invoices arrive faster.

What Meridian wants at the war's end is for the contracts to survive the peace. The Board's actual nightmare is not an Imperial victory — Imperial trade policy is negotiable; everything about the Imperium is negotiable if you find the right house — but a *sudden* peace: demobilization without a transition schedule, a hundred logistics contracts void in a fiscal quarter. Meridian therefore lobbies, discreetly and expensively, for exactly the outcome the UFS officially seeks — orderly victory, long consolidation, generous reconstruction budgets. It is the Union's most reliable partner for the most disquieting reason.

Meridian has never lost a battle. It has never fought one.

### Republic of New Tyre

New Tyre was an Imperial world once, liberated in the war's first decade, and it has spent two generations making sure nobody forgets — least of all itself. The old nobility's palaces are preserved as atrocity museums; schoolchildren tour the tithe-cellars and the levy-registries annually; the anniversary of liberation is a holy day in a state that officially has none. Its volunteer brigades fight with a fervor that unsettles UGF regulars, who kill because the fire mission is approved. The Tyrians kill because they remember. UGF officers privately note that New Tyre's brigades are the only Union formation the tempo doctrine does not demoralize.

What New Tyre wants at the war's end is the war's *point*: total liberation, the crusade completed, every tithe-cellar in the Imperium turned into a museum. It aligns naturally with the Union's war party and regards the Directory's careful arithmetic as accountancy performed over graves. Its sharpest quarrel, though, is with PALT — New Tyre lobbies relentlessly to administer newly liberated worlds itself, *we know how it's done*, and PALT keeps refusing, on the unstated grounds that New Tyre's idea of deprogramming involves rather more zeal than the mandate contemplates.

The Union liberated New Tyre once. New Tyre has never forgiven it for stopping.

### Osei Federation

The Osei joined for protection in the war's third year, when an Imperial expansion wave made the choice for them, and they have been regretting the terms ever since. The great FLEETCOM anchorage in their home system sits on a ninety-nine-year lease signed that year; the Osei foreign ministry can tell you the remaining term to the day. It is not a fact a partner should keep that fresh. Proud technocrats with an engineering culture older than the Union's charter, the Osei endure "advisory oversight" the way a master shipwright endures a client who stands too close — and the advisers, to their credit, know it, which changes nothing.

What Osei wants at the war's end is subtraction: the lease concluded, the oversight ended, its own fleet un-capped, its skies its own. Nobody serious believes Osei would join the Imperium — the feudal aesthetic nauseates them — and this is precisely what makes them dangerous, because flipping does not mean changing sides. It means neutrality: a rich, defensible, technically brilliant nation stepping out of the Union's sensor picture and taking the Reach's biggest anchorage with it. Imperial diplomats send polite feelers through Skarn at respectful intervals. The Osei file them. They do not burn them. FLEETCOM's counterintelligence knows about the filing cabinet and has decided, so far, that a filing cabinet is not a crime.

Osei does not want to change sides. It wants to stop having one.

### PALT — Provisional Authority for Liberated Territories

PALT is the client that nobody signed. It is the war's sediment: every liberated Imperial world that cannot yet be trusted with self-rule accretes to it, and after five decades it administers thirty-one worlds — more people than several Partner Systems combined — with no vote, no flag anyone salutes, and a mandate whose middle word is doing unspeakable work. Its staff deprogram ancestor cults with curricula, census unplanned Vatborn populations with forms that have no box for what they are, and mark each world's file *transitioning* in the tender tense of a process that has outlived everyone who began it.

The war from inside PALT is a moral wound examined daily under fluorescent light. Every staffer learns to dread what the manuals do not name and the veterans call **the Question** — the moment a liberated farmer, grateful, fed, three years into freedom, quietly asks when the Emperor is coming back. What PALT wants at the war's end is for someone, anyone, to define what winning means for it: graduation dates, a dissolution clause, an answer. The Directory has never provided one, because the honest answer — that PALT worlds become UFS territory and always were going to — is the crusade's hypocrisy said aloud. So the Authority grows by conquest, shrinks by nothing, and files its annual reports.

PALT's organization chart has a box for everything except an exit.

## 4. How Worlds Change Hands

Battles take worlds slowly and expensively. Defections take them overnight, and the last decade's clearest lesson is that the cheapest offensive either power can mount is a good offer, patiently made.

The textbook case is nine years old. The **Margraviate of Kest** — three poor systems on the Imperial side of the Reach, sworn to House Normand — spent a generation watching its patron's attention drift to fresher conquests while Kest's convoy tithes rose and its garrison requests went unanswered. Margrave **Uwain of Kest** opened contact through Skarn's back channels and negotiated for two patient years. When the moment came, a UGF squadron crossed the shoal into Kest the same week the Margraviate's tithe fleet was due to depart for Normand space; the tithe fleet simply never left. House Normand demanded a punitive expedition. The Great Council ran the arithmetic — three poor systems, a prepared defender, a war already grinding elsewhere — and declined. Instead the Imperium struck Kest from the rolls entirely: Imperial charts now mark its systems by their old Concord catalog numbers, as if no oath had ever existed there.

The UFS calls it **the Kest Accession**. The Imperium calls it **the Treason of Kest**. The Margrave lives comfortably on New Geneva with a pension and a security detail; his grandchildren are Residents, accruing service credit toward citizenship in the state their grandfather delivered three systems to. Both sides drew the same conclusion, and both sides' client-management budgets grew the next year.

Three systems changed hands, and the only shot fired was a salute.

## 5. The Alien Fringe

At the far fringes of human space: small alien stellar nations — occasional trade, simple diplomacy. The giants are too focused on each other to care.

That indifference is mutual, and it is the strangest fact about the fringe. Humanity arrived in this region loud, numerous, and eventually at war with itself, and its alien neighbors reacted the way settled people react to weather. Every fringe nation was already old when humans arrived. None of them will say what the region was like before. Both superpowers maintain small legations at the principal fringe ports and receive, in return, courtesy without curiosity. The aliens trade. They do not ask about the war. After fifty-two years, they have never once asked.

| Nation | What is known |
|---|---|
| **The Shathi Lattice** | Sell refined isotopes and flawless crystal at fixed prices unchanged in two centuries. Never haggle, never explain, never seen outside their suits. Their honesty is total and, over long acquaintance, unnerving. |
| **The Cantorate** | Speak in gravity pulses; their border is marked by dead moons that hum. The one frontier both superpowers' charts label with the same word: CLOSED. Neither archive records who decided that, or why. |
| **The Miroth** | Buy human culture — dead languages, old music, ships' logs, grief. Pay in exotic matter that keeps half of Skarn's fences solvent. Asked once, formally, what they remember of Earth, they named a price. No one has paid it. |
| **The Undertakers** | Human name; their own is a gesture, not a sound. Arrive after battles and buy the wreckage — hulls, debris, the unrecovered dead. Both navies sell. They pay promptly and well, which somehow makes it worse. |
| **The Loi** | Solitary peddler-ships, one at a time, in every human port from Mars to New Geneva. No Loi fleet, homeworld, or government has ever been observed. Their nation is an inference. The Loi decline to confirm it. |
| **The Ould Procession** | An entire civilization in transit: a fleet-nation crossing human space at sublight on a journey begun before human records. They buy fuel, sell nothing, and answer no questions about the destination. They are, at current speed, about four centuries from leaving. |

The fringe nations are minor by every measure either superpower uses — tonnage, population, guns. The measures the fringe nations use are not on file.

## 6. The Machines

**AI: simple and narrow only — ship computers, data processing, assistants. No strong AI (so far).** The rule is not a treaty. It is two civilizations arriving at the same flinch by different roads.

On the Imperial side, narrow machines are Guild work: **reckoning-engines** that compute fire solutions, run crèche logistics, and keep centuries-old capital hulls alive. The theological framing — the Guilds' business, and jealously kept — comes down in practice to a bright line every Imperial officer knows: the engines *reckon*; they do not *advise*. A machine that speaks in the first person would be a horror on the order of a walking corpse. Commoners, of course, cannot tell a reckoning-engine from an angel either way.

On the Union side, the line is statutory. Certified **bounded systems** operate under the **autonomy ceiling** — a lattice of law and audit requiring a human decision at every lethal or governing node. UGF drones are recon partners, not robot swarms, because the ceiling says so; every fire mission has a name attached, because the ceiling says so. It is a genuinely held principle, and it is also, colonels mutter, a tax measured in seconds — paid at exactly the tempo where the Imperium spends lives to make seconds count. The temptation to raise the ceiling is now spoken aloud in the Union's war party, and is that faction's business to argue and the Union's soul that is at stake (see the Union file).

The line frays at the edges, as lines do. Forward UGF units run their pre-clearances wider every year, so that the human decision is real but increasingly resembles ratification. Imperial fire-control on the great old hulls is, Guild whispers say, rather better company than doctrine admits. Skarn's grey market sells expert systems of no certified origin to buyers of no recorded name. And out on the fringe, the Cantorate's vessels have no visible crew, the Loi peddler answers questions a shade too quickly, and neither superpower has any way of knowing whether some of their small strange neighbors *have* strong AI — or are it.

Both powers agree on almost nothing, except that no one should build a mind. So far, no one admits to having tried.

## Open questions

- Skarn's vaults hold fifty years of both superpowers' deniable business. Who actually keeps the Duchy's ledgers — and what happens to them if Skarn is ever forced to pick a side?
- If a desperate election candidate finally grants Novgoroy its thirteenth chair, what is House Rurikov actually prepared to do about it?
- Which of the five Khagans believes the Imperium's tribute is owed to *them* alone — and what happens to the Drift when one of them decides to collect?
- Has anyone ever followed an Undertaker convoy home? And if someone has, why is there no record of the attempt?
- What price did the Miroth name for what they remember of Earth — and which government has come closest to paying it?
- If the war ends, does PALT dissolve — or does it wake up one morning and discover that thirty-one worlds with a shared administration, a shared grievance, and no vote are a nation?
- Who inside the Osei Federation maintains the filing cabinet of Imperial feelers, and is FLEETCOM's patience with it policy — or bait?
- The fringe nations were old and quiet before humanity arrived. Are they peripheral because they are small — or small because something taught them to be?

# The War: A Chronology
*Fifty-two years, six phases, and no agreement yet on where one ends and the next begins.*

---

## 1. On Naming a War While Fighting It

Both sides date the war the same way and refuse to admit it. Union staff colleges number **war years** from the Serevan Incident — war year 0, A.M. 1098, CY 558 — and Imperial staff colleges do too, in the privacy of their own planning rooms, whatever the liturgical calendar says on the memorial plaques. Neither government will cite the other's convention in public. Both use it in private, because a shared clock is the one piece of Concord-era inheritance neither side has managed to improve on, and campaigns fought across a light-lagged frontier do not run on anyone's feelings about it. The **Free Duchy of Skarn**'s neutral archive has quietly published a synchronized war-year chronology every year since roughly the war's third decade, cross-referenced to both native calendars; both admiralties subscribe; neither cites the source.

What follows divides the fifty-two years into six phases. The boundaries are not a native category of either empire — no Union statute and no Imperial decree ever declared a phase open or closed — but a retrospective convenience that emerged, oddly, from both academies at once: Union military historians and Imperial court chroniclers, working from the same campaign records and hating each other's conclusions, drew the joints in almost the same places. Campaigns do not respect propaganda. They respect logistics, and logistics leaves a signature no chronicler can argue away.

The names for each phase, however, remain entirely unshared. Union usage is what Union usage always is: clinical, retrospective, embarrassed by its own occasional poetry. Imperial usage is what Imperial usage always is: liturgical, present-tense, allergic to admitting a period has *ended* rather than been *fulfilled*. Read both columns. Neither is lying. They are describing the same fifty-two years from opposite sides of an argument neither expects to win.

*Six phases, twelve names, one war.*

## 2. Phase I — War Years 0–8: the Expeditionary War / the Going-Forth

This was the war both sides expected to fight, and it lasted almost exactly as long as that illusion did.

For the Union, the Expeditionary War felt like doctrine vindicated: FLEETCOM's forward posture, the UGF's networked combined arms, the whole peacetime theory of the professional force — all of it worked, on schedule, against opponents who fought like history books. For the Imperium, the Going-Forth was a liturgy of righteous response that kept discovering, engagement by engagement, that righteousness does not defeat a fire-control network. Neither side yet understood what it was fighting. Both would spend the next nine years finding out, at cost.

### The Serevan Incident and its year (war year 0)
The war's opening has its own file and needs no repeating here beyond the shape it left: *Right of Conquest* opens fire in Serevan Anchorage; five months later, **Operation RECEIPT** burns House Normand's anchorage at Bregil and kills some two thousand sworn men of three houses; within the year, the first Imperial legions land on Union-surveyed worlds in the Reach. Two governments that had spent three centuries rehearsing this moment discover that rehearsal is not the same as readiness. Neither improvises well. Both improvise anyway.

### The Fivefold Landing (war year 2 / A.M. 1100 / CY 560)
The war's first deliberate UGF offensive, and its first demonstration piece: five simultaneous beachheads seized along the Reach's coreward edge inside eleven hours, each executed to a rehearsed plan, each holding. FLEETCOM's after-action report is four paragraphs long and reads like a training manual because it was one. Union schoolbooks still open the war with this battle, not Serevan — Serevan is a tragedy; the Fivefold Landing is a *proof*. The Union spent the next six years discovering how rarely the enemy would again be so obliging.

### The Coria Muster (war year 3 / A.M. 1101 / CY 561)
The Going-Forth's first reckoning, on the mining world of **Coria**: a coalition of marcher levies, summoned by three lesser houses eager to be first at the new war, advanced across open ground on the old assumption — mass, faith, the banner — into a UGF defense in depth that had not yet learned to expect anything else. The muster broke in ninety minutes; casualty estimates run past eleven thousand and no side has ever published a firm number. Imperial staff colleges do not teach Coria by name. They teach its lesson without the name attached, which every officer who passes through the course recognizes anyway. *The tempo doctrine was not invented at Serrat. It was ordered at Serrat because Coria had already made the alternative unbearable.*

### Halvard's World (war years 6–8)
Union landings on Halvard's World in year 6; a continuous front by year 8. The garden world's own long history belongs to the ground war's file — here it matters only as the moment the Expeditionary War stopped expecting to end. A war of movement does not survive contact with a front that cannot be flanked, resupplied around, or starved out by either side. Halvard's did not create the stalemate that followed. It simply arrived first, and everyone else caught up to it within a decade.

By year 8, both staffs had quietly stopped drafting victory timelines. Neither said so publicly for years.

## 3. Phase II — War Years 9–17: the Hardening / the Unresting Years

If the first phase was a war of movement, this one was a war of *method* — each side discovering, campaign by campaign, what actually worked against the other, and hardening doctrine, terrain, and temperament around the answer. The Union learned that its symphony had a refresh rate an enemy could exceed. The Imperium learned exactly how to exceed it, and what that costs in men who do not get to be doctrine, only its proof.

### The death of Roul fitz-Osbern (war year 9 / A.M. 1107 / CY 567)
The man whose reading of a clock started the war lived to see it become someone else's war entirely. Roul fitz-Osbern, Margrave of the Serevan March, died in year 9 leading a relief column through the **Ilenne Strait** — a supply corridor, not a battle anyone remembers, against a UGF interdiction force that did not know or care whose name was on the manifest. He was not the highest-ranking casualty of that week. Chroniclers on both sides have noted the anticlimax and declined to improve on it. The clock outlived the man who misread it by nine years, and then it, too, stopped mattering.

### The Vigil of Guns — Serrat (war year 9 / A.M. 1107 / CY 567)
The proving of the tempo doctrine belongs to the ground war's file in full; here it marks the hinge of the phase. Thirty-one days of continuous assault against the war-college position, kill ratios exceeding ten to one throughout, and the line moved anyway. The Union rewrote a field manual. The Imperium wrote a liturgy. Both documents describe the same thirty-one days and neither would recognize the other's version of them.

### The Cinder Line, Tarrow (war year 12 / A.M. 1110 / CY 570)
Halvard's was the first front to harden past all recognition; it was not the only one. On the volcanic world of **Tarrow**, three hundred light-years coreward, a UGF colonial garrison and a Chandragupt expeditionary corps dug into the ash fields around the world's one habitable rift valley and simply stayed — neither side able to force a decision, neither willing to withdraw and cede the rift's mineral wealth outright. The **Cinder Line** never produced a battle worth a name in either archive. It produced eleven years of raids, wire, and slow attrition that both staffs quietly used to validate lessons too expensive to test on Halvard's. A front nobody remembers is still a front somebody dies on.

### The Truce of Two Fevers (war year 14 / A.M. 1112 / CY 572)
On the Wet Flank of Halvard's World, a waterborne fever swept both trench systems inside the same fortnight — the same disease, the same water table, the same mosquitoes indifferent to which flag flew over them. For eleven days, without any order from either headquarters, forward units on both sides stopped shooting and started sharing quinine. No truce was signed. No general authorized it. When the fever passed, the shooting resumed within the hour, on both sides, without comment. Neither government has ever officially acknowledged the episode happened. Every soldier who was there insists it did.

### Maren Col — the Spite (war year 17 / A.M. 1115 / CY 575)
Forty thousand Novgoroy auxiliaries died in a pocket Rurikov's guns could have opened and did not, for six silent hours, while a censured marshal watched a client-rival humbled at wholesale prices. Prince **Vsevolot IV**'s envoys entered the dead into the **Ledger of Sons** within the season, bound in the same white leather as every other entry, formatted — as always — as a credential rather than a grief. It is the single bloodiest page the Ledger has ever required, and Novgoroy has carried it to every Council session since as evidence for a thirteenth chair that has never come.

By year 17, "hardening" had a price tag on both fronts, and both staffs had begun, independently, to plan for a war that would not end inside anyone's career.

## 4. Phase III — War Years 18–22: the Anchorage Crisis / the Wager Years

Five years, one crisis, one afternoon that decided thirty more years of naval doctrine. This is the shortest phase and the one both admiralties still study hardest.

### Forward Anchorage 9 (war years 18–22)
FLEETCOM began assembling a repair yard and magazine complex at Cerulyn in year 18: nothing dramatic, a logistics project, the kind of thing that wins wars by making other things possible rather than by being visible itself. Every Imperial admiral who saw the projections understood exactly what a forward yard would do to the convoy war within a decade. None would place his house's fleet under another house's banner to stop it.

### The First Cerulyn Muster (war year 18 / A.M. 1116 / CY 576)
The first attempt at a joint expedition to interdict the anchorage collapsed before it left dock, over a dispute between Rurikov and Jorvik contingents for **the honor of the van** — an argument neither side has ever agreed a resolution to, since Rurikov's mass-levy patience and Jorvik's raider pride recognize no common measure of precedence. The fleet dispersed to its home ports without firing a shot at anyone but its own signal books.

### The Second Cerulyn Muster (war year 19 / A.M. 1117 / CY 577)
The second attempt fared no better, and fared worse in a stranger way: three weeks into assembly, House Aztlan's contingent withdrew in its entirety, without explanation acceptable to any other house present, on a date its own captains could apparently justify only by reference to a ritual calendar nobody outside Aztlan has ever fully charted. Aztlan's absence reopened the one question a joint muster cannot survive asking twice — who, now, held the van — and the remaining houses sent it back to precedence committee to redraw. The committee never reported out. The second expedition died the same death as the first, in stranger clothes. Two full expeditions, two years, and Forward Anchorage 9 kept growing.

### The Cerulyn Picket (war years 19–21 / A.M. 1117–1119 / CY 577–579)
While the houses re-litigated the van twice over, someone still had to slow what the van was meant to stop. A rotating UGF destroyer screen picketed Cerulyn's approaches for three years — eleven actions, no capital ship committed on either side, one destroyer lost to each fleet — intercepting Imperial fast packets sent to scout, harass, or simply confirm what the anchorage had become. It earned no name in Imperial chronicles and only a case-file number in Union ones. It also bought Forward Anchorage 9 the years the joint expeditions kept failing to spend, which both staffs understood at the time and neither has ever cared to commemorate.

### The Battle of Cerulyn Deep / the Emperor's Wager (war year 22 / A.M. 1120 / CY 580)
The war's one great fleet battle has its own file and needs no repeating in full: Alexios IV stakes the Sacred Armada to force nine houses to sail as one; **the Withering of the Flower** costs the errantry half its strength in three days of strike battles; a fifty-three-minute press unmakes both gun lines; roughly a hundred and eight thousand die in nine days. Fleet Admiral **Odile Nakamura-Reyes** breaks off and runs for the shoal with everything that can still burn. **Megadux Isidor Kantakuzen** dies on the flag bridge of the *Autokrator Nikephor* with an order that outlived him. Forward Anchorage 9 burns to slag the same week. Neither battle fleet has returned to its year-22 strength since.

The Anchorage Crisis ends the way crises end: with a lesson too expensive to unlearn. *Capital fleets cannot be replaced on war-relevant timescales.* Every phase that follows is downstream of that one sentence.

## 5. Phase IV — War Years 23–35: the Long Middle / the Tithe Years

Thirteen years is the longest phase on the table, and it earns the length by being the least eventful in the way that matters and the most eventful in the way that kills people. This is the war after both admiralties learned to count their hulls before they spent them — chess navies, a convoy war ascendant above every campaign that mattered on the ground, and a string of witherings that proved the same point from different angles until neither side needed it proven again.

For the Union, the Long Middle felt like patience made into policy: Nakamura-Reyes canonized for declining to spend what the Union could not mint, FLEETCOM's promotion boards quietly favoring officers who had never lost anything. For the Imperium, the Tithe Years were exactly what the name says — the age when the war stopped being glory and became a bill, paid in Guild lease-prices, tithe-fleet losses, and the slow arithmetic of what a house could still afford to feel proud of.

### *Antaios* stranded and falls (war years 26–27)
Mired in the Cauter Fens by a UGF mobility kill three engineer battalions spent a month preparing; fought as a stationary fortress for nine months while House Normand mounted four failed relief operations and lost a campaign season in the trying; fell in the spring of year 27 when the fourth relief arrived in time only to watch his veil fail. The **Antaios Stone** still marks the charts of both armies. It has its own file, its own cult, and its own unanswered question.

### Convoy HW-91 (war year 28 / A.M. 1126 / CY 586)
Thirty-four hulls and three escorts, bound for Halvard's World, caught at emergence by an Ederi arrow-host and taken apart across eleven days of stern-chase. Fewer than a third of the tenders made the window. On Halvard's, in both armies' slang, "ninety-one" has meant *promised and not delivered* ever since — the phase's single clearest proof that the convoy war, not the gun line, was where the Long Middle actually happened.

### The Vantry Standoff (war years 29–33 / A.M. 1127–1131 / CY 587–591)
On the mining moon of **Vantry**, a UGF division and an Osmanli-Chandragupt joint garrison spent four years facing each other across eleven kilometers of crater terrain, neither side able to muster the naval margin to force the other off the rock, neither willing to abandon the ore concessions underneath it. No offensive of any size was ever launched by either garrison. Both sides dug, both sides rotated crews on schedule, and both sides eventually stopped bothering to shell each other except on anniversaries. Vantry ended not in battle but in a quiet mutual withdrawal in year 33, negotiated through Skarn, when both admiralties independently concluded the moon had never been worth the fleet margin either side would need to actually take it. Neither official history calls it a defeat. Neither calls it a victory either. Staff colleges on both sides now teach Vantry as the purest specimen of the Long Middle's whole logic: a war fought to a standstill by people who did the arithmetic honestly and found it said *stop*.

### The Corvant Pocket (war years 31–32 / A.M. 1129–1130 / CY 589–590)
The war's clearest proof that winning every battle is not the same as winning. When the convoy war closed the resupply window over Corvant, FLEETCOM — nine years past the Wager, husbanding hulls it could never replace — declined to force it open. The **Ninth Expeditionary Army**, ninety thousand strong under General **Ansa Reyes-Okafor**, fought on alone for thirteen months and won twenty-nine consecutive engagements while it starved, and eleven thousand were still standing when she capitulated to the Yamatai marshal **Lord Benkei Ashiwara**, who paraded his own regiments to salute the army he had just defeated and returned her sidearm with the surrender. The full account belongs to the ground war's file. Here it marks the moment the naval arithmetic's grip on everything beneath it stopped being argued and started being assumed.

### The Shared Shoal (war year 33 / A.M. 1131 / CY 591)
A small, strange coda to the phase, still told in wardrooms on both sides. A UGF resupply convoy and an Imperial tithe-fleet emerged at the same threshold, from different vectors, within four hours of each other — a coincidence neither side's charts had flagged as more than remotely possible. Both fleets froze rather than fight blind; both spent the Long Burn signaling frantic, light-lagged requests for orders that could not possibly arrive before the encounter resolved itself one way or the other. By the time either commander received instructions, the other fleet had finished unloading its own cargo at its own destination and departed. No shot was fired. No formal incident was ever logged, because neither side could agree on who should have fired first, or whether firing at all would have been within anyone's standing orders. Union officers tell it as a parable about the limits of doctrine. Imperial officers tell it as a parable about providence. Both agree it is the only battle of the war that both sides won by doing nothing.

### The Burning of the Golden Train (war year 35 / A.M. 1133 / CY 593)
House Ming's grand tithe-fleet, nineteen heavy tenders carrying a year's supply for two army groups, caught at a threshold by Hunter Squadron 9 with its escort still forming up — late because two houses had spent six weeks disputing the honor of command. The recriminations outlived the campaign. So, eventually, did the war, which is the only reason anyone still argues about it.

By year 35 the Long Middle had nothing left to prove and kept proving it anyway. The war did not end. It changed shape instead.

## 6. Phase V — War Years 36–44: the Stone Years / the Buried Years

The war turned inward here — literally, at Vell; institutionally, at Skarn; morally, in the growing UFS argument over what the crusade actually was. Both names for this phase gesture at the same thing from opposite angles: the Union's clinical *Stone Years*, for a war fought increasingly below ground, in tunnels and galleries and the audited patience of siege engineering; the Imperium's *Buried Years*, for a war that had started keeping its worst decisions where the Remembrancers and the Council did not have to look at them.

### The Siege of Vell (war years 36–38)
Nineteen months to reduce one arcology, taken from below, gallery by gallery, at a pace Mutapa's engineers published in advance and met to the week. The **Quiet Floor** — the hospital level no weapon touched for the length of the siege, by no agreement anyone signed — belongs to the ground war's file. Here it marks the Stone Years' whole character in miniature: a war that had learned to be patient, precise, and, in exactly one negotiated silence, almost decent.

### Halima Voss's first election (war year 37 / A.M. 1135 / CY 595)
The Directorate of Industrial Mobilization's outgoing Director-General, twenty-six years in the building, was elected First Director by her fellow Directors-General on the first ballot — a fact her colleagues found less surprising than the speed of it. She has been re-elected twice since. Nobody who was in the room in year 37 has ever fully explained, on the record, why the vote took under an hour.

### The Undertow (war years 37–42 / A.M. 1135–1140 / CY 595–600)
Six years of a war neither admiralty has ever officially conceded happened: deniable strikes against picket stations, forward caches, and at least two Guild subcontractors, credited in both fleets' incident logs to unspecified "irregular forces" and attributed, privately and never provably, to a standing Jorvik raiding compact operating outside any house's sworn levy rolls. FLEETCOM's official position is that the Undertow does not exist. Nine incident reports filed in DIS's own archive disagree with FLEETCOM's official position, and DIS has not reconciled the discrepancy in five years of asking.

### The Affair of the Nine Sealed Ships (war year 39 / A.M. 1137 / CY 597)
For four days in Skarn's Glass Quarter, nine cargo manifests surfaced — through channels neither government has ever acknowledged existing — showing bonded cargo moving between Imperial and UFS registries under falsified flags of convenience, in quantities too large for ordinary smuggling and too specific to be coincidence. Both legations denied everything, simultaneously, in nearly identical language — a coincidence neither has explained. The manifests were never published. The ships were never identified publicly. The Duke of Skarn's office issued a single statement — "the matter is resolved to the satisfaction of all concerned parties" — that both governments have quoted approvingly ever since, neither specifying what the matter was.

### The Pilgrim's Answer (war year 40 / A.M. 1138 / CY 598)
The corvette *UFS Tally*, stopped and boarded by the Jorvik drakke-nest hidden aboard what her charts listed as a pilgrim barque, belongs in full to the void war's file. Here it marks the moment the Stone Years stopped observing even the oldest shared liturgy: FLEETCOM's rules of engagement changed within the season, and the one ritual both sides had kept unbroken since before either empire existed — the silent orbit of dead Earth — acquired, for the first time, a targeting exception.

### *Katalu* opens the Anders Line, and the Whole House Amendment (war year 41 / A.M. 1139 / CY 599)
Two events, same year, no relation, and both chronicles have separately noted the coincidence without quite knowing what to do with it. On one front, the Krios-Pattern siege-breaker *Katalu* breaches the Anders Line — the campaign belongs to the siege-breakers' own file — and the tide pours through behind him. On New Geneva, Assemblywoman **Coretta Nwosu-Lindh** introduces the **Whole House Amendment** for the first time, beginning an eleven-year, unbroken run of reintroduction that has not yet succeeded and has not yet stopped. One institution breached a wall that year. The other laid siege to one — and eleven years on, the siege is still going.

### The Kest Accession / the Treason of Kest (war year 43 / A.M. 1141 / CY 601)
Three Normand-sworn systems defect without a shot, negotiated across two patient years through Skarn's back channels — the full account belongs to the client-states file. Here it marks the Buried Years' plainest arithmetic for the Imperium: the cheapest offensive the Union ever mounted against it cost nothing but patience and an open door, and neither cost anything Mars had budgeted for.

### The Greywater (war year 44 / A.M. 1142 / CY 602)
Ten thousand long-service Vatborn of the Fortieth Legion advance into the estuary fog on the world called Vasseur and do not come out — no bodies, no wreckage, no distress traffic, no fire mission on record from either side. The full account, and the Guilds' sealed survey, belong to the ground war's file. It closes the phase the way the phase deserves to be closed: with a silence nobody has managed to explain, in a war that had spent nine years teaching both sides how comfortable silence could become.

By year 44, both home fronts had begun, in different rooms, asking the same unspoken question: how much longer is this supposed to go.

## 7. Phase VI — War Years 45–52: the Equilibrium / the Emperor's Vigil (present)

The quietest phase on the table, and — every staff officer on both sides will tell you this off the record — the most dangerous. Nothing is failing. Everything is slower. Both empires have entered the strange, brittle calm of two exhausted systems each convinced the other is about to break first, neither quite willing to test the proposition.

### The slowing (war years 45–52)
Recruitment meets its targets later each year, with better bonuses; house fleets find reasons to keep hulls near Mars; the war has not lost momentum so much as spent it evenly, on both sides, in ways neither government's instruments were built to measure as failure. This is the whole texture of the Equilibrium, and its clearest proofs arrived on schedule.

### Corin Adeyemi-Strand's appointment (war year 46 / A.M. 1144 / CY 604)
Voss's most quoted political act: elevating the Resolution Bloc's own leader to Director-General of War, the one chair where wanting more war means owning what it costs. Six years on, the appointment has done more to blunt the hawks than any argument the Quiet Border ever made against them. Six years in the chair have done to him what the chair was chosen to do.

### Voss's third term begins (war year 47 / A.M. 1145 / CY 605)
Re-elected to the First Directorship for a third time, on a margin narrower than either of the first two — a fact the Full Franchise bloc reads as opportunity and the Resolution Bloc reads as the equilibrium finally cracking, and neither has yet been proven right.

### The Ninth and Tenth Liberation Loans (war years 48 and 50)
The Ninth Liberation Loan falls short in year 48 — the first in half a century of oversubscribed drives to do so — and is renamed the **Victory Loan** for its Tenth iteration two years later. The Tenth sells worse. DFT's quarterly reports describe both shortfalls in language so carefully neutral that DIS analysts reportedly use it, internally, as a case study in how to say *this is bad* without ever writing the words.

### The Aix Recall (war year 50 / A.M. 1148 / CY 608)
House Karolus begins quietly recalling elite regiments from three fronts toward Mars, framed in every public communiqué as reinforcement for the coronation guard the election has not yet scheduled. Two allied houses have privately complained that the withdrawals thinned sectors Karolus itself had sworn to hold. Duke **Lothar Karolus** has offered no public explanation beyond the framing, which satisfies no one and which everyone, including the UGF's own analysts, currently accepts for lack of a better one.

### The Ashen Signal (war year 51 / A.M. 1149 / CY 609)
An unscheduled emergence flare lit the shoal of a system with no convoy due, no fleet movement logged by either side, and no explanation offered since. Both navies' picket logs recorded it independently, within minutes of each other, and neither has produced a follow-up contact, a wreck, or a claimant. FLEETCOM's report calls it a sensor artifact. The Imperial equivalent calls it unexplained and declines, for now, to call it anything worse. It is the newest item on either side's ledger of things nobody has finished being frightened of.

### Present day (war year 52 / A.M. 1150 / CY 610)
Emperor Alexios IV is ninety-seven and has not been seen to stand in six years. The Conclave-in-waiting has already begun rehearsing a funeral no one has scheduled. First Director Voss keeps the war exactly the size it is, for reasons three factions understand and one of them, eventually, will stop tolerating. The war grinds on.

### The Phase With No Name Yet

Here the chronology runs out of the one thing it needs to finish its own job: distance. Every boundary drawn above was drawn by historians who could see how the phase ended before they named it. Nobody yet can see how this one ends. The Equilibrium and the Emperor's Vigil are, both sides quietly admit, provisional names — the kind a chronicler assigns to a period still in progress, fully expecting to revise them once it is safe to know what actually happened. Both academies have, this year, begun drafting the phase that comes after: contingent chapters, filed and unfiled by conclave date and election outcome, waiting on an old man's health and a chamber's vote to tell them what to call it. Some staff officers on both sides privately suspect the next phase has already begun — quietly, in the recalled regiments and the unexplained flares and the loans nobody can sell — and that the six-phase chronology on file at Skarn will need a seventh line within the year. No one has offered a name for it. That is usually how the worst phases start.

## Open questions

- The Aztlan withdrawal that broke the Second Cerulyn Muster was never explained to the other eleven houses. Was it truly the ritual calendar — or did House Aztlan know something about the coming Wager that the rest of the Conclave didn't?
- What did the Nine Sealed Ships actually carry? Two governments and one duchy have spent thirteen years agreeing, in identical language, not to say.
- The Vantry Standoff ended in a negotiated mutual withdrawal that neither side calls a defeat. Who on each staff pushed hardest for that withdrawal — and were they right that the moon was worthless, or just tired?
- Nobody has produced a follow-up contact for the Ashen Signal. Is FLEETCOM's "sensor artifact" the truth, a cover, or an honest admission that nobody knows?
- The Shared Shoal is remembered as an accident by everyone who tells it. Do either navy's own logs actually support that — or has "coincidence" simply been easier to file than the alternative?
- What is House Karolus actually recalling regiments *for*? Coronation guard is the story two allied houses have already stopped quite believing.
- Skarn has synchronized both sides' war-year chronology for three decades without either government citing the source. Who at Skarn decides what belongs in it — and has anything ever been left out on purpose?
- If the war-year convention is jointly maintained and both academies agree the current phase needs a name, who actually gets to name it first — and does the other side's chronicle ever adopt a name it didn't coin?
